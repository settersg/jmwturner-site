Chapter 34: Just Fears

Chapter 34

Just Fears

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Just Fears

Bob’s journal entry…

Private collecting by connoisseurs provides for scholarship outside the strictures of officialdom and encourages research driven by passion alone. Independent study should hold sway over dogmatic policies of a status quo that constricts the lust for knowledge and eventually conceals the truth. Policies and procedure that develop on the basis of self-interest and for political expedience can only undermine good judgment and distort reality. The desire to control one’s environment is natural, and is ever the case in the hierarchal world of governing bureaucracies; but when any authoritative body gains a monopoly in a certain area, that particular area is bound to decay.

  In the short term, control can be propped up by force of influence, but eventually, if the regime is controlled entirely by self-interested officials not only will it eventually destroy itself, but also that which it stands to represent. At this point, the management of Turner interests has forced the art of their namesake to suffer immeasurably. The British art establishment have consistently ignored private connoisseurship and denied the rediscovery of lost paintings. Peter Landesman fears a “museum culture defined as much by commerce, politics and academic provincialism as by a commitment to accurate art history.”

It is inarguable that authentic Turners have been condemned, shuffled around and possibly destroyed stemming from such contempt for the private collector, independent scholarship, and connoisseurship outside of officialdom. Sadly, this intellectual malady is not isolated to Turnerism, but is prevalent wherever the stakes are greatest, whenever a particular artist or group of artists is considered rare or precious, and of course—valuable. And also where there is the need to protect prices at an artificially high level.

Yet, in sympathy with officialdom, once they support or condemn a painting, they automatically have a legal attachment to it. People will undoubtedly come back for reckoning if the status of an artwork changes. In his ‘heart of hearts’ would Martin Butlin have the heart to make such a big commitment and support a multi-million dollar picture even if he believed it to be absolutely right? What if he had been in some way involved with condemning the picture in the past? He told us that he dreaded courtrooms, considering lawyers “a terror.” Could this be one reason for his adamancy even in the face of indisputable evidence? In light of new European laws, which place greater liability for misrepresenting works of art onto the art merchants, and the inevitable sharing of such liability by any who mistakenly support a wrong attribution, it is understandable that even a brave expert might cower. What if he or she was not even aware of the former status of the artwork under review?

Certainly, many are afraid to change the status of any potentially important work of art because of legal ramifications and getting tangled in lengthy and expensive ballyhoos. If a work was determined to be by Rembrandt and a multi-million-dollar transaction took place because of the attribution, then if at a later time the piece was denounced, somebody would likely be drawn into battle. If a work by Picasso was denounced and later accepted by a different expert, the former owner who had consequently sold it as a “corking dud” (do to the bad appraisal) would be out for blood. Fallout from this might drive the erring expert to cover up mistakes. This largely makes modern connoisseurship, art dealing, collecting—and for that matter, art scholarship, somewhat of an exercise in futility.

One should not understand this as an entirely modern phenomenon confined to an English-speaking world. As noted by J.H. Duveen, litigation was a pervasive worry for him and his Dutch cohorts nearly a century ago. He said he could not be cited as a witness in any notorious law case… “You know what the Dutch Courts are like when it comes to delays and enquiries: I shouldn’t be my own master for two years!”

“Who said what,” is clearly part of the problem. Not to know which authority has made what attribution to establish the status of a work creates a concern that can never be forgotten. Timid bureaucrats protecting their job first and foremost could be the biggest problem in this regard. Those willing to “go to the stake” or at least go to court in support of the truth seem few and far between. We must remember that some public institutions have been refusing to give opinions on works in private hands for over half a century now. If the only accepted authority has a monopoly position and at the same time is responsible to a mute institution, one must ponder the consequences. Give the collector a form letter of indifference, sweep the matter under the rug and hope that they will just go away.

There appears no respite for those that are being damaged by the fainthearted. The only recourse must be to take the problem out of the political realm and apply science with a vengeance. There must be a thrust toward developing more reliable tests and trials in order to mandate acceptance of all relevant evidence by those that maintain control through privileged appointments rather than honest scholarship. Would it work for the injured parties to play the role of martyr and subject themselves to the slow, expensive, and tormenting fuse of litigation in order to smoke the timid out of their ivory towers? The tenured must become more fact oriented than fiction obsessed, and more scientific at the expense of magic intuition. …end journal.

What followed was a report from Martin Butlin on behalf of Christie’s. It tried to refute the evidence supporting an attribution to JMW rather than presenting concrete reasons for rejecting me. Even if there were only stylistic anomalies that could be plied against me, surely these would have been carefully articulated first and foremost.

Martin must have realized the reciprocal risk involved in his present decision. He may have been in a precarious position regardless of whether he supported me or not. I wonder what Frosty (my former owner) would think if he saw his once-condemned picture sell to the Getty Museum for a fortune. How certain would Martin need to be before changing the status of a painting such as me, and particularly if he was implicated in condemning me in the first place?

A recent court case in underlines this concern—E.S.T, Inc. v. Christie’s. When Christie’s old master painting expert Crichton-Stuart misattributed The Entombment of Christ by Annibale Carracci, calling it a student work, the auction firm was sued for “negligence and breach of fiduciary duty on the grounds that Christie’s failed to recognize the painting’s true author and dramatically higher value.” The difference in value placed by the expert, $10,000-$15,000 versus the value of the painting as a Carracci, $300,000 made for litigious fodder. The court’s antagonism towards Christie’s defense caused the auction firm to settle the claim out of court.

Someone in this precarious circumstance might not be willing to jeopardize self, friend, or foe by supporting a piece he had once condemned—even if there was irrefutable evidence subsequently discovered supporting authenticity. It would make more sense for him to put up the battle of his life. If it is true that there was a conflict of interest, why had Martin come to in the first place? Alas, therein is the chafe, by his own confession, he is effectively the only Turner expert who could get such a major Turner painting into a Christie’s or Sotheby’s sale. Without this reference, the painting would always have a cloud of suspicion hanging over it.

The fundamental problem here is that the consumer of art services and the artwork itself are clearly at risk of being damaged, and especially if there is only one authority available to give a fully accepted authentication. This holds true also if there is only one sales venue to get top dollar, for example when auction houses collude effectively to create a single venue. This is a given if the two or three top firms will accept only one authoritative opinion before standing behind a work and promoting it.

How willing might any auction house be to stand behind an important work, or how willing would a consumer be to expend a huge sum without some equally large guarantee? This is a major problem. Imagine paying a million dollars at a good auction house in for a small Manet, only to find out later that the Wildensteins, the venerable art dealers with offices in , , and did not like it! If such a high-level authority questioned the authenticity of the Manet from , there would be turbulent times at the auction house.

In the face of art politics this problem can be capricious. Over a half-century ago, Friedlander was already talking about the impact of this sort of vacillation by the influential:

“Let us say that the expert recognized a picture as a work by Rembrandt. Out of confidence in him somebody acquires it at a high price. Later he arrives at the conclusion that he has made a mistake. Even if his love of truth now overcomes his vanity, he is yet reluctant to harm someone who has believed in him… Most people do not confess their mistake or they try and confuse the hard facts, more particularly as they know from experience that their clients never forget a financial loss.

Has Bob ended his subplot with an antihero of sorts? In the beginning, Martin Butlin was seen as a charming comedic celebrity. Had he become a slapstick villain? A villain, not only because he had condemned Me, but also because he discharged the brilliant connoisseurs: Griffith, Robinson, and Orrock in the process. Although gravely dishonored by Leviathan practices, “like incest in a way,” Friedlander comforts, “There is no choice but optimistically to rely upon the fact that ignorance and unscrupulousness will gradually be discovered in the circle of collectors.”

Comic—possibly not! Let us move closer to Martin’s fantasy of crime-thrillers, and espionage… The Royal Family and thus Prince William, the irresolute art scholar must certainly have an inside knowledge of the inherent influence of the art world, and in recognizing this must acknowledge a need for greater personal involvement. There was “Sir Anthony Blunt, the art historian, director of the Courtauld Institute and surveyor of the Queen’s picture collection. In 1964, Blunt was unmasked as a Soviet spy, the elusive ‘fourth man’ in the espionage ring.” Then there was Martin’s grandfather, a member of that same museum community years earlier and his father the biochemist. One don, Andrew Gow, noted that “he never understood the link between biochemistry and communism, but in it was close.”

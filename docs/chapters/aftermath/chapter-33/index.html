Chapter 33: Steamy Chat with Christie's

Chapter 33

Steamy Chat with Christie’s

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Steamy Chat with Christie’s

Connoisseurship is the thread tying much of Bob’s story together, but there is a proviso—one must beware of the “magic eye.” It certainly serves its master well if left to its own volition, but if there is a political fog in the air one might just as well be blind. Lawrence Gowing, Martin’s admired predecessor at the Tate implored, “When a letter from your girlfriend is dropped through the door, you don’t have to open it and read the name to know who it’s from.”

 The legendary Friedlander concisely assailed such a theory when he wrote that coldly analytical scholars make fewer mistakes. And as for Martin, in lieu of any concrete argument against me, he was forced to claim the superiority of his own connoisseurship, and suggest that this should be good enough. But what no one knew during the investigation and heated debate; James Orrock was a preeminent connoisseur when the nomenclature was widely used and an eye was the only defining element of expertise. He wrote: “Turner’s masculine oil-work, such as ‘The Shipwreck’ or ‘ Pier,’ stands forth like a giant on guard over his tender and gem-like drawings. Nothing was too mighty for him, nothing too delicate. As you can see, Orrock was a silver-tongued wizard as well.

                                      Photogravure by Walker E. Cockerell

Orrock’s position within the art community is exemplified by his relationship to one of the most important private art collectors of his day—Lord Leverhulme. “One who was most influential over his buying tastes was James Orrock, a passionate advocate of British art. Lever bought Orrock’s own collection in its entirety three times…”

Christine was getting ready for her last day of teaching for the school year and Bob was out on the back deck serving (Piggy) her morning orange juice. Christine called Bob to the phone—“someone with an English accent,” she said.

It was Nicolas White from Christie’s! He immediately apologized for his delay in answering. It was , over three months since Bob’s registered letter to him. The telephone conversation was likely meant to be a short one, but by the time the hour was up, we were both exacerbated.

“At the end of the day,” as White implored, Christie’s’ hands were tied. If Martin and his friends down at the Tate agreed that I was not by Turner then Christie’s would not deal with me. He made a feeble attempt to endorse some of their own in-house staff, but this did not go far when Bob apprised him of Martin’s edict whereby, he directed Christie’s staff onto the floor and away from the more scholarly pursuits.

He asked if I had been shown to anyone else—if Sotheby’s had been contacted for example. After a confident “No,” Bob told him that recently and quite mysteriously, a brochure was sent to him from Sotheby’s office in . It listed someone to contact in reference to Turner’s work. Bob went on to assure White that according to Martin, Sotheby’s would certainly contact him over any Turner painting, notwithstanding his professional allegiance to Christie’s. In other words, whether talking to Sotheby’s or Christie’s, one would be speaking to the same pair of ears. At this hint of collusion, and with protective zeal White made an instinctive attempt to distance his firm.

Mr. White tried to condense the issue by asking, “Well, if you were asked: what are the strongest points that favour calling it a Turner, what would they be?” Bob started to list the stylistic ones dealing with my lozenge composition and brushwork—two things that within the first few years of the nineteenth century would point at JMW exclusively. Before he got to the premier scientific points White broke in and said, “Yes, but these are stylistic points and might come up against different opinions.” Martin might have rejoined, “Of course, people can always find reasons for not believing something.”

Mr. White’s incisive question begged the impossible. If fingerprints were not good enough, and reputable provenance was not good enough—in the end, all one is left with is an all-encompassing debate over style—a debate where all factors must be considered. On Bob’s part at least, all had been accounted for.

Again, Bob made an attempt to give the strongest arguments, but this time concentrating on material usage including pigments, and support materials. He appealed, “There are a hundred different supporting points, each of which has been supported over and over again by highly respected scholars. All are equally important to the whole, how could they be narrowed to just one or two points?” The raucous banter had now become hollow silence. After a pause Bob mentioned the support given to me by two art historians Alan Chong and Daphne Lawson. Lawson went so far as to date me by my lozenge composition!

Bob told White that Ruskin had been over to Orrock’s house and had certainly seen me previous to being artfully reproduced for Orrock’s biography. This powerful provenance is self-supporting without considering the other previous owners Sir Charles Robinson, and Thomas Griffith. Mr. White seemed casually interested that my original photogravure was found in the Orrock biography, but seemed more taken with the fact that the twin volumes dwarfed the B & J Catalogue.

Mr. White then advanced the argument that “surely the Tate would be pleased by the discovery of any new Turner.” “On the contrary,” Bob railed, “they have enough to occupy their time trying to unscramble the Turner Bequest.” It is likely perceived by the Tate team that there is a glut of the artist’s work. It would not be to their particular advantage whatsoever. From the time Ruskin first started to unpack those boxes of musty Turner drawings, right up until recent history, there has been concern over cataloguing, organizing and preserving the State’s share of Turner booty. In a petty competitive sort of way, it would be counterproductive to the Tate’s effort and ’s best interest to start finding another stockpile of the artist’s work in a foreign land.

The junior and somewhat naïve staff members at both Christie’s and the Tate Gallery were probably left to believe that merriment would ensue at the discovery of a new Turner. Certainly Christie’s would make a commission, but the British laws of precedence stretch far beyond ordinary parameters. It was Martin who indicated that Mr. White was junior; he was not even able to place him when his name was mentioned. Bob could only assume that he was left to communicate with a buffer employee. It was one step from archives and Jeremy, but was it a step in the right direction?

Bob mentioned that according to Martin’s own confession he had never authenticated a Turner painting brought to him at the Tate (in all the years he was there), and that he feared being sued. White argued that a Tate edict would be one based on unbiased scholarship and could be trusted. Bob protested, “They are bureaucrats first and foremost—out to keep their jobs.” He mumbled afterwards—far from White’s attention, “There are grave legal consequences attached to giving a wrong opinion.” At some point or possibly many times in the past the Tate had disparaged me and killed any chance of an equitable sale. Hypothetically, millions of pounds along with reputations had evaporated. If legal action ensued and the Tate Gallery officials were held to account things could certainly get ugly. Employees beneath such an ominous cloud might be obliged to team-up in order to watch for lightning—and to watch each other’s backs.

Again White suggested going to another firm. Because the provenance came through Christie’s Bob was adamant that there could be no other agent. If Christie’s would not stand behind their own provenance, who else was going to? If Bob and Graham were to “shop me around” (as the expression goes) it would only muddle the issue or at least diffuse Christie’s responsibility. Perhaps this was the wish.

He made one last attempt to give credence to Martin’s standing as a Turner authority by bringing up his “Hand” theory. White was disappointed to learn that it was a highly impeachable target. When Bob prodded whether or not Christie’s “grasped at straws” while gathering provenance (as Martin insisted) Bob was dumbfounded at the reply, “Not always.” He had expected an accusation of blaspheme, not a whisper of contrition. Alas, maybe Martin was right after all, possibly Christie’s provenances were not to be trusted; albeit, that is exactly where many had been gathered for the B & J Catalogue.

Ultimately, Mr. White concurred fully with the point that if I was proven genuine, I would be the most important Turner discovery of the last century. The conversation ended with a somewhat amicable stalemate. White assured Bob that after talking to Martin Butlin again, he would get back to him within the following few days. The extended conversation took place on June 27, 2002. It is now 2005 and Mr. White has not been heard from since.

Had Martin been riding his high horse, ducking confrontations for years already? How was he to be treated by Christie’s, now that his trusty steed had been tempted from its elegant stable in order to jump puddles in ? Could Martin stay on its back in perpetuity, or was he to be slapped to the ground by one of his very own “Hands.”

Chapter 32: Portrait of a Hero

Chapter 32

Portrait of a Hero

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Portrait of a Hero

                     Great wooden vessel bears roughest of seas,

Tossed by the beckoning deep;

While souls near free from mortal distress,

                                 Still fight till their last precious breath.

Lost in their toil, despair and exhaustion,

                                 Sheer fright their last dying grip;

                                 Aye, an accomplice observer apart,

                                 Sits motionless out-but-within.

Might that be JMW’s own self-portrait stabilizing the stern of my Rescue boat?

                                  Shipwreck, the Rescue, enlarged detail

If yes—why is he there? He is the immediate focus round which the scene is composed; sitting there in his red waistcoat with cravat loosely piled about his neck. At first, the authority and calm demeanour of the figure might bring to mind a courtier, or even a British military officer, observing from his makeshift command post. There he sits, clad in his red tunic, impotent, unable to control the upcoming events. Nature is fully in charge! As we look closer at the collar and lapel, they are not those of a soldier. Where are the obligatory hat and cape? Apparently, the red is only to capture our attention, and so it does. But this is somebody different and apart, somebody that observes us straight on—a fallacious participant wearing an elegant cravat knotted in the fashion of the dandies of his day.

Yes, JMW was an artist seldom seen without his cravat; even as a young man in his early twenties it seemed part of his identity. His good friend, Clara Wheeler, wrote that one day the servant opened the door to a great uproar. JMW was sitting on the ground and the children were winding his ridiculously long cravat round his neck. William Vaughan’s prophetic statement may help define who our eyes meet in the stern of the boat. He wrote that Turner entered fully into the events themselves. He recorded what he had witnessed, not so much by description as by the creation of the pictorial equivalent.

Besides the full white cravat round his neck, the red velvet waistcoat was also part of the artist’s often observed uniform. When Linnel did Turner’s portrait from memory, he was wearing a red velvet waistcoat—a dandy coat with velvet collar. Although private and often reclusive, it seems the artist set himself as a visual target throughout his life. His colleagues saw him, even in late life shine out at Academy meetings in a red velvet waistcoat.

Like a portrait, the quiet expressionless face is carefully composed and familiar. And comparing his body gestures with those around him—integration within the scene is lost. His activity level is in astonishing contrast to those other tormented tangled bodies fighting for survival, or fighting to save others. Those not fighting are near death from exhaustion or passed out from fear.

Wait! Seldom did an artist do a self-portrait frontally. This inconsistency is resolved when looking at JMW’s self-portrait from 1798. It is lightly earlier in date but the face and personal deportment are very similar. JMW disliked his own profile. His two other self-portraits were frontal. He was concerned about his nose.

Rothenstein and Butlin wrote: “the only portrayal of a human being that Turner could look at without embarrassment was himself as seen in his wonderfully sensitive and searching early Self-Portrait.” Jack Lindsay agreed that only once he succeeded in staring someone in the face and plainly putting down what he saw. The face was his own and the result was the early self-portrait out of which he insistently stares. Mayall the photographer, who knew him near the end, mentioned that in speaking, Turner kept his eyes on the ground and did not look his companion in the face.

What about the tangle of doll-like figures? Are they a recurring metaphor? The physiognomies of the individual cast-members in the boat are unmistakable. Instantly recognizable, JMW’s people are as distinctively Turner as Pieter Brueghel’s are distinctively Brueghel or Rubens’s are Rubens. Even Martin could not deny the familiarity of the figures rendered: “There is even a Madonna group as in The Shipwreck.” Madonna’s tragic moment is a metaphor. (detail see here)

The comparison with a Christian Madonna surely goes beyond a trite reference. It is not merely a mother-child bond that you are given to observe, but something much more profound. The contraposta positioning of the one woman’s head and upper torso is surely taken from a medieval or early Renaissance archetype. One example is the Pietà by E. Quarton. The Louvre, .

What about that lone black man in the rescue boat? He is dressed similarly to the other sailors and is teamed up with two others in an epic attempt to save those clinging to a spar. In 1802, this man would have been a slave not an equal as portrayed here, and certainly not a hero. “At the best of times Turner tended to portray his staffage as doltish or ‘low’ mankind. Man, in Turner’s works, is rarely given the opportunity to be heroic.” Why here?

Chapter 16: Noblesse Oblige, or Not!

Chapter 16

Noblesse Oblige, or Not!

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Noblesse Oblige, or Not!

In young democratic , the work ethic was ablaze for . Things were evolving at a revolutionary pace, and it was not so much who you knew, but what you were able to accomplish by due diligence. People in were confident in their ability to succeed without the need to be connected or ennobled, and to progress at a pace commensurate with ability and effort. So entered the fray as an innocent. It is apparent from his actions that he mistakenly felt that after applying conscientious effort, he was able to blaze into the obdurate British art world and make his mark. Ah, but became a victim. As in Bob’s case, what doused his flame was the title-gazing inattention that often accompanies privilege? Fortunately for me, Bob was still smoldering.

’s plans were inarguably altruistic. He wished to have his collection go to the Tate. After reading much about JMW, he had become a Turnerphile. It is clear by his own declaration that he was compelled to act accordingly. Aware of JMW’s ardent love of his native country, endeavoured to find a way that his collection of Turner’s drawings and sketches could become the property of the English nation. It was in deference to what he believed Turner might want that in late life attempted the generous offer of his collection to the Tate Gallery:

I was prepared to make a great monetary sacrifice in order to bring this about… A visit was made to my home, a small portion of my collection was examined, and the receipt of the letter, from which an extract is given, followed: “I have to inform you that your collection is of no interest to this gallery… J. B. Manson, Assistant Director, Tate Gallery, .”

Almost every drawing and sketch shown to the representatives of the Tate Gallery came from either the Landseers (father and son), John Henderson, Lady Leicester, Sir Charles Fellows, or Sir Wilfred Lawson, although no mention of this was made at the time.

Instead of being welcomed “as a possible possessor of valuable material which might prove serviceable in throwing additional light upon the remarkable career of one of the world’s greatest artists,” Anderson was cast off as if he were a peddler of third rate forgeries. Even to the casual observer, a person would have to be at best a lunatic to try and load the Tate full of fake Turners. Dubious works would not stand the test of even a cursory review.

 And what about the Tate collection that already existed at the time? Ruskin classified the Turner Bequest of Drawings into three classes according to quality: in the first were Rivers of France (45), Roger’s Poems (57), Rivers and Harbours of  (23), and a few others, 135 in all. There were 1757 studies in the second class, and in the third were the drawings in black and white…The third class evidently comprised nine-tenths of the entire collection.

Ninety percent of the Bequest is effectively third rate and not worthy of cataloguing. Even most of the second tier fails the test. It has been argued that much of what Turner did not sell was waste, except for a number of key pieces fated for the Nation by the artist early on. At his death, much was discovered haphazardly jammed into drawers. A. J. Finberg believed the fact that Turner, “the water-colour painter is represented at the National Gallery [now in the Tate Gallery] is purely an accident. The bulk of his water colours are in private collections.” The Nation had received some 19,000 works on paper; it becomes evident that most were of curatorial interest only. Plainly, the main catalogue of Turner watercolours produced by Andrew Wilton supports Finberg’s theory since most of his listings are not from the Turner Bequest. The number of major untraced works is also noteworthy. There are many with no history subsequent to the time of ’s pillage. If Turner sold most of the better works in his lifetime, then those available to while sleuthing the English countryside would have been the better pieces.

 A group of eighteen Turners recently came up for sale in America. They had been ’s and were being sold with letters written by Anderson himself. According to the vendor, the sale also included a collection of large gilt-bound books: Turner’s Rivers of France, Harbours of England by Turner & Ruskin, Turner Gallery, Turner by Sir Walter Armstrong, and engraved plates for Armstrong’s Turner. They had a recent on-line appraisal valuing them at 50,000 dollars each.  There were no takers at an opening required bid of 40,000 dollars for the whole lot. Bob wrote the owner and informed her of the tenuous nature of an  provenance:

The John Anderson Jr. collection is something I have been researching for a while now, and have talked at length about it to the leading Turner expert, Dr. Martin Butlin. This is the same expert that was co-author of the Oxford Companion to J.M.W. Turner published in 2001, as well as the complete catalogue of Turner’s paintings. I in fact have a watercolour from the collection. Butlin has informed me that he believes all the works from that collection are dubious at best and has described them under the Fakes section in the Oxford Companion. I do not personally believe this and have been working to try and prove that much of ’s collection was okay. The unfortunate thing is that without Butlin’s support, the auction houses and dealers would have a hard time selling them and might even risk legal problems.  Is there any way that you could get copies of the letters to me? This might prove helpful to both of us.

Bob has not been able to maintain contact with Nancy from Hotchkiss, Co., . The lack of cooperation is understandable considering that a stranger—Bob, had just given wings to her anticipated fortune.

The question still remained, if those missing watercolours listed in Turner’s catalogue raisonné, had not been banished to obscurity in a foreign land, then why are so many of them untraceable? What other suitable explanation could there be for so many important works becoming lost? Many of them had clear provenance as indicated in ’s catalogue. Agreed, there are likely many of them hidden away, passing from generation to generation without the necessary death duties being levied, but by conservative calculations, Turner produced an enormous number of images that are not presently accounted for. It is often described how Turner’s need to create was compulsive, he was continuously sketching and often in the most unlikely of circumstances.

To inject another dark thought into the mix, one that is more often skirted than debated: the probability that certain works and sketchbooks were stolen from Turner’s studio during his late years. There was a lengthy stretch that he was cohabitating with Mrs. Booth and away from his studio. It is well known that during his feeble years, JMW became unwary and less concerned over his studio.

Human nature being what it is, the lack of security, and the market value of Turner’s work makes it likely that at least some skullduggery took place. Could this be the reason the secretive and suspicious Turner appended so many codicils to his will? Was he angry for some reason? The changes were seemingly meant to exclude people that were formerly near and dear to him, people who would have at least had limited access to the studio during his absence. “One codicil from , revoked the bequests to close relations and his housekeeper.”

“I have to inform you that your collection is of no interest to this gallery….” Clearly it would have been valuable to see the complete letter that received from the Tate Gallery eighty years ago. The ostracized with his narrow concept of the world, blamed his situation on individuals making insular decisions, and although the abuse he suffered had come from a long-established authority, the Tate, he did not believe their verdict for a minute. This connoisseur had come too far in the study of Turner to be misled, but he was ultimately unable, or unwilling, to do much more than express his opinion in a book. The tome was certainly produced for the benefit of posterity with hopes that eventually his unjust treatment would be revisited.

Even a slightly suspicious person struggling to make sense of what happened to might believe there was a plot, one that may carry on today. The only mention warranted in the Oxford Companion to J.M.W. Turner is a couple of lines under the section dealing with fakes and copies. Does this mean that he has been swept away again without adequate appraisal? To be generous, even if one could prove furtive intentions by those making the decisions, those intentions might not be clandestine in the normal sense. They might merely be part of doing one’s job within a milieu of dutiful obligation—something so familiar to the noble system of patronage.

Bob is now at variance with Anderson in his belief that individuals alone are making those obstructive and self-serving decisions, preferring to believe that it is a mob of interconnected self-interests, and although not likely planned as a conspiracy, the consequence adds up to deception of equal significance. It is not only ‘human nature’, as believed, with individuals being vindictive; but instead, human nature on a macro-level. Institutions, governments, art organizations, dealer cartels, expert coalitions, etcetera; all have a vested interest in whatever happens in the art world. In working synergistically, they are naturally subject to being in each other’s pockets. It takes a very special member of any established group to break ranks. It takes integrity on the part of any individual to make judgments for altruistic reasons alone, and unimpeachable honesty to make a judgment contrary to self-interests.

An unfair review of a painting on the part of an individual, group, or institution can happen for a variety of reasons. Selfishness, bad judgment, animosity between competitors, support of colleagues, fear of making a damaging error, greed, or just plain incompetence are some reasons. One thing for certain: if ’s 15,000 pictures were accepted as authentic by the appropriate expert, the whole body of JMW’s work would be enormously devalued by oversupply.

In 1848, there was a superlative example of how oversupply deflated the market. As a result of external and personal pressures the Duke of Buckingham and Chandos went bankrupt. Their cherished collection that took several generations to assemble was thrust through Christie’s and onto the marketplace. These near-priceless treasures were so vast in quantity that there was just too much to be absorbed at one time. Every collector of note attended. The occasion was described at the time as “the greatest bargain hunt of the era.”

In 2001, one of Turner’s watercolours sold at Sotheby’s for over three million US dollars. What might it have sold for if there were several hundred loose in the marketplace—one cent on the dollar perhaps? For understandable reasons collectors champion a stable economic environment for their investments. Any market can slip away quickly from lack of confidence. This happens for prices of individual artists’ work as well as for artwork within the macrocosm of the art world generally.

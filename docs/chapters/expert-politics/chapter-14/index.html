Chapter 14: The Unknown Turner

Chapter 14

The Unknown Turner

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

The Unknown Turner

Bob’s Journal Dec 2000…

Was a madman? John E. Anderson. Jr. had a thesis, several in fact. These were so far off the mark relative to traditional Turner scholarship that there could be no question. According to the way history has treated him, if not mad, he must have at least been a fool.

As fate would have it, I was able to acquire, not one, but three copies of his rare book published in 1926, The Unknown Turner. The first was somewhat musty and looked as though it was rescued from a shipwreck, but the contents and images were intact. It is a large-format artful publication treading a discordant path between prose, art scholarship, and science. Unfortunately, in his enthusiasm to reinforce his thesis, the author’s tone is quite defensive. One is left with more than a suspicion that he was challenged considerably about his ideas prior to the book’s publication.

For several decades was an avid Turner researcher, and even became a connoisseur, but where did he go wrong? His plausible arguments focus on two main points: that all Turner drawings and watercolours, at least the ones in his own collection were secretly signed; and only half of Turner’s pictures worldwide are accepted as authentic. Of the other half he claimed to have the lion’s share. He reasoned conservatively (in the case of Turner who was wildly prolific) that with two drawings a day for 300 days of the year, and over an active period of sixty years he likely did 36,000 or more drawings and sketches. This included everything from summary sketches to finished masterpieces. Anderson’s number might be as reasonable as the commonly believed number—22,500. In 1820, “Turner traveled to for the first time, producing over 2,000 sketches in two months…” Averaging almost thirty-five a day.

Even accounting for a lifetime obsession, for to accumulate 15,000 drawings in “thirty years of steady, persistent search,” would require a greater acquisition rate than one per day. One would have to be as committed to collecting Turner’s work as the artist himself was to producing it. This passion took back and forth between and numerous times. His sources ranged from naïve dealers to top-end aristocratic collectors. From some of the most respected patrician families of the day, he was even able to buy collated collections of drawings.

Let us assume for a moment that this huge body of work was authentic. What impact would it have, and who might not want to acknowledge its legitimacy? Imagine the monumental task that would be needed to catalogue it! It would then have to be conjoined with the rest of the artist’s accepted work. Turner’s life history would need rewriting, and in doing so there would be a tremendous jolt to the status quo. This of course would debase much Turner scholarship to the chagrin of modern Turner experts. And for those Brits with a financial vested interest—it could certainly drive down the market value of all Turner’s artwork.

According to Eric Shanes, the Turner Bequest alone has always presented the nation with display problems due to its size. In 1960, Sir John Rothenstein admitted that adequate cataloguing had not yet been completed and in all its range the collection was inadequately known. Ironically, at the time he was speaking only about the Tate holdings of Turner’s work. Surely, to ’s detriment, there were no more Turner pictures welcome!

was on the perimeter of art scholarship. Reviewing the times before and after his collecting spree might help to understand an important evolution that effectively sealed his fate. By 1926, when his book was published, seventy-five years after Turner’s death, was just barely getting a handle on the enormous Turner Bequest. Modern-day art scholarship had yet to fully emerge with its fundamental documentary needs. Before this, the gentlemanly activity of art-connoisseurship was private, derived from family wealth, and carried on by people with aristocratic means.

In more recent history and particularly in , industrial fortunes from the nineteenth and early twentieth century became the foundation for acquiring great art treasures from abroad. These new “squillionaires,” as Bernard Berenson referred to them, realized early on that a broader appreciation of culture was needed to make them fully-fledged nobility in their fresh new land. This along with new tax benefits for gifting art to public collections enticed a deluge of art from abroad, which in turn made fortunes for several European dealers. During this upheaval of shifting art fortunes and because of distrust created by voracious competition between dealers and collectors, instability was soon met with demands for greater guarantees. There was apparently decreasing confidence in the old courtly way of doing business, and as chivalrous attitudes waned, the need for documentation became more important. Duveen confirmed that:

The multi-millionaires became much more prudent. They bought from reputable dealers, or employed them to collect for them; but then the professional expert began to assert himself. There were many of these, from the late Doctor Wilhelm von Bode downwards. Many of these experts charged fees for their opinion or their ‘certificates,’ and some even expected a percentage of the value. It cannot surprise anyone that with such a fertile field to exploit, some of these experts became “industrialized.” Soon, work by certain masters had to be “certified” by one or more of the particular specialists in the various schools. What had begun as a most honourable occupation became, in many cases, a furious race for gain, frequently degenerating into the behaviour of a pack of hungry wolves. Those experts who did not accept fees were so jealous that it was often sufficient for one deus ex machina to pronounce a judgment to move another one of the same elevated standing to contradict it. During the last half century, the law courts had to deal with many cases resulting from these internecine wars.

According to Geraldine Keen… support by a recognized expert for a painting can put the monetary value over the top. The power that scholars wield in this way can have at least two unfortunate effects. The more honest would avoid publicly expressing doubts about paintings on the market or in private hands that may pass or have passed for years as definitely the work of the artist in question. The less honest may be prepared to exchange their confirmation of a painting’s authenticity for cash remuneration; there are many certificates of this sort in circulation, some signed by scholars of undoubted eminence.

The cozy payment-for-services relationship between dealers and experts apparently included not only individuals, but also institutions. “Joseph Duveen should have died a baronet, for he was offered a baronetcy in reward of his gift of the Turner wing to the Tate Gallery.” It is difficult to say which is more insidious and which could more likely lead to corruption: the outright payment for services as with Bode, or the giving of high recommendations by noble institutions such as the Tate Gallery. Social advantage bestowed by the latter is of course cyclical and begs mutually supporting patronage. Particularly, in England from ancient times, patronage has been effectively folded into the social fabric under the guise of duty.

After being gifted great collections and large endowments, private or public institutions overgrew private interests and struck a death knell to effective individual involvement. Art history students began to flood out of art schools. This flood was particularly apparent in where new policies required art history departments to be connected to each art school. These were of course not seasoned collectors, but instead university graduates looking for employment and a secure future in a position.

Beyond this apparent dimming of Renaissance thought, there seemed to be a flickering light. From his parents Paul Mellon inherited, in his words, “A desire to own, to enjoy, to savour, and to conserve rare and beautiful things, a desire which must infuse all collectors. Anyone who has felt the impulse to conserve rare and beautiful things has been infected by the virus of collecting. This is one reason the world has its great museums.” Unfortunately, Mellon presents us with an oxymoron as he takes the individual joy of collecting and almost as an afterthought gives it over to the institutional museum.

What was the context within which was struggling? As was mentioned, there were a number of highly successful dealers operating at that time who were instrumental in gathering foreign treasures for those wealthy American collectors. The most notable players at the time were Bernard Berenson and Joseph Duveen. This was a collaborative effort between private scholar and gentleman dealer. Berenson was a young émigré from and eventual Harvard graduate (1887). By studying and carefully cataloguing Italian Renaissance art, his erudite labours formed the model for modern art scholarship. “B. B.’ as he was affectionately called, had read Morelli, Crowe and Cavalcaselle, as well as the new school of art historians who were more scientific and less rhapsodic than their predecessors.”

There was nothing random about Berenson’s approach, and because one of his desires was financial independence, it was this element of his success that came back to haunt him. The suspicious observers, who wanted to believe he was Duveen’s puppet, sullied his reputation by saying he stretched authentications to favour Duveen and Duveen’s clients. This damaging critique may have gone well beyond hurt feelings. When contemplating the donation of his famous home called I Tatti (near ) to Harvard as a post-doctoral study centre, Berenson in late life lamented the path he himself had taken. “He wanted the student body of his new school to write about the way artists and their works have been appreciated through the ages rather than to concentrate on the material history or the provenance of a given work of art. In short, he wanted the Institute to promote aesthetical and humanistic, rather than philosophical and antiquarian interests.” With this edict the coffin nail had been set; all that was needed now was for it to be driven home.

Sentimentally in later life, Berenson smote himself and much of what he stood for throughout his exultant career. He admitted a dramatic change in personality in his old age. When he had reached financial independence, the wanderings of his idle mind sent him searching for an ideal beyond those needs of his own vigorous youth—new ideals that were at odds with the more mercenary side of his nature. Regardless of the reason for attaching this dramatic shift in philosophy to his auspicious gift of I Tatti, it is certainly somewhat responsible for the revolutionary state of art scholarship today. It must also have been responsible for the move towards social interpretation and away from “material history and antiquarian studies,” shifting the natural order to a bureaucratic jumble of cross-purposes.

In their own day, Berenson and Duveen proved that there was still room for the private scholar. But alas, by helping to launch an overpowering institutional presence, by helping redefine art scholarship and the reason for collecting, they marginalized impassioned students such as . In Tate, The Art Magazine, Martin Bailey and Sarah Greenberg confirm that, “contemporary trends in museology often shy away from exhibitions celebrating private collections and connoisseurship.”

From a more philosophic perspective, the advantage of being a nonprofessional private scholar like is that one is unbeholden to any person, institution, or power group. One can therefore speak his or her mind fully and honestly, and, unencumbered by special interests or social demands, can investigate purely for curiosity. was a private scholar yet was still collecting as a connoisseur.

Chapter 13: Clairvoyants and Necromancers

Chapter 13

Clairvoyants and Necromancers

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Clairvoyants and Necromancers

Bob’s Journal entries, Sept 2000…

…The accepted “expert” today needs mainly to be a good cataloguer, and largely exists apart from “old fashioned” connoisseurship. If someone is able to collect into one catalogue, the entire body of an artist’s accepted work, the same diligent actuary is given authority to be an expert. The weakness inherent in this process is that the expert is not always a connoisseur or able to think outside the limited scope of standard literature on the artist. More important, he or she is unable to recognize un-catalogued new discoveries. Here one must be able to go beyond mere recognition and into the realm of synthesis.

The process of cataloguing in itself might be viewed as an attempt to squelch unfettered research on a particular artist. To “close off” the number of accepted works by an artist assures an orderly maintenance of the marketplace. The person who has arduously carried out the task might also be expected to defend their “definitive” catalogue listings against potential new intruders in order to avoid expensive revisions or to have their research considered lacking. Nobody wants a defective brainchild. Does it not seem a bit presumptuous in the first place that anyone would proclaim their catalogue complete?

Zealous antagonism towards new art discoveries is more explicable if one accepts carte blanche Bernard Berenson’s statement, “By bestowing a name on a work of art we ignoble that work in all the love that it arouses in us and at the same time shut our eyes to the elements which, seen dispassionately, make it wrong. One easily tends to exclude adverse factors.” The problem with extrapolating this much-adopted attitude is that Berenson was working ostensibly with artists that had no name to begin with, and much of his life’s work was based on invention; whereas, in an era after that of the Renaissance, when the name of an artist, in itself, might connote fame, there is always the likelihood of having stylistic comparables to pacify the incredulous art historian and expert alike.

Skepticism is not an uncommon frame of mind for many within the curatorial realm. What are acceptable limits though? Are attempts by individual owners of un-catalogued paintings to correlate and match information about a particular artist, only a sinister scheme to deceive? Of course not! They are the normal first steps in trying to find solutions.

Justice Herman Black’s closing remarks came after a famous debate between experts over the authenticity of La Belle Ferronnière believed to be by Leonardo da Vinci. These experts included: Berenson, Roger Fry, Sir Charles Holmes, then director of ’s National Gallery, and Duveen. Justice Black made pointed reference to experts who offer “nothing better than their own sixth sense as evidence for a picture’s genuineness.” The judge also criticized that it required “a good deal of mental agility to follow some of the experts from their positive evidence on the stand, to the diametrically opposite views they had expressed in their books long before.”

There was apparently a complete flip-flop of curatorial opinion. Duveen’s own cousin James Henry Duveen described this same sensational lawsuit but with more zest: “The reports of the interminable proceedings prove the truth of an English judge’s well-known dictum that there are three kinds of liars: liars, damned liars, and expert witnesses.”

After Duveen died the owner of La Belle Ferronnière published The Rape of La Belle. The book was a vicious attack on Duveen, who, Hahn said, “headed an international plot by art dealers to control the market, and Duveen’s array of experts, no better than fortune-tellers, clairvoyants and two-bit prophets.” Berenson himself eventually expressed this same sentiment in shared self-criticism using the terms, “clairvoyants and necromancers.”

 From the other side of the authenticity debate and in sympathy with the curatorial position—the calamity that would result from a mistake in judgment could be fatal for one’s career. As one British art critic observed: “people love to see authority figures slipping on a banana skin.” The mystery and high prices associated with this type of fraud, makes forgery attention grabbing. This is not only for those deeply involved in the industry, but also for the art-illiterate.

To the dismay of many in the art world, and particularly museum and gallery officials, the stir of publicity created by scandals over art fraud usually overshadows other momentous events. The distorted view left in most people’s mind, including the minds of many sophisticated collectors, is that much out there is “fake.” This is untrue. Luke Herrmann worries us similarly in relation to Turner, but fails to substantiate it: “As a painter in oils Turner attracted few followers, though there were numerous efforts to produce fake Turners in the years immediately before and after the artist’s death.” Who were they? Other than the three nefarious, albeit hard to define shadowy spirits, “Hands A, B, and C” of Martin Butlin (discussed later), no one seems able to finger these mysterious fakers. End Journal…

After breakfast on the fourth day, Paul and Bob sat at length enjoying the fresh morning air. He reviewed Marilyn Laver’s report, the one that had been done quickly to facilitate the Crown’s need for evidence in support of its case against Visnei. Paul disagreed with the background information that had drawn suspicion to the painting in the first place. Likely some characteristics of the painting that Joyce Townsend considered unusual for Turner were a result of incomplete data. The significance of the canvas thread count, colour of the ground, the canvas size, number of relinings, and the canvas being joined in the centre—these were all questioned by Paul.

Again there was banter about the way many experts such as art scholars and curators were so hesitant to give well thought-out opinions on works owned by private individuals, even when powerful scientific evidence was present. Paul agreed that in some ways there appeared to be animosity toward private art scholarship generally. Bob had already concluded that much indifference shown the private sector had to do with fear over litigation. Dr. Maryan W. Ainsworth alluded to this in the 2001 IFAR journal when she wrote that “Max Friedlander never calls anything a ‘fake.’ It is always a ‘replica’ or a ‘copy.’ I don’t think he ever even says ‘later version.’ It’s interesting that he, by choice, didn’t include fakes in his volumes… So he wasn’t talking either, for some reason. Probably for the same reason we don’t.” It might be noted here that for the sake of Friedlander’s discretion, the substitute terms: “copy” and “replica” have taken on a much more derogatory connotation than they deserve. Confucius knew the importance of “the rectification of names.” Did Friedlander?

This same trepidation was expressed by Kenneth Clark over what should have been considered fair art criticism. In his 1975, The Romantic Rebellion, he condemns trite mimicry by contemporary artists in light of some of Fuseli’s emotional and distorted artwork, but fails to name names. He wrote: “The laws of libel prevent me from naming several contemporary examples.” If the fear of litigation, even over art criticism, immobilized such a monarch of critical thinking, it is no wonder that infective paranoia has made much of the art world dumb to the needs of a collector like Bob.

Another salient reason for lack of support by the curatorial community for the private sector might be the personal frustration over the miniscule benefits they receive from giving an attribution. If a student’s work was upgraded to that of a great master such as Raphael, the painting could become a hundred times, or even a thousand times more valuable, and therefore provide its owner with a huge benefit. Could resentment at times, be at the root of feigned indifference?

There should be little argument that the monetary value of art has much to do with altering attitudes and stimulating activity in and around academic endeavours, including those at the elementary level. Aristotle Contemplating the Bust of Homer was bought at auction by the for the record price of 2.3 million dollars in 1961. An art history professor in the area recalled that the attendance in his introductory art appreciation doubled shortly after the Rembrandt sale – “most of the new students cheerfully admitted that their interest in art had been provoked by the press accounts of the Rembrandt Aristotle.” The energy that lights the flame of curiosity and eventually that of erudition is often sparked by the clatter of coins. From his College Window A. C. Benson contemplated—“to what extent it is permissible to use motives that are lower than the highest, because they may possess a greater effectiveness in the case of immature minds.”

“Banana-phobia” is probably one of the most unnerving perils for the curator/expert who works under public and administrative scrutiny. While most of them are certainly trying to perform their duties as well as possible, those often-antagonistic external forces that are outside their direct control, very much govern their lives. Public interest in scandal is one of those things. Intrigue over art forgery has been popularized by several contemporary writers in their attempt to titillate the fancy of the uninformed public. This exercise indubitably makes any bureaucrat cringe. Art critic Geraldine Howard, having worked to expose forgers, was intrigued with the public’s fascination with them. The experts don’t have all that much faith in themselves to be absolutely sure it’s a fake as opposed to van Gogh on a bad day. They’re not certain… is how she expressed it.

Taking all the pitfalls into account, ‘curators’ may have a right to feel squeamish about getting involved in anything close to the grassroots of their ivy-covered tower. Yet, where does that leave the potentially important painting like me? What will happen to a lost masterpiece after the offhand condemnation by a disinterested or queasy official? It will probably end up in limbo while its disenchanted owner sells his or her “lost hope” and newly born “orphan” to a Sunday-painter for the price of the frame. Or for the price of a song at an Aldergrove auction sale!

The next morning it was a steady boarding as Paul bid farewell. His steel clad case in hand, along with one-hundred-fifty photographs and my stretcher key (the one with the fingerprint). Because of the better equipment back home he decided to take the key in order to get the best possible images for analysis. This would assist both him and whoever else was needed to corroborate the comparisons. But the question still remained: was the fingerprint Turner’s? Bob was already satisfied with the results of Paul’s visit. His consultation had clarified and uncovered enough new information that his five days in was a success regardless of whether or not the print proved a positive match.

An e-mail from Paul two days later expressed that his father and brother believed in me. How sweet! From the images they saw and what he had told them, they were now also convinced that I was the real McCoy. Bob took this as great news because they had been involved with art most of their lives. In addition, they had battled a frustrating decade trying to prove their own Turner to be “right.” Their hands-on experience and ponderings over Turner’s work would certainly qualify them to give a valuable opinion!

Chapter 2

The Rub

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

The Rub

Bob once told a personal story that mocks the fickle footing of the art world and at the same time his own novitiate efforts. I’ll share it with you…

…“Antique …Tonight at ,” read a discrete little sign outside the Christopher Centre. Christine, my wife and I wandered into the large hall. To our bewilderment the auctioneer was in a heated argument with a short heavy-set fellow. Gerry Niessen, the auctioneer was well known to me, but not his pugnacious assailant—a man in his sixties with a flushed round face. His grave concerns were made even more dramatic by his broken English and strong German accent.

After skirting Gerry’s conflict, it became clear that the whole setup was unusual. The discrete venue, the seeming lack of publicity for the extraordinary items on view, and the raucous banter in the entryway; put together it all made for a surreal moment. To our left was a case filled with expensive-looking gold jewelry set proudly atop a black lacquered and heavily carved oriental table. Above the table was an outstanding impressionist painting of cattle. Those gentle creatures condensed from broken brushwork lazily observed us from their mountainous isolation – probably somewhere in .

The argument at the entrance door seemed to be heating up, and becoming progressively one-sided. The mystery man was insisting that the sale be cancelled. Gerry was trying to temper the argument by standing taller, emphasizing further his normally stiff demeanor, and possibly to get enough altitude to remove himself from the embarrassment of the moment. This ploy was effective. The elderly aggressor soon wandered off arm in arm with what looked like his attractive daughter of about thirty.

Other items scattered about the auction room included medieval arms and armor, eighteenth-century German ceramics and pewter, pictures, rare oriental rugs, fifteenth and sixteenth-century religious carvings, very fine quality miscellaneous household items, and a small robot that looked like a miniature version of Expo Ernie from Vancouver’s 1986 World’s Fair.

Analyzing estate auction items can give interesting clues to the background of the former owner, but here the objects were so rare and unusual that I was dumbfounded. To add to my confusion, most of these items would have been at home in a centuries-old European castle and did not seem to mesh with Expo Ernie—the leading edge of technology at the time. What also seemed curiously out of place was a set of contemporary glassware engraved with Hermani-Benz.

 Later that evening before the auction began I was able to learn from the auctioneer that the collection was part of an estate that belonged to Rudolph Hermani, a wealthy German who had lived out the last years of his life on an orchard five kilometers from town. As I settled in for the sale, warming the seat in front of me was the stout German who had given the auctioneer such a hard time earlier. I was still perplexed about his female companion—possibly she was his private nurse. The riddle was solved when he leaned over and nuzzled her neck.

The auction sale began with a few inconsequential household bits and pieces. As the evening progressed interesting treasures came and went. Three quarters through the sale, my stout neighbor had collapsed into a child-like slumber and his companion was in the process of reviving him. Before long, away they went without offering so much as a single bid on anything. But why had he antagonized Gerry about choosing as the venue for the sale? He had even charged that the sale was too important to be held in , and that it should go back to . Why was he so adamant?

Next on the auction block was an ancient looking panel painting, the size of a book page (see image No. 1). I had viewed it during the preview period but was at a loss to its significance. The back of the piece looked new but the front had the stress of great age. The subject included a strong looking peasant carrying a small naked child on his shoulders. The child lay in an odd horizontal repose and the peasant had the physiognomy not unlike that of my German friend who had just been escorted home to bed. Loose in its frame, it embarrassed its handler by nearly tumbling to the floor. Once everyone regained composure the bidding began. As the porter thrust it towards my corner of the room I was spontaneously drawn to bid, especially since the trifling opening-bid amount was motivation enough.

Soon the little panel painting was knocked down for the price of its broken frame, and I was the new owner. This strange little curiosity sat without judgment for several months in a prominent corner of our front room. On occasion I would bring it to the attention of a visitor, but to no avail. The character of the work was familiar, but as is often the case, images seen years earlier can remain trapped in the memory and influence behavior, but not break consciousness. There it remained, an odd yet remarkable little curiosity waiting for proper recognition.

 It turned out that the subject of the painting was the least difficult part of the puzzle; I had seen similar images on pendants growing up and when reviewing art books on Gothic art, my memory was refreshed. It was the burly St. Christopher transporting the baby Jesus on his shoulders. In the biblical story the weight of the child during the river crossing became crushing for the Saint. The incredulity of the Saint about the Godly importance of his charge was soon erased by this profound miracle. It was a popular Christian image north of the  before Martin Luther’s Reformation of the early sixteenth century. In more recent times, images of this patron Saint have been carried as a talisman to provide safe passage. Was it providence that St. Christopher was found at the , a hall that was named in the Saint’s honour?

After the sale I learned more about Rudolph Hermani; he had been associated with Mercedes Benz and his estranged wife was of Benz lineage. Thus the mystery of the glass tableware etched with Hermani-Benz was solved. This former business mogul, I was in due course apprised, had descended from the Krupp industrialists of . He had lived in his safe haven on for a number of years with little fanfare. His new home was likely a necessary getaway from the former responsibilities and pressures of the old country.

Strange renovations included: a complicated flight path down to his fortified boat launch, massive oil storage bunkers, and double locks on every door—even the interior ones. This German gentleman must have imported some of the fears prevalent in at the time. It was long after WWII, but activists were still terrorizing high-ranking aristocrats and industrialists. In contrast, the history and lifestyle of the quiet Okanagan valley should have been far removed from such fear.

Shortly after he passed away, the Hermani orchard was put up for sale. The wayward treasures had been waiting in to make their trans-Atlantic voyage (a voyage that never took place). In a mysterious fire that blazed endlessly with the fury of Hades, the house, bowling alley, and bunkers of oil burned, and burned. Christine and I recall pondering over the inferno and enduring trail of black smoke that took everything with it. It was somewhat of a miracle that the antiques had been taken out beforehand.

The St. Christopher painting had suffered much. There was considerable over-painting which gave the hulking figure in the foreground a more ominous look than his bulk had intended. The eyes were distorted and mask-like; and in a piteous attempt by some unknown restorer to strengthen areas such as the hands, the image looked as though a less than talented amateur had painted it. The baby perched atop the rounded shoulders of the Saint was the only visual relief. It offset the dark drama of the image—lying in a horizontal position of questionable stability as the Saint negotiated the turbulent water. To add to the sublime image, ominous clouds boiled overhead while a jagged escarpment seemed to close in on the figures—its overhang covered with root-bare vegetation.

Occasionally I would pick it up and scour it visually. One day it was almost like a Zen awakening as my eyes focused on an understated mark in the lower left-hand corner. Tangled in the dark undergrowth at the edge of the river was a capital A surmounting a capital D. One visitor jokingly suggested the monogram AD might be that of Albrecht Dürer—considered by most to be the greatest of German painters! The fellow that made the comment was from and took his own suggestion lightly; a painting by Dürer away from the wall of a great museum would be impossible in modern times. But considering its provenance—anything was possible. Rudolph Hermani’s family had direct links to Nuremberg, Dürer’s hometown, and because he had other priceless treasures—why not a Dürer!

Bound

 Within the month, I was  bound with my tiny panel dressed with its few grams of priceless oil paint. I had made arrangements to meet Gregory Martin of Christie's, then one of the most experienced auction house representatives in the field of old master paintings.

Heathrow airport was busy as the passengers awkwardly made their way through customs. After declaring my prize, St. Christopher, I was shunted into a waiting area where the customs officials had me unpack my treasure. It was declared to be worth a baronet’s ransom. A number of them wandered around the Saint; this was clearly out of curiosity more than official duty. After several minutes of muffled mirth implying disbelief, they sent me on my way buried with luggage and clutching my panel.

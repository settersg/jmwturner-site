Advocate: Home and Abroad

Bob’s Journal entry …

Graham tunneled through security at the airport and made his way down the long corridor to the waiting jet that was ready to thrust him to another continent. Once off the ground, within moments, he would appear like a speck on the horizon. Bob’s sentimental thoughts were brought to an abrupt halt when he realized Graham’s laptop was still at the security-check—forgotten and alone, except for his passport and cash which was tucked in the carrying case.

As Bob anxiously tried to explain the dilemma to a very suspicious and dumbfounded security guard, he noticed arms and legs flailing in the passageway as Graham attempted a new sprint record. Minutes later, cool and composed, he was over the horizon, with laptop, passport, and a pounding spirit for adventure.

Graham’s invitation to study abroad at the International Study Centre (ISC)— Queen’s University’s Herstmonceux Castle was going to leave things pretty quiet around home…

Not all was gloom for Bob, as one of his first projects abroad Graham wrote a bloody good paper on me. His Art History professor, Daphne Lawson was quite remarkable, allowing him to choose his own study projects throughout the year. In my opinion his best was, Analysis of J.M.W. Turner’s Shipwreck, the Rescue. Among the galleries and museums he haunted while searching out JMW’s artwork, were the National Gallery, the , the Tate Gallery, the Courtauld Institute and the & . His resulting paper shows an interesting synthesis of antiquarian interests and social interpretation. At the time, Bob wasn’t too keen on the latter. You’ll see later.

Except from: Analysis of J.M.W. Turner’s “Shipwreck, the Rescue”

The determination of the closest possible dates for artwork is an ongoing challenge for art historians. They appeal to a variety of sources: primary, a bill of sale; and secondary, discoveries about artwork written at a later date. Usually more in-depth research is required, in which case the analysis of both the physical and stylistic characteristics of the work is conducted. An art connoisseur undertakes the examination of stylistic characteristics whereas scientific analysis is required for the examination of physical characteristics. For Shipwreck, the Rescue, considering both physical and stylistic criteria, a date of c1802 is assigned.

 Turner, like many of his contemporaries, was forced to look back at the theory and colour practices of past great masters due to a lack of technical teachings at the . This working method is one that Turner retained throughout his life.  The informal training of the Romantic period made the apprentice system obsolete and in some cases artists had to rely on technical handbooks for direction.  During Turner’s formative years he “felt his way slowly, and invented his procedures as he went.” From his earliest development, he was coached with the idea that an artist should find another artist that is comparable in talent and imitate his or her style with the intent of surpassing their skill.  It was thought that if an artist could do this he would achieve a higher position within an artistic hierarchy.

 Turner had the unique opportunity to put himself ahead of Willem Van de Velde the younger (1633-1707) in this artistic hierarchy when in 1800 he was commissioned to paint, Dutch Boats in a Gale: Fishermen endeavouring to put their Fish on Board (Bridgewater Sea piece). The Duke of Bridgewater wanted Turner to paint it as a companion for his sea piece, A Rising Gale by Willem Van de Velde after which these two sea pieces would hang side by side.  Even though Turner’s end result was a significantly different sea piece, it was unanimously well received in the press. This work helped to ensconce him as a great artist after both these works were exhibited together by Lord Francis Egerton in 1837. It was at this point that Turner gained Old Master status.  Turner’s successful and well-received effort of 1801 encouraged similar projects: Calais Pier, with French Poissards preparing for Sea: an English Packet arriving; Shipwreck, and most certainly Shipwreck, the Rescue

 The ‘ piece’ was an important start to a powerful series of sea pieces; however, Turner’s voyage to  was a distinctive turning point in the way he would portray the sea. Surely Turner was excited about sailing to  because of his fascination with the sea. This voyage changed his outlook forever.  The weather during his journey was rough and Turner described his landing at  Pier as, ‘Nearly Swampt.’ After this harrowing experience Turner’s sea pieces such as, Calais Pier and Shipwreck took a markedly turbulent shift in style.

 Turner’s style was also branded by his mother’s prolonged illness and wretched death in 1804. The Shipwreck and The Wreck of a Transport Ship both embody the emotional turbulence that was part of Turner’s new fabric “…the tumult of a catastrophe.” The new intensity with which Turner painted The Shipwreck has been described as an overwhelming impression of realism and terror.  The dark tonality of Turner’s early oils is magnificently justified by the subject. Shipwreck, the Rescue and Calais Pier were done prior to the death of Turner’s mother in 1804, which surely accounts for a lesser sense of ‘terror’ and ‘catastrophe’, but undeniably, they still reflect deep anxiety. This might suggest that Shipwreck, the Rescue and Calais Pier are works executed by Turner between 1802 and 1804.

 The presence of a pier in both, Calais Pier and Shipwreck, the Rescue gives a sense of optimism for the passengers because it provides for them a chance for survival. In Turner’s sea pieces subsequent to his mother’s death, as in The Shipwreck and The Wreck of a Transport Ship this symbol of hope is completely obliterated and replaced with increased furor.

 To determine the date of a painting from within the body of an artist’s work it is important to compare changes in technique. The somewhat a-typical use of brown ground in Turner’s Shipwreck, the Rescue helps in determining its date. In a sketchbook, dated approximately 1798, at a point when he was beginning to experiment with oils, Turner reversed his watercolour technique.  Instead of the traditional white ground Turner began working from a dark ground in order to achieve a stronger range of tones.

 ...Turner eventually brightened his paintings, giving the desired light to his work by means of a white ground, but the dilemma arose for him later on when he tried to reverse the impact of the predominantly white ground by using bituminous glazes to darken the tone of such paintings as Rembrandt’s Daughter Reading a Letter, 1827. Of course, this work inspired by one of Rembrandt’s own works would need a much darker overall tone, and when Turner darkened it, disaster ensued. Glazing with a bituminous colorant in an attempt to impart the appropriate chiaroscuro proved a tragic exercise. One only needs to see the disastrous results of such an effort by observing the ugly degradation of the surface that resulted over time. Clearly in this case Turner should have started with a dark ground and worked his illumination with lighter pigments and finally heavily applied white impasto as Rembrandt did or he himself had done during the earlier years of the century.

 On February 13, 1802, Turner was described by a fellow Academician as painting “…on an absorbing ground prepared by Grandi…It absorbs the oil even at the fourth time of painting over…by this process he thinks he gets air and avoids any horny appearance.” Grandi prepared these grounds for Turner starting early in 1802. They would have been a darkish colour similar to that found on Shipwreck, the Rescue.  After 1800, Turner was already interested in the effects of a dark ground for his oil paintings because of the elusive “Venetian Secret.”  It was thought by many members in the  that the Venetian painters, such as Titian, used glazed transparent colours over a “dark absorbent ground.”   Turner’s pilgrimage to the Louvre in 1802 gave him a first-hand experience with Venetian masters such as Titian. Turner sketched and made notes on over thirty paintings during his visit there.  He paid particularly close attention to Titian’s Entombment. In his words:

This picture may be ranked among the first of Titian’s pictures as to colour and pathos of effect, for by casting a brilliant light on the Holy Mother and Martha, the figures of Josephe [sic] and the Body has a Sepulceral [sic] effect…The Flesh is thinly painted, first by a cold colors over a Brown ground, so that it is neither purple or green, some red is used in the extremities, and the lights are warm…his brown figures lose in great measure the gray colour which in fact is produced by the ground…

  Turner’s thorough analysis of this painting shows his interest in the Venetian master’s technique for achieving effect. He emphasizes the point that Titian used a brown ground and glazing to achieve the ‘brilliant light.’ The techniques used for the Entombment and many other paintings left a considerable impression on Turner.  Like his contemporaries, he might not have realized that on some of the worked up areas, Titian’s ground had been a white gesso over a dark brown ground and consequently he did not achieve the desired “Titianesque” effect. The result was too dark.

 Because of these somewhat unsuccessful results (which many of his contemporaries criticized), Turner abandoned the brown ground used in Shipwreck, the Rescue and used white grounds for Calais Pier, and Shipwreck. This is another point that helps to establish a date of c1802 for Shipwreck, the Rescue. Over the next decade, he continued to use dark coloured grounds for non-aquatic genera.

  The style and technique are paramount when dating artwork, however, the scientific analysis of physical characteristics is also highly significant. Turner was known to have used all pigments that where manufactured during his day.  Unlike many of his contemporaries, he worked with new pigments almost immediately after they became available to artists.  He was born during an age of much chemical change in the manufacture of pigments, and all new colours he daringly used; for example, chrome yellow, emerald green, and cobalt blue.

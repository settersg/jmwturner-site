Chapter 27: Loose Lips On Board

Chapter 27

Loose Lips On Board

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Eve Aug 21—Loose Lips On Board

The winery tour planned for late in the afternoon had gone well, and it proved somewhat revitalizing after the tenuous discussions that day. The Butlins were deposited back at their hotel for a rest. Graham and Bob were starting to show signs of overload though, Bob especially. Chris, who had wisely avoided the battleground in the living room earlier, was showing some exhilaration about the upcoming dinner cruise. Their trusty watercraft, which had plied for a decade, was the Casabella Princess. When everyone arrived at the small paddle wheeler, the Butlins admired its quaint charm. The only disappointment was that the evening was blustery and wet. Likely because of the weather, Ken Davis, our captain, had only one other couple on board.

The choppy lake was an ideal setting to discuss a sublime and stormy piece like myself. Regrettably, by now it was unlikely there’d be any budging those stoic Brits over my attribution. According to Bob’s journal the only meaningful discussion that took place that evening was shortly after dinner. Up until then, the regal demeanor of and the comic dramatizations of Martin entertained regardless of substance. Graham jested earlier about some obscure literary tale. How wine is an effective truth serum. Soon Martin had medicated himself with the heady stuff beyond safety. He talked relentlessly and the things he shared would have made many in his circle back home cringe. The fun character and humorous content of his banter was the code needed to unravel certain furtive activities being carried on by the auction trade, galleries, and museums. Martin had over the years dealt with all of them on the highest level. The churning, slapping, and grumbling of the vessel muffled many jewels of privileged insider knowledge from the recorder, but those that made it through were set in gold.

Apparently, while Keeper at the Tate, Martin would conspire with certain dealers (who he implicated by name). They were always doing deals. If a pair of pictures came up at auction, for example, they would fix it with the two pictures being split between the two parties afterwards.

Bob urged, “Although Christie’s had to sell at the highest price?”

“Yes, to a certain extent,” confirmed Martin. “They would make conciliatory deals and let us see the pictures much longer in advance.”

Martin again implicated, “X,” who apparently said at one point, “You shouldn’t be buying, Martin. We have to get the price down.” As it seems, competition met with a certain amount of disdain within this privileged core of the art trade. Albeit on a grander and more sophisticated scale, might a corollary be mapped out as follows?

The attorneys general of three states sued a group of seven stamp dealers for allegedly conspiring to rig auctions in several states, and . ‘These defendants’ collusive practices adversely affected sellers and undermined the marketplace.’ In 1996 and 1997, the ring allegedly fixed bids at most of the large stamp auction houses in the , including Sotheby’s, Christie’s… Over $150 million worth of stamps were sold at public auctions that year.

On behalf of the Tate, Martin had another interesting slant on acquisitions. “We’d go to the dealer who is most likely to want to buy the picture you wanted—for stock. Some dealers buy just stock and then resell. Then we’d get them to buy it for us, because that sort of knocked out the competition…we always got special terms, sometimes we would get it for no commission at all because they wanted prestige.”

This overriding need for prestige was highlighted when Martin exclaimed, “Of course” to the enquiry whether those involved in the art scene in support the public collections toward getting a knighthood. Martin discussed clear examples along this vein where the competition between dealers and colleagues was relatively fierce and wrought with jealousy. One allied dealer bidding on behalf of the Tate bid above the set limit. This was necessary to win the piece and thus be the gallery able to provide it for the Tate. As a drawback to this somewhat vain, somewhat business-wise exercise, the dealer purportedly absorbed the difference. One dealer apparently was reticent to compromise on his prices, even for the Tate, and would use an anonymous benefactor foil to disguise lowering his otherwise inflexible prices.

Indulging in the prestige-for-barter concept, whereby status is available for a price, Sir Joseph Joel Duveen “was offered a baronetcy in reward for his gift of the Turner Wing to the Tate.” In the tiny 1913 Tate Gallery publication, the National Gallery people wrote that “because of the munificent gift of the late Sir J. Duveen, the Turner Collection would now be adequately housed and exhibited.”

Support for public collections and the significance of such matters as they relate to “caste” was made evident in the listing of British supporters to the exhibition in 1966: all had auspicious titles. This was a propitious group of supporters, without doubt, and the contagious nature of this ideal was clearly being mimicked abroad in . As Martin had previously mentioned this was the exhibition that absolutely established Turner in “smart” circles.

It could be said that the far reaching fingers of the British Aristocracy dug deeply when it came to Turner, and that the exhibition was possibly staged not only to establish Turner in smart circles and to a degree educate the general public, but also to suppress the unruly nature of the vast accumulation of Turners outside England. Could it also have been organized to thwart undesirable competition? “Something in the spirit of the age…rivalries of nations,” Wheeler wrote this in the forward of the same exhibition catalogue, slightly out of context here, but nonetheless, prophetic.

Many Brits invariably feel anxiety over having so many of their historic treasures sold abroad the previous century, including many Turners. This was at a time when it was both legal and presumably ethical for great pictures to be shipped from without challenge. Seeing pictures such as Gainsborough’s, The Blue Boy; or Turner’s, The Fifth Plague of Egypt at home in a foreign land must tempt disdain, and for some, possibly even a counterproductive need to avenge the loss. For the resentful few, casting aspersions against the integrity of American collectors and their collections, as well as challenging authenticities would be a sound way to accomplish such a task.

Robert Cecil, a former British diplomat, admitted that there was a “latent anti-Americanism that infected so many of his generation.” For Anthony Blunt at the Courtauld:

The pollution of the language by Hollywood movies, the plundering of great works of art by transatlantic plutocrats, or any number of similar issues, the Americans had by the thirties become identified in the minds of many British intellectuals with an evil materialism that threatened their inheritance. The anti-Americanism that Blunt shared with many of his academic contemporaries was more than the traditional British snobbism toward the colonials…he was vehemently outspoken about the need to halt the flow of artworks to the United States…Blunt wrote The Times in 1971 to condemn the selling of artworks from major British collections as a ‘sign of cultural barbarism

It appears this warning came far too late. The hoard was permanently ensconced in American collections. And what about Martin Butlin, the graduate from the Courtauld during Blunt’s directorship, might it be true that he inherited a bit of the same animosity in accordance with Blunt’s socialist edict?

The 2004 upheaval at the caused by the predominantly British Beaverbrook Foundations, focused attention on similar sentiments. Although in this instance the kafuffle seemed propagated as much by animosity toward a perceived ‘colonial’ backwardness. The CBC reported that: “All but three members of the gallery’s board recently resigned in protest when the two foundations came forward and said they were considering removing several significant works from the gallery… [Sotheby’s] declared two of them too rare and valuable to remain in the gallery.”

At issue was whether the Beaverbrook Foundations gave or loaned the pictures to the Beaverbrook Gallery. Foundation vice-chairman Vincent Prager said, “However much one likes , is not the centre of the world and these are world-class paintings. It was felt by the foundation, as far as I understand it, that they should be taken back…, in exchange for a very large sum of money.”

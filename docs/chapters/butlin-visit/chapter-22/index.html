Chapter 22: Disrespectful Tomato Indeed

Chapter 22

Disrespectful Tomato Indeed

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

A Very Disrespectful Tomato Indeed

Journal entry continued (Magnum’s)…

Even Martin expressed certain doubt that anyone younger than the young Turner could have painted Shipwreck, the Rescue: “The thing is, people can always say, well, you know—if it’s not Turner, well, who else could do it? But in fact, there was, whatever, whoever… there was always somebody else who could perhaps do it. Not necessarily do it, but.”

Above this bit of doublespeak Martin remained unflinching; he maintained that by virtue of a lengthy study of Shipwreck at the , some “great admirer” had at the end of the day copied the heart from the great Shipwreck. Martin believed that his argument was strengthened further by comparing similarities between Shipwreck, and Shipwreck, the Rescue. Ah, but there was a fatal flaw to this reasoning! What was it?

Bob: Well, Shipwreck, the Rescue is very accomplished.

Martin with modest agreement: Uhum.

Bob: Especially the figures in the boat and the way they’ve been developed into that interwoven turmoil. It’s really quite exciting.

It has been noted by other students of his work that, “Turner frequently treated humanity rather as a mass, a force of life, than as individuals.”

Graham: Every time I look at that boat I see another limb somewhere, it’s just so complicated. Actually, what makes me think that it is directly related to the Shipwreck is looking at the costumes. I’ve been able to find almost the identical costumes, which makes me think that whoever did it, knew Shipwreck intimately.

Martin: Yeah. If it is not Turner—if it is not Turner, it’s somebody who saw the Shipwreck.

Graham: And studied it probably.

“Yeah, at the ,” Martin declared.

Bob: So, if it is not Turner, it is somebody that has seen Shipwreck?

Martin insisted: But there was no difficulty there, there was no difficulty there because it was exhibited at the !

Of course, Martin was wrong about Shipwreck being exhibited at the . This mistake left him in a difficult position for his follow-up report. Except for a short controversial stay elsewhere, Shipwreck had been held within the close confines of Turner’s own private gallery. Knowing what we know about Turner’s character, he certainly would not have allowed anyone to pull a chair up to Shipwreck in order to plagiarize.

Hamerton assures us that his need for privacy was so strong, “he would admit nobody into his painting room.” Turner’s guarded personality would have made it impossible to copy anything in his studio. “Turner would himself refuse young artists permission to make sketches of his works when they visited him.” “The difficulty with which his patrons; and even his relations obtained access to his presence was well known.” “He never would suffer me to see him draw, but concealed all that he did,” said one architect; when Mr. Britton entered the artist’s studio, “in an instant, Turner covered up his drawings.”

Nor would the intermediate owner of Shipwreck, Sir John Leicester have made the painting available for copying during its short stay with him. His wife wanted rid of the ‘sea-disaster’ because of its gloomy nature and because it was a painful reminder of a tragedy in her own family, “Lady Leicester having lost a favourite nephew at sea was unable to bear the association called up by the scene.” It certainly would not have been front and center at the ’s while they prepared to do a quick exchange with Turner for a less personal subject, Schaffhausen.

To make his theory come together, Martin reinforced his mistake by certifying the as the venue at which Shipwreck was copied. Calmly for the most part, except for the odd outburst of conciliatory laughter, he fought this point relentlessly as we all mumbled over dinner.

It was prophetic that Martin brought up Turner’s, The Wreck of a Transport Ship. This was because of its ostensibly one-of-a-kind pictorial device—the sailor in the back of the rescue boat who is seen trumpeting for help with a voice trumpet. This device is also found in Shipwreck, the Rescue. So if it were not the same hand that painted both paintings, the earlier painting would have had to inform the latter. There was no argument that Shipwreck, the Rescue was painted earlier, and therefore, would have informed Wreck of the Transport Ship. In fact the latter painting was not seen until fifty years later when it was exhibited at the British Institute in 1849, and thus could not have been used as a model for this trumpeting reference.

Chapter 30: Sticky Wicket

Chapter 30

Sticky Wicket

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Evening Aug 22—Sticky Wicket

From Bob’s Journal…Our journey to was solemn and soggy. I had promised the Butlins nice weather but our trip to the island via was anything but cheery. They loved the mountain vistas, at least the ones they could see through the cloud cover. The Romantic grandeur demanded a silence that was refreshing for all of us. The blustery two-hour ferry crossing to was made bearable by the vaporous beauty of the mist that cloaked the little islands along the way. Except for the week of the Butlin visit, it was otherwise a year of drought in BC.

Once in , we settled into our little hotel by the inner harbour. It was central to most of the tourist amenities. That evening we went for dinner at an upbeat place where our transformation from sophisticated diner to party-animal was a matter of ten steps.

Graham kept questioning Martin about his relationship with Christie’s and Sotheby’s and it came up again that Sotheby’s uses his expertise. He clarified his involvement more critically when he confirmed that he had an effective monopoly. This disclosure came when he demurely challenged us to take Shipwreck, the Rescue to Sotheby’s for a second opinion. “There’s nothing to stop you from going to Sotheby’s,” he said, “but my guess is that the moment they got the photograph, they would almost certainly ring me up—unofficially. There again, I wouldn’t be paid and they couldn’t use my name. In a way it sounds awful, one’s got a monopoly.”

The auction-room topic expanded and Martin revealed that it was often difficult tracking pictures down in order to catalogue them, and that existing records at the saleroom made it easier. Not only was he beholden to Christie’s archives for the history of many B & J Catalogue entries, but he also recanted previous mockery in the same breath, making it clear that Christie’s was not always “grasping at straws” in order to establish provenance.

Discussions led to my least favorite topic and a topic that even Graham got animated over—art being used to promote issues through narrow sociological interpretations. Martin ended up offering to wring the neck of one of Graham’s professors who had told her students during the first class of the year: “If you expect to look at art as something aesthetic, you should get up and leave the class right now.” We were abruptly freed from this unpleasant conversation; a table had just come available in the pub. We were off to join the inharmonious racket, perhaps a din of infidels. All we could do was assimilate.

Some of Martin’s biographical beginnings made it through the noise. His attendance at , was apparently granted through his father’s connection to the same college. On entering the interview room, there was this enormous cloud of smoke and a voice called from within, “Come in, come in.” It seems that the cigarette smoke was so heavy he could hardly make out who else was in the room. The first thing Martin remembered hearing was “post-war bulge.” This bulge was made up of the hordes wanting to attend the college. Martin believed it was mentioned to prepare him for rejection, but when he said that his father E. J. had the same problem when he applied years earlier, the interviewer appeared from out of the cloud. “Oh well, was your father at Trinity?” The barriers came down and shortly thereafter Martin was invested.

The way he was hired at the Tate was similar. His grandfather had known the Rothensteins as neighbours in the country. Sir William Rothenstein (1872-1945) was an eminent artist and director of the Tate Gallery from 1927-33. The grandfather had gone for tea at their home on numerous occasions. When Martin applied for the job he managed to mention the connection. Abracadabra, Martin had his first and in many respects, his final job. He worked there until retirement. With Sir John’s mandate, he and Martin co-authored a Turner book soon after his appointment. Thus, a new Turner expert was made. Pure serendipity, Martin’s attitude towards his work must have been as much a happy family relationship as it was a job.

Martin had read History at , a couple of years of art history at the Courtauld Institute, and much to his amazement he received his Master’s degree from his Alma Mater by virtue of having paid-up his “caution money.” Martin felt it was like a damage deposit. He believed that his honourary doctorate came as a result of his contributions to the Butlin and Joll, J.M.W. Turner Catalogue, albeit, he was also heavily involved as a cataloguer on William Blake and Samuel Palmer. Martin assured us that while he seemed to slip easily into his lot-in-life, it took more than just nepotism to accomplish one’s purpose and maintain a job once potted. He insisted there was still a chance for someone to “fall flat” if they were not fully prepared.

Martin also acknowledged that he owed part of his good fortune to timing. He came into the museum world at the leading edge of the great art history program-expansion. It was at a time before art history departments were well established, and competition for jobs was far less fervent. This could explain why Martin works more as an expert than a scholar. Things changed so quickly during his tenure; with the new emphasis on art scholarship, and advances in science and technology, he must have felt a bit awkward. There was also the reality that most young people graduating had much stronger academic credentials.

Was this why Martin and his predecessor, Gowing, felt that looking, looking, looking, and the “magic eye” were all-important? Must they, for the sake of sheer survival, have promoted the visceral approach when dealing with art expertise? They were certainly being trapped by progress, like the average seventy-five-year-old in today’s computer age. Some keep abreast—most do not! This seemed like a credible theory considering Martin’s evident animosity towards both academia and scientific involvement in authenticating works of art. Berenson’s portrayal in his dying year is perhaps poignant here, “We should live more with things as they feel and look, and not insist on what is their ‘scientific’ make-up. Blessed are the simple for they make the best of things.” Martin was certainly making the best of things.

It is not meant here to disregard the importance of having the “eye” or to disparage connoisseurship and the advantage derived from looking at many quality pictures. If an eye can be trained, that is the way to do it. In his prickly little book, The Charm of Turner, James Mason explains that “the human eye has remarkable limitations, and only those who have trained their sight by dint of hard and painful labour can realize how little we truly see.”

But students must be careful that there is not a false prophet in their midst. It is one thing to have “an eye” and another to only profess such a gift. Most curators can get away in their jobs with the ability to see the aesthetic in what has already been accepted as artistic, but it takes a connoisseur of true merit to see the artistic in what has not yet been discovered, or at least what is yet to be accepted as an important example of art. If it were not for independent connoisseurship, Turner might not have been such a successful landscape painter. “Turner’s development entered a new phase following his acquaintance in 1793 of a medical psychiatrist and art connoisseur, Dr. Monro, who gathered several artists around himself to do copies of drawings he possessed.” Considering the choice of the artists in his care, there is little doubt that Monro was the rare person who could choose successfully from unproven talent.

A good example of a connoisseur with a good eye is found in the person of a successful art and antique dealer or collector, who must continually enter into an overwhelming amount of miscellaneous barter, and out of the confusion, find the gem. Such success is dependent almost entirely on a good eye. A collector such as Orrock was one such person. He was willing to enter the fray, buying and selling things from galleries, auction rooms, and privately—risking his own money, but in the process successfully building a venerable reputation. Almost a century ago, a noteworthy furniture manufacturer, Mayhew of , developed a line of Thomas Chippendale revival furniture named after most respected personalities and connoisseurs. In their 1910 advertisement, “The James Orrock” chair was at the top of the list.

If the rarity of an item determines its desirability for a museum, and if these important things are often one-of-a-kind, then it should be agreed that a curator experienced exclusively with things that are fully recognized, would have difficulties. Might this also entail the search to rediscover and preserve forgotten treasures? Without a somewhat innate sense for recognizing “quality” first and foremost, it is unlikely that he or she could even aspire, let alone ascend to the highest levels of connoisseurship.

Consider the relative abilities of those who collect for a living, versus those who collect institutionally. It probably depends as much on the force of curiosity as it does anything else. Every fine object is significant for one reason or another. To determine how important it is amidst the jumble-at-large… that’s the rub. The collector tends to migrate towards the familiar, that which he or she recognizes and understands as having a value or importance. The difference between recognizing what is good and what is exceptional is surely a synthesis of at least three factors: visual, intellectual, and invention. Experience, which is also important, comes from looking, looking, and looking.

Yes, even imagination can help to place the importance of an item into context. It is unfortunate that the spontaneity and vigor often felt by a connoisseur just before making the ultimate decision—whether to purchase or not—is stolen from most curators by institutional policies inhibiting them from making personal purchases. If one believes the oft mentioned need to make a personal investment, then it is fair to believe that a sense of danger, the fear of loss, or the anticipation of great reward is needed to reach the necessary level of psychological intensity that forges the skills of true connoisseurship.

Around our little pub table Martin vied with the loud music trying to answer my questions about what was conferred by the title, Keeper. He shouted the following explanation:

It was a really old-fashioned term; it was a medieval thing. There used to be someone called Keeper of the Privy Seal, which meant the private seal, the thing you made the seal on documents. A document wasn’t valid until the private seal of the monarch was imprinted on it. The monarch wasn’t supposed to carry it around in his own pocket, so there was this court official whose job it was to carry this bloody great seal around with him. Then the term, “keeper” was spread around to all sorts of government departments including curators in the National collections. I’m rather sad in a way that the word Keeper is no longer used. When I first started in the Tate, I was Assistant Keeper grade II, and it wasn’t the figure two, it was the Roman numeral two, really just to show we’re old fashioned. Then I was promoted to Assistant Keeper Grade I.

Considering that the Lord High Chancellor and Lord Keeper is the same person, and the position is directly below The Archbishop of , Lord Primate of All , it was clear why Keeper was a fancy label. Within the pervasive and lengthy British noble hierarchy, Lord Keeper was next to God and the Sovereign. This is an over-simplification in a way; there are certainly interveners, the Prince of Wales and a couple of direct descendants for example. It is interesting that the royal patron of the Turner Society is Prince Charles, HRH the Prince of Wales. It draws a link between the uppermost levels of influence and Turner interests—interests that are not necessarily focused on the posthumous benefit of the great painter himself. It is in the best interest of those in charge to make the Tate Gallery as exclusive as possible, and at the same time be the key authority for authenticating Turner pictures.

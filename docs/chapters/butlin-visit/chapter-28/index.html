Chapter 28: Backroom Whispers

Chapter 28

Backroom Whispers

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Backroom Whispers

The boat rocked and flapped its way towards shore. Bob was still silenced by what Martin had just said: “We’re all in each other’s pockets – it’s like incest in a way.” In his tome Peter Watson expounded on this point when he wrote that “Christie’s was run by a close-knit coterie of like-minded men – ‘in-bred’ in John Herbert’s apt term – who tend to assume that the firm would always have a monopoly on Old Masters and great furniture by virtue of its pedigree and its close links with the best families.”

Martin, now a Christie’s representative, esoterically whispered what amounted to clandestine implications of such control, “If something comes up, like a Blake, we try and steer it towards the Tate.” According to Martin, Christie’s would ask him for a valuation on a Blake or a Turner for example, and he would give them a figure.

“How much do you think it’s worth?” they would ask.

Martin: “I’d say a figure and they hated it.”

Dispirited (according to Martin) they would then concur, “Oh, all right then.”

Martin gave a whimsical example of one of these esoteric evaluations. “William Blake, two and a half thousand pounds,” he might offer as an appraised value.

With the reply from Christie’s being: “We were thinking of thirty thousand pounds.”

Martin’s retort: “Oh, pull the other one!”

Bob: Did you get it for your price?

Martin: Well, this was my recommendation to Christie’s.

Martin was not going to be unreservedly persuaded to admit that he was still buying for the Tate, and thus recanted his confession by waffling, stating that he tells Christie’s to “go away” when they ask for valuations. He tells them to go to their computers and look up the last ten years of sale records.

That same morning Bob had already been surprised. He learned that the declared rivals, Christie’s and Sotheby’s had a shared interest—Martin Butlin! Notwithstanding his employment as a senior expert for Christie’s, he revealed that if Sotheby’s got a Blake or Turner they would come to him.

Bob asked, “Because of your preeminence they wouldn’t go to anyone else but you?”

Officiously he replied, “Well, Sotheby’s have to, because I work for Christie’s…” Then he immediately added, “But in fact, Sotheby’s do come to me… That’s another story.”

Half an hour later Bob tried for further clarification, “With the sale of a painting like Shipwreck, the Rescue… the only place to sell it is through Christie’s…”

Martin gently interrupted, “Or Sotheby’s.”

Bob: “Yeah, but to get the proper money for it…”

Martin again interrupted, “Well, Christie’s or Sotheby’s.”

He then gave a humorous anecdote comparing Sotheby’s and Christie’s, “There’s a saying about Christie’s and Sotheby’s: sell your pictures at Sotheby’s. Buy your pictures at Christie’s… which is supposed to reflect on the honesty of the two firms.”

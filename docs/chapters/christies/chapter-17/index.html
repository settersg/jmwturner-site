Chapter 17: Back to Christie's

Chapter 17

Back to Christie’s

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Back to Christie’s

After several months without contact with Christie’s, Bob finally decided to phone Jeremy Rex-Parkes to determine why his seemingly smooth correspondence had been cut off so abruptly. Jeremy’s cheerful greeting betrayed his own deception. As it turned out he had been forwarding Bob’s e-mails to Mr. Nicholas White of the picture department expecting him to respond to Bob, but for some unknown reason they went no further. According to Jeremy, Mr. White said he answered the inquiries; but the truth of the matter was Bob heard nothing from anyone. After a brooding wait, he could only assume he’d been brushed off.

Now that they were back in touch, he told Jeremy about JMW’s fingerprint. The one discovered by Paul Biró. He was quite intrigued. Bob also prodded him trying to find out why the three-month interruption of communications. It was no secret that I had been shopped-around by many a previous owner, but once Christie’s was shown my persuasive provenance—what was the problem? Was it some message from the Tate that muddied the waters?

The Tate people had been contacted about me many times previously and must have been aware of my sordid legal problems, problems stemming from Visnei’s criminal prosecution of 1982. Was the staff intimidated by such a high level fraud investigation by international law enforcement? It is understandable that an institution such as the Tate would shy from bad publicity.

True, many people for many years tried to flog my not-so-decorative canvas at varying venues without success. It is an axiom of the art world: if a painting is not by a famous artist, and does not have strong art historical value, it had better be “pretty” and have decorative value. The Tate officials were probably weary seeing those wretched victims in my “rescue boat.” There had been three instances alone, where requests were directed to Martin Butlin, the chief for Historic British Art at the Tate.

The somewhat dumbfounded Jeremy again relayed a message from Bob to Nicholas White. By this time Bob was certain he was being evaded, but to his surprise Mr. White called back within ten minutes. He acknowledged that he had previously contacted Martin Butlin and that he was skeptical. White seemed enthusiastic about the new fingerprint evidence though, and assured Bob that he would contact Butlin again. In addition, he promised to contact Evelyn Joll. He explained that Butlin and Joll were the authors of the large two-volume catalogue raisonné of JMW’s work. He said that they had come across the listing of me in the Christie’s catalogue of 1904 (Orrock sale), but at that time were cynical.

This was at a time before such catalogues had photographs and there was only a scant description. They must have assumed I was “in the style of,” or by a “follower of” Turner. One can understand their reticence. Quite fairly, the question could have been posed: “If Shipwreck, the Rescue was right, why had it not at some point been recorded, or been published alongside the artist’s other great early sea pieces.” Both cataloguers where likely at a loss without an image and not knowing my whereabouts. 1904 was well before works were systematically relegated to catalogue raisonnés, and after 1904, I disappeared. Unfortunately, I was unavailable for proper assessment at the time Butlin and Joll had a crack at producing their definitive catalogue raisonné.

When Mr. White consulted the two experts, they must have felt shadowed by a nemesis. The thought must have crossed their minds that I could truly be a great discovery, and they would be the discoverers. At least one of their regrets was that they had expended much effort compiling the most comprehensive listing of the JMW’s paintings, and now after already revising it once, their brainchild was again looking deficient. It was published first in 1977 and again in 1984.

Nicholas White, with renewed enthusiasm requested professional photographs. It had been a year since Jeremy Rex-Parkes had identified the stock number and Bob was only now being asked for photographs. He assembled a complex package of photographs and text, twenty pages in total, including: my front and back, close-up details, images in colour, black and white, and ultraviolet. There were essays about previous ownership, stylistic references, images of the fingerprint comparisons, and a compact disc with some high-resolution details. Bob was uncertain if including the compact disc would prove futile, at the time Martin Butlin was without a computer.

 In her report, Marilyn Laver stated that the question of attribution to any particular artist should be addressed by a curatorial expert on Turner’s work based on content and style. In the letter to Christie’s and Butlin, the physical characteristics were emphasized, leaving the visual and style comparisons to Butlin, who hypothetically was the curatorial expert.

This broad trust in Butlin’s connoisseurship was an uncomfortable surrender for Bob. And especially after he had just heard a discourse by Professor Susan Greenfield: “Vision is not about simply soaking up the outside world, instead, it is an active process which invents, ignores, and distorts what is entering through the eyes.”

A century ago, Robert de la Sizeranne described Turner’s own special facility for mingling perception and invention with the natural. There are two modes of being naturalistic: “doing what Nature has realized or doing what Nature can realize; copying her results, or inspiring oneself by her laws. Turner did not perhaps paint any one of his pictures ‘after’ Nature, and some hundreds he certainly painted without having Nature before his eyes. Is such a thing legitimate? Is it absurd? May it not be necessary sometimes?”

In light of the mind’s capacity to autonomously invent, one must be left with uncertainty over connoisseurship generally. The hypothesis of Professor Rudolfo Llinas from New York University is that “seeing is just another form of dreaming; dreaming and being awake are next of kin if not exactly the same thing… the brain is about making images.” Conversely, if the visual can be explained sensibly using content and style arguments, then surely, connoisseurship deserves due regard despite the potential pitfalls, or delusions. There was little choice under the circumstances anyway, Christie’s held complete sway over the situation and Butlin’s opinion was to be their final word.

 Included in the same letter was an extensive interpretation of the letter to Thomas Griffith dated . It was the possible reference by Turner to Shipwreck, the Rescue calling it a Stormy Picture.

The word ‘Hero’ in Turner’s letter could have led to the assumption that the “stormy” painting under discussion was Hero and Leander, but this idea was dismissed by John Gage in his book on Turner’s correspondence. Not impossible, but it would be somewhat difficult to believe that a painting that was exhibited at the R.A. in 1837 (Hero and Leander) would so desperately need dragging from its dark abode for lining and cleaning seven years later.

The condition had suffered according to Walter Thornbury, but even if it were bad enough to need such radical treatment, the image of Hero and Leander, although ominous, does not necessarily bring to mind a full-blown storm. Luke Herrmann calls it “turbulent splendour.” In 1807, when negotiating a trade of paintings to regain ownership of Shipwreck (My younger brother), JMW again used the title, Storm to describe the painting. What “stormy picture” was he describing in his letter to ? I’m certain it was me.

In the above letter, the discussion centered on the more vulnerable “early large works” and their state of dirty neglect. You should have seen me back then, I was a mess! I was painted early in 1802. Yeah, that would be about it, 1802. JMW wrote Griffith that the “large Pictures I am rather fond of tho it is a pity they are subject to neglect and dirt… if I could find a young man acquainted with picture cleaning and would help me to clean accidental stains away, would be a happiness to drag them from their dark abode. The Stormy Picture you saw in the Parlour for Mr. Foord’s Hero to advise with about cleaning and lining…” Yep, now I remember.

The noteworthy interest that Griffith showed for that neglected “stormy picture” in 1844 was clearly the inducement for JMW, in the autumn of his life, to refurbish me for presentation to his dear friend, respected business associate and executor—Thomas Griffith. As noted earlier, according to Christie’s archive, I was presented by JMW to Mr. Griffiths [sic]. “Turner sometimes, but seldom gave away pictures,” although Thornbury discovered that he seldom visited Mr. Griffith without bringing his touched proofs as a present. The generosity JMW felt towards his friendly agent was sincere.

JMW proposed to gift a picture to each of his executors including Thomas Griffith, presumably for their services in that official capacity. In one of the codicils to his will Turner proposed leaving each of his executors a picture. “They were to choose for themselves.” The Chancery court decided that Turner was not competent at the time he added this particular codicil to his will and thus cancelled it.

JMW had been known to trade artwork for services in the past and particularly with . One oft-quoted case took place in 1842, when sold nine watercolours for Turner. Ten were painted and Ruskin tells us that the tenth was offered to the dealer in lieu of commission. “ agreed, and Turner went home content.” It’s hard to remember exactly what happened back then, but if I was not entirely a gift, I may have been bartered to , and remained a somewhat private holding in memory of JMW. Ruskin wrote a friend that other artworks had often been exchanged for a Turner picture.

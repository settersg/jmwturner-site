Art-Science Reverie

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Art-Science Reverie

Now what’s Bob scribbling?

“Connoisseurship takes experience and education and is necessary to differentiate genuine original paintings from later versions; including collaborative efforts between master and pupil, copies done by studio assistants, contemporary followers, late followers, or even forgers. The false premise that the archetype is immeasurably more important has of course led to a greater reliance on the ‘art expert.’ This is regardless of the expert’s connoisseurship. Scholarship must necessarily be combined with proper science or whatever additional corroborative evidence to ensure that the art collector/consumer is protected from error in judgment or outright dishonesty by crook or courtier…” How in the world does that tie in with My story? I’m sure it must or else it wouldn’t be on his mind right now…We’ll see.

Turner, the old wizard! He was affected as much as anyone by advances in science and technology. A real Renaissance man he was… a zealous and intelligent student of the movement towards exact knowledge.  I wondered at times if he wasn’t a member of one of those esoteric societies, like Francis Bacon started. The irony is; today there are fears about combining science with art, it’s been a step backwards for certain. Could the following 1887 diatribe by nineteenth-century Turner expert, James Orrock be the first challenge by connoisseurship against science invading the purview of the art establishment?

 The chemist, in his analysis and experiments with pigments… has never so much as touched the practice of the watercolour painter and its results, and it is confidently submitted by Mr. Orrock that the chemist never can. What has the exposure under known test conditions of a simple colour to do with the exposure of infinite mixtures and manipulations of semi-tones and infinities of compound colours, which express the genius of the magician of the Art, as Turner expressed it in one of his wondrous aerial and brilliant Landscapes? James was one of my favourite owners.

 At the time, this argument against science’s “interference” was won by force of rhetoric. The actual truth turned out to be contrary to Orrock’s argument. It was eventually accepted that watercolour pigment is in fact damaged by exposure to light. Rhetoricians are still the winners of most modern debates over such issues, and to go one step further, such arguments are usually made to support special interests.

There was an interesting contest at a 2002 IFAR conference. A query was lobbed at one of the team who had just completed the catalogue raisonné on Georgia O’Keeffe. The banter took place during the question and answer period after the cataloguers made their presentation. It was recorded in the IFAR journal as follows:

Question: In addition to your examination of all the sheets of paper, did you do technical analysis of the media, for example the pigments?

Answer: We didn’t. It wasn’t practical to do it. The problem with any kind of analysis such as you describe is that it relies very much on the scientists doing it. Technical analysis is not magically correct because it’s done with a machine. And so the scientist who does the work has a lot of subjective control over what he or she finds…”

 As science is the least subjective of any method of analysis, it is possible that the point the cataloguer followed with had more bearing on the matter: “Yes, but it is incredibly time consuming.”

 In the world of art, scientific solutions are not always as clear-cut as one would expect. Nonetheless, Marilyn Laver, Bob’s champion in this realm was to try her best to analyze me—Shipwreck, the Rescue. Much to his relief, she was able to gather all the available records from her previous analysis in 1982. It took several months to draw together the results and provide a synopsis. The package Bob was waiting for with such anticipation finally arrived but unfortunately it didn’t prove anything about who painted me.

The paradox of scientific study is that although seeking to be absolutely conclusive in its findings, it can do little to prove art authorship conclusively. It is more effectively exploited by law enforcement to prove fraud than by art historians and collectors to prove if an object is genuine. From paint samples for example, “pigment identification can deny but rarely confirm a painting’s age.”

If any miniscule bit of data places a painting out of context (as the agents in the Visnei case were hoping would happen) the entire artwork can be rejected. If certain pigments, canvas, paper, wooden supports, or working techniques were present on a picture but were never used prior to a certain date; then a painting purporting to be earlier would automatically be condemned unless there was an explanation for said incongruity. Yet, if innumerable similarities are found that connect favorably to an artist, such as art materials, subject matter, composition, format, brush techniques, palette of colours, and so on, but only a sliver of this information is atypical—what then?

What if the preeminent authority on a particular artist disregards the whole raft of information gathered because of the errant albeit inconclusive bits of datum and takes a position more favourable to his or her more narrowly defined agenda? This does happen, and when it does, one can only speculate whether the reason goes beyond integrity. There can be solid reasons for dismissal, such as those relating to overall style, but there are also many rejections that are largely ungrounded and illogical. In 1938, Frank Jewett Mather, Jr. argued: “It seems to me…that most of our art historians are entirely untrained in historical method; hence have no objective check upon their subjective, stylistic intuitions.”

Because of its importance as an aesthetic tool and the foundation on which true art passions owe homage, I hate to appear hostile toward connoisseurship, but in order to get at the truth and away from the politics, this derision is necessary in hopes of encouraging scientists to take a more confident role. They must be encouraged to state their conclusions with authority rather than compliance.

To exemplify the profound affect a small group, or for that matter, an individual can have on the world of art, we need look no further than the renowned Wildenstein dynasty. Established in the 1870s, it has grown more influential over the ensuing three generations by virtue of erudition, stealth, accumulation of wealth and influence, and above all, the production of catalogues. “Being the publishers of such books confers enormous power – [and among other things] the ability to authenticate paintings…as a publisher of catalogue raisonnés, the Wildensteins virtually control the scholarship on many artists from Boucher to Monet.”

This frustrated one art historian, and with regard to the Wildenstein Gauguin catalogue, rightly or wrongly, he felt that “pedigrees and quotations had been juggled to suit private needs, and that Gauguin’s oeuvre has been shorn of some authentic works and adulterated with others that did not belong.” What about Rembrandt; and those who have been charged since 1968 to write a comprehensive catalogue of his paintings and “especially to free his oeuvre of the accretions that were thought to have disfigured the image of Rembrandt the painter.” One must contemplate whether implicit in this manifesto is the need to preserve only what the Rembrandt Research Project (RRP) considers to be Rembrandt’s better works? In her role as an analytical statistician, Geraldine Keen gives pause for thought over what it means to control any market:

At any one time there is usually one particular scholar whose opinion is considered the most authoritative about the work of a particular artist, though sometimes several scholars may contend for this distinction. In expressing their opinions they may wield tremendous financial power. For instance, no definitive catalogue of Rembrandt’s drawings existed until the 1950s when Otto Benesch published the results of his long years of research. Around 1950 three drawings catalogued as ’’ were sold at Christie’s for about $280; another, attributed by Sotheby’s to one of Rembrandt’s contemporaries, fetched only $25. By the time Benesch’s catalogue was published, its inclusion of these drawings had raised their value to 28,000 - 42,000 each.

  What about Turner, and me—Shipwreck, the Rescue? Bob disparaged, in a reply letter to Marilyn Laver: “a conclusion as to authorship in all cases would be based on probability unless an honest J.M.W. Turner was to come back to life. It seems almost futile to approach authentication of a picture by scientific analysis when critical authorities, even within the scientific camp, attempt to discredit a painting based on a meager number of anomalies.”

Why the frustration? It was because of the response forwarded to him by Marilyn. It was from one of her colleagues in , Dr. Joyce H. Townsend. This Senior Conservation Scientist at the Tate Gallery had responded to her inquiry over the painting (from her letter dated October, 1997): “The join in the canvas [my canvas] is not typical, as Turner could evidently obtain coarse hessian supports as wide as he required them. I know of only one which has a joined canvas, and it is larger than this one: The Garden of the Hesperides at the Tate Gallery with a horizontal join almost in the centre.”

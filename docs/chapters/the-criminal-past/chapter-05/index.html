Chapter 5: Visnei, Provenance or...

Chapter 5

Visnei, Provenance or…

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Visnei, Provenance or

Bob’s been scribbling again… In business parlance, rarity equates to increased monetary value; in a museum context it equates to greater prestige. Stealthy management of art resources elevates the influence of expert opinion and those involved in giving it. For example, when much of an artist’s ‘body of work’ is either ignored or condemned surreptitiously… Enough of that! This quasi-conspiratorial tone is just like Bob. I suggest instead that much of this dark imagery can be laid to rest by proposing something much less sinister. It’s a paradigm shift, in a way. With my model most participants play out their roles without fully understanding the implications of their actions. Most are just dumb followers of what is going on, of course some have vested interests but most are just plain oblivious!

“ is three time zones away. Let’s see, that would make it shortly after …” Bob sat muttering to himself attempting to get his thoughts together. He wanted to field a few questions with a conservation scientist who had worked for the Canadian Conservation Institute (CCI) in for a number of years. She was now operating a related business in Toronto, Marilyn Laver, P. Eng.

Bob was stunned to learn that back in 1982, she had worked as the chief conservation scientist charged with heading up scientific testing on the very painting he had phoned about—me! I could have told him that. The Mounties (RCMP) and Interpol had sent me to her for scientific analysis in the course of a previous investigation.

This bizarre discovery left Bob with many unanswered questions. Rather than searching only for art provenance, he now felt part of a covert scheme. One nagging question he’d been haunted with was now partially answered. It was to do with a strange call he received shortly after buying me, and just after talking to Mr. Dowding, the lawyer. An assertive voice demanded, “Be careful what you do with the painting you bought on Sunday; I’m friends with the RCMP member, Montague; the one who did a criminal investigation involving it twenty years ago. The owner went to prison!” Bob shook his head. He later confided to a friend, “I don’t know who it was but by the intonation of her voice she could have been teamed-up quite comfortably with Maxwell Smart of 1960s TV fame.” Dumbfounded at the harebrained provocation, and with confidence fortified by his recent discussion with Mr. Dowding, he boldly rebuked her, “That’s not true. Nobody went to jail.” The phone went dead!

What was the reason for trying to confuse and intimidate Bob? Not only had this farce raised his curiosity, it had also raised his ire. He chastised himself on subsequent occasions for not getting the caller’s number. “I could have demanded a curtain call,” he lamented.

In short order, Marilyn Laver sent Bob a cost estimate to cover her time and expenses for gathering fragments of data from the previous tests done on me. The sum was minor compared to what an analysis from scratch would have cost. For this he thanked “the people of .” It was their tax dollars that paid for the work done on me two decades earlier.

In 1982, the investigation of a Mr. Visnei was wrapped up instantly after he fled the country. I, the subject of the investigation, was somehow being used as bail collateral at the time, and after the collapse of the proceedings I was hurriedly packed in my crate and shipped back to . Consequently, the Canadian Conservation Institute (CCI) never completed a full report on me, and because the defendant had skipped bail and disappeared, all forty square feet of me was liquidated at one of those police auctions in . It was an attempt to recover some of the trial expenses.

The months wore on and Marilyn Laver went about her task, traveling back and forth between and collating her files from the former investigation. Because of her earlier tenure with the CCI, she was considered a colleague and had a comfortable working relationship with them. At times she would send a photograph of me: one time, black and white, another ultraviolet, then infrared, and eventually a very professional normal colour photograph. They were all in large format and were copies of those taken at the time of the investigation.

                   Ultra violet photograph of Shipwreck, the Rescue

It was comforting that the process was moving along at a steady pace. Bob took great interest in the scientific techniques used in gathering the necessary data for the investigation, partly because of his vested interests, but also because of his own degree in science. had a sophisticated research capability even back then when scientific studies on works of art were at a formative stage internationally. The CCI was first established in 1972.

Marilyn sent Bob the odd pamphlet and academic paper relating to Turner’s standard working methods. These were published from studies done subsequent to the 1982 investigation, and ironically, they were published by the Tate Gallery in , which at the time of the investigation had little to share with the CCI.

Meantime I stood like an encapsulated fossil at the back of Bob’s garage. Packed tightly in my sarcophagus of plywood and Styrofoam—alone. Those were trying times. After about six months of entombment I received an unlikely visitor. An out-of-town friend stopped for an overnight visit with Bob and after dinner he insisted on seeing the “would-be Turner.” Because it took two people to wrestle me from my crate and into the light, Bob was more than willing to take him up on his offer.

His helper was G. T. W. Edwards, an anthropologist by dedication and a pharmacist by profession. William’s keen sense of observation came from years of studying and cataloguing Native American herbal remedies, customs, and artifacts at the remote Bella Coola Reservation, British Columbia. The native studies had led him towards a D. Phil. in community medicine at . They cracked my crate, just a hair. “Ahh,” the fresh breeze was intoxicating. Like a methodical automaton moving with stout linear ease, William gradually made his way around my shipping crate. He was a middle-aged graceful man of manners and culture, but seemed somewhat slowed by his gentleness. Dry as parchment, his keen wit bubbled up and broke the surface only on occasion.

They dragged me and my crate from the garage and into the sunlight of mid-afternoon. The day was as bright as it was at Frosty’s, about a year ago when Bob first saw me, but it was considerably warmer. Behind my back William paused—silently. Soon he started snapping pictures of my stretcher, including a new discovery. His keen eye had registered a tiny two-centimetre fragment of paper on the top left corner of my stretcher. Darkened with age, the “air-burn” as it is often called had camouflaged it during previous scrutiny. On the corner that remained of this unpretentious scrap was printed, “THOS. (?)…” It was not until later that a possible connection to a previous owner would be suspected. This added one more interesting piece to the growing puzzle. Uncovering this artifact made William’s day, and as Bob wrenched the last clasp closed and the two of them struggled my crate back into the garage, William hummed with self-satisfied contentment.

Bob finally got motivated to track down the court documents promised by Mr. Dowding, the lawyer. He had given him his home phone number in Maple Ridge, just outside . His phone rang many times and just as Bob was about to give up, a vaporous voice answered. After several clues, Mr. Dowding finally remembered Bob. Frightening indeed! He always believed that when the time was right, he could approach Mr. Dowding and prod him for the key to unlock his part of my mysterious history. By now, it seemed as though he was having difficulty getting the key out of his pocket, never mind into the lock. He was getting older and the provocation required to prompt his recollection of their previous conversation assured Bob that his call was none too soon.

Alternatively, it was assumed that if unable to get the necessary information from Mr. Dowding, the Freedom of Information Act would ensure access to the necessary documents. But this reasoning appeared flawed. According to a local crown prosecutor court records are periodically destroyed—purged every three years! Mr. Dowding was all Bob had! To discover what happened in court twenty years ago must become his main focus and it needed doing before the inevitable loss of his only source of information.

During his first conversation with Mr. Dowding, Bob was assured that there were pertinent documents somewhere in his files. It gave comfort hearing him repeat this same claim. Bob called back several times hoping to hear good news, but ultimately, his records were not to be found. Bob’s sinking feeling was buoyed slightly when he volunteered to go down to the courthouse and look for documents. The journey never took place! The only thing Bob accomplished with his persistent calling was to aggravate the tired old gent.

Two months later and much to Bob’s surprise he received a call. Mr. Dowding had been clearing clutter from his home office and came across the court document for the examination and cross-examination of Montague, the agent provocateur. He gave another elevating and crucial snippet of information during the short talk; information that dealt with a subpoenaed auction house representative who stated during the trial that, “Shipwreck, the Rescue was sold in their sales room.”

According to Mr. Dowding other information was given at the time confirming that I had first come from the estate of one of Turner’s executors. He did not have any supporting papers, but assured Bob that to the best of his recollections it was true. With renewed enthusiasm Bob opted to overlook the lawyer’s previous failure of memory, and accepted this as a turning point in his struggle to get the historical provenance! Bob made arrangements to retrieve Graham from UBC and stop by Mr. Dowding’s home on the outskirts of to pick up the document.

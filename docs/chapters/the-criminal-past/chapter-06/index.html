Chapter 6: Interpol, Mounties, and the Hapless Sting

Chapter 6

Interpol, Mounties, and the Hapless Sting

Excerpt from Art World’s Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters

Interpol, Mounties, and the Hapless Sting

Bob spent the next two days reading and re-reading the section of the trial documents Dowding rescued from his cluttered office. It was the segment where the undercover agent was examined and cross-examined. This was also the same RCMP member about whom Bob received that mysterious and menacing phone call just after buying me.

The questions were repetitive and dry, but by the end he had a clear image of what happened over the duration of the investigation. My unfortunate owner at the time, on thin suspicion of wrongdoing had been subjected to the full force of police high jinks, until finally—or so it seemed—he was coaxed to commit an offence. In a scattergun approach at gathering evidence, the Mounties commissioned tests on five of Visnei’s paintings, including myself. We were all labeled as potential forgeries. The other accused included a Gainsborough, a Picasso, and two Collins’. Alas, Visnei, worried over the outcome of these bizarre machinations, skipped the country.

The problems for Visnei started when he introduced yours truly to a doctor in . Having recently been disheartened by an unsatisfactory deal with an acquaintance of Visnei, the incredulous doctor instigated the investigation by reporting misgivings about Visnei and me to the RCMP. Once the locomotive was stoked and Visnei was bound to the tracks, the police made their run.

To negotiate my sale Visnei met the agent for the first time at Doctor Crow’s suite. The second confab culminated with Montague, Crow, and Visnei heading out of town to a large bungalow where I was stored. The cloak and dagger mischief reached its zenith as the agent’s provocative banter stimulated Visnei to expand the program. Their negotiations advanced to include another painting that was being held as collateral for a 600,000 dollar loan at a Zurich bank—The Last Supper by El Greco. Visnei wanted to retire his loan on it by bumping the deal to include both me and El Greco for a cool two million dollars.

During this international-flavoured negotiation, inaccurate currency conversions took place and the interpretation of documents in foreign languages became a real problem. In fact, according to the accused, some very relevant documents eventually disappeared from police custody. There were still two credentials; the El Greco ones in Spanish, and my document in German. Neither had been translated yet, or if so, it was certainly denied under oath:

Dowding (D) questioned the RCMP agent, Cpl. Montague (M):

So, as far as the El Greco is concerned, have you any information on it at all, as to its validity or otherwise?

  M. No, Your Honour, I don’t.

D. But you did have documents you could have had translated which are exhibits here that appear to authenticate an El Greco, [the] Last Supper.

M. Well –

D. Did you have it translated?

M. I never had a translator.

D. Has anybody else done so?

M. Not to my knowledge, no.

D. Has anyone ever given them to a Spanish speaking person to interpret?

M. I can’t answer that, Your Honour, I don’t know.

D. And same with the German documents of authenticity; did anyone translate those, and do you have copies of any translations?

M. No, Your Honour, I can’t answer that question; I just don’t have that knowledge.

 It might have been wise before proceeding with a full-blown sting and prosecution, for the agents to get these relevant documents translated; they were crucial papers and the foundation on which Visnei was supporting the claims of authenticity. It is not as though translation was out of the RCMP’s scope; John Sawatshy’s bestseller Men in the Shadows describes their facility as more than adequate. “The functions of E Section (Electronic Surveillance) proper are routine enough, it has responsibility for transcribing wiretapped and bugged conversations for investigators and analysts to study. Many of the targets are foreigners and often the conversations need translation, so E section is filled with translators and stenographers.” By the way, paintings like me can’t actually read, but Bob has a tendency to read aloud on occasion and I have big ears.

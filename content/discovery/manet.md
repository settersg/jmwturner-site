![](/jmwturner-site/images/landscape%20detailsm.jpg)The
complexities of art appraisal, art evaluation, and art authentication are such
that most of those with an advanced understanding of the art world are loath to
get involved unless they have something to gain.
In the museum setting experts are most often unwilling to speak out in
support of a newly discovered or re-discovered painting because of orders from
senior institutional bureaucracy. Terms and expressions such as society,
institute, community of experts, research project are somewhat euphemistic.
Members of such self-supporting groups are in fact allies in a battle against
litigation first and foremost.
Most museum curators and institutional experts have little to gain and a lot
to lose if they wrongly accept a "dud" as authentic; and thus, in an attempt to
distance themselves from responsibility they more often become an obstruction to
those seeking a proper identification for their art work. Ultimately, it is
important to remember that a picture is often condemned for more complex
reasons than because it is spurious or fake. The following are areas that

Manet on the Seine
During the summer of 1874 Manet painted scenes of the Seine at Argenteuil
alongside Renoir and Monet, the В‘arch-exponents of ImpressionismВ’.В  This period
marked, В“one of the most important periods in impressionism, Monet, Manet, and
Renoir painted side by side and succeeded in further developing their personal
styles.В”[[i]](#_edn1)В 
This also led to an intermixing of ideas resulting in much influence on each
otherВ’s work.В  MonetВ’s influence on Manet is apparent in Boating Near Rueil
(seen above) and several other В‘seinescapesВ’:В  В“ManetВ’s association with Monet
led to a change in his work, but it was very much a cross-fertilization with
each artist learning from each other at different times.В”[[ii]](#_edn2)В 
This В‘cross-fertilizationВ’ caused Manet to embrace certain impressionist ideals
while retaining elements of his earlier style. Works such as Boating
(portrait detail above), Boating Near Rueil, and The Seine Banks at
Argentueil (seen here![](/jmwturner-site/images/seine%20banks%20at%20A%20adj.jpg))
all exemplify a shift in ManetВ’s style while demonstrating his unwillingness to
completely conform to the impressionist movement.
The practice of painting en plein air (in open air) is an important
characteristic of the impressionist movement.В  In 1869, В“Monet and Renoir, whose
figures and compositions done in the open air had been inspired by Gustave
Courbet, painted together at Bougival, a small village on the Seine.В”[[iii]](#_edn3)В 
In an effort to capture В‘the dynamism and joyВ’ of a picturesque wharf which was
always surrounded by a, В“restless confusion of boats and women in gaily colored
dresses,В”[[iv]](#_edn4)
each artist painted their version of La GrenouillГЁre en plein air.В  After
the Franco-Prussian war, Monet settled in Argenteuil and in 1872, В“began
painting the new type of open-air landscapes.В”[[v]](#_edn5)В 
These paintings, which, В“were dominated by the phantasms of light, attracted
Renoir, Sisley and Manet.В”[[vi]](#_edn6)В 
This attraction led Manet to spend the pivotal summer of 1874 in Argenteuil;
however, certain evidence suggests that Manet never fully accepted the practice
of В‘plein airВ’ painting.В  For example, it is thought that Boating was
painted in the studio because of its large size (38 ВјВ” x 51 ВјВ”). It was В“much
larger than the portable canvases Monet and Renoir were using at this time and
would suggest it was done in the studio.В”[[vii]](#_edn7)
The В“rather contrived nature of the compositionВ”[[viii]](#_edn8)
would also support this theory.В  Argenteuil is another example of a work
during this period that was doubtfully painted en plein air.В  Firstly,
the size of the canvas (60В” x 45В”) was В“much larger than the typical
impressionist canvases, which had to be easily portable to facilitate plein air
practice.В”[[ix]](#_edn9)В 
Also, В“the apparent spontaneity of the work belies its rigorously premeditated
system of horizontals and verticalsВ”[[x]](#_edn10)
As Manet started to abandon the В‘contrived natureВ’ of his compositions for a
freer, В“more spontaneous approachВ”[[xi]](#_edn11)
his canvases started to decrease in size. В Boating Near Rueil is smaller
(30В” x 40В”) which rendered it more portable; and thus,В  it was conceivably
completely or partially executed from life.В  The loose and spontaneous brushwork
apparent in the background suggests that Manet was trying to capture a fleeting
impression, possibly before the loss of daylight.В  The model, however, appears
to be carefully painted and both it and the area near the hat exhibit extensive
petimenti.В Unlike Monet, Manet was known for his changes, those made to achieve
a more balanced and harmonious composition.В  In The Seine Banks at Argenteuil
the size of the canvas decreases once again (24В” x 39 ВјВ”).В  This smallerВ 
more portable canvas is almost exactly the same size as MonetВ’s The Regatta
at Argenteuil.В  The subject matter of Manet's painting is described by Linda
Bolton as: В“pure ImpressionistВ”[[xii]](#_edn12)
and, В“the broken brushwork, which conveys a sense of movement, is also in the
Impressionist idiomВ…В”[[xiii]](#_edn13)
Boating Near Rueil undeniably hasВ  Impressionist elements, however
it is clearly only a stage in ManetВ’s evolution, one which culminated in works
such as The Seine Banks at Argenteuil and Claude Monet painting in his
Studio Boat.
В В В В В Manet described Monet as, В“The Raphael of water." His high regard for
this idea was revealed when attempting to emulate MonetВ’s ability to depict
water.В  This admiration led Manet to, В“attempt to catch the fleeting effects of
light and water that so entranced Monet.В”[[xiv]](#_edn14)В 
Manet also felt that Monet had an understanding of waterВ’s mystery and В“all of
its moods.В” Consequently, Manet took Monet's lead in this regard and tried to
emulate similar reflections in water.[[xv]](#_edn15)В 
In works such as The Seine Banks at Argenteuil and Boating Near Rueil
MonetВ’s influence is easily observed as Manet uses broken, directional, and bold
brushwork to capture the reflections and the effects of light.В  Although at this
time Manet employed a more directional brushstroke than in his earlier work, he
never came close to the formality that Monet applied it in works such as The
Regatta at Argenteuil.В  Manet, however, did not limit himself to this type
of brushwork; in Boating the water has a much more flat, non-directional
application of paint.В  It was apparent that Manet admired and tried to emulate
MonetВ’s ability to delineate reflections in water; however, unlike Monet, В“he
did not record the reflections in the water with any close attention to their
actual appearance.В”[[xvi]](#_edn16)В 
In Boating Near Rueil the reflections of the modelВ’s dress and the stern
of the boat are vaguely suggested with a tonal change and oblique brushstrokes,
В“whereas MonetВ’s reflections were always closely observed, spreading down
directly below the objects reflected.В”[[xvii]](#_edn17)
The transition of ManetВ’s style during his summer at Argenteuil can be easily
observed in his three paintings: Boating, Boating Near Rueil and The
Seine Banks at Argenteuil.В  As aforementioned, ManetВ’s execution of water
frequently mirrored the influence of Monet; however, other changes are also
apparent in other areas of the works.В  For example, in these three paintings
ManetВ’s shift from an emphasis on figural to landscape is very distinct.В As a
general rule, В“figures were always more important to him than landscapes.В”[[xviii]](#_edn18)В Manet
made an observable shift in emphasis from the figure; as seen in Boating,
to landscape; as seen in The Seine Banks at Argenteuil, with Boating
Near Rueil falling somewhere in between.В  The figure in the painting is
still given a prominent location that demands attention; however, it does not
monopolize the picture as is the case with Boating. The Seine Banks at
Argenteuil marks the final stage in ManetВ’s transition, В“the figures,
usually of paramount importance in ManetВ’s paintings, are here merely an
incidental part of the landscape.В”[[xix]](#_edn19)В This
gradual transformation has been interpreted as Manet, В“submitting himself both
to the landscape and to the technique of his younger colleagues.В”[[xx]](#_edn20)В 
В ManetВ’s tendency to allocate more importance to certain elements in his
paintings, namely his figures, В“contradicts the ImpressionistsВ’ almost
egalitarian approach to the landscape В– where one element is rendered with as
much or as little importance to the next.В”[[xxi]](#_edn21)В 
Further contradicting this approach Manet endeavored to emphasize his modelВ’s
individuality.В  Manet accomplishes this with the use of, В“clothes, particularly
hats, to provide emphasis and bring out the individuality of a face.В”[[xxii]](#_edn22)
ManetВ’s use of clothing in his works also brings out the individuality of his
models due to social attitudes of the day.В  The following extract from a
contemporary magazine illustrates the contemporary ladyВ’s mentality towards
their fashion:
В 
In 1869 the EnglishwomanВ’s Domestic Magazine
reported on a visit to a couturiere en vogue that stated: В‘Each lady
comes to ask me not what is worn but what has not yet even been seen or wornВ…В’[[xxiii]](#_edn23)
В 
Because ladies were desirous to be clothed in an original and distinctive
way, it would make it very improbable for them to allow their clothes (i.e.
hats) to be worn by other ladies. For this reason, when examining works painted
by Manet, it is possible to identify the models simply by using their clothing
as a type of character trait.В  For example, Suzanne Manet can be identified in
On the Beach, and The Swallows because she is wearing the
same hat in both works.В  Madame Monet can also be identified in The Seine
Banks at Argenteuil and The Monet Family in the Garden because she
also wears her hat in both works.В  Following this rationale it can be deduced
that the same model is posing for both Boating Near Rueil and Boating,
as the hats are identical and only appear in these two works.В  This emphasis on
the individuality of his models shows ManetВ’s tendency, В“to select from the
scene and emphasize certain elements.В”[[xxiv]](#_edn24)В 
This undeniably opposes the В‘egalitarian approachВ’ of the Impressionist idiom;
however, it exemplifies his interest in figures that would dominate his future
works such as Un Bar aux Folies- BergГЁre.
An arch-characteristic of the impressionist movement was their use of colour
in regards to colour theory of the day:
The impressionists were particularly concerned with the truthfulness of
colour, understanding that no colour stands unaffected by the colour of its
neighbor, techniques were developed of optical colour blending in which the
viewers eye would combine adjoining patches of seemingly inappropriate colour
into a new and suitable (perceived) colour.[[xxv]](#_edn25)
В 
Although, В“Manet was tremendously influenced by the lighter paletteВ…of his
two colleaguesВ”[[xxvi]](#_edn26)
he never completely adopted their theories.В  The Seine Banks at Argenteuil,
said to be the culmination of ManetВ’s impressionist evolution, is a great
example of ManetВ’s appreciation of his younger colleaguesВ’ theories without
adopting them in their entirety.В  In this work ManetВ’s use of, В“color, although
light and bright, is not used in the Impressionist prismatic manner, nor does
Manet seem concerned with a precise color notation.В”[[xxvii]](#_edn27)В 
A very distinctive difference exists between ManetВ’s use of colour in regards to
Monet and Renoir: В“В…although he lightened and brightened his palette in response
to his friendsВ’ theories, he did not restrict himself to primary colors,
continuing to use black and some earth colours.В”[[xxviii]](#_edn28).В 
In his work The Seine Banks at Argenteuil ManetВ’s decision to paint the
hulls of the boats black, В“ [a] pigment outlawed by the orthodox
impressionists,В”[[xxix]](#_edn29)
showed his reluctance to abandon the teachings of his master, Couture.
Manet has been long considered the father of the impressionist movement.В 
This has, however, caused misconceptions about his attitude and involvement in
the movement.В  Although Manet admired Monet and RenoirВ’s artistic ability he
never completely adopted their style and theories in his own work.В  During the
summer of 1874 ManetВ’s admiration for the impressionists, namely Monet, caused
him to selectively apply what he liked to his own style.В  As a result his style
took a decisive shift in which many similarities can be found between his work
and that of Monet.В  However, Manet also chose to retain elements from his
previous style refusing to completely conform to the impressionist movement.В  In
ManetВ’s most impressionist seinescape, The Seine Banks of Argenteuil,
components such as his treatment of the water, the loose spontaneous brushwork,
light palette, and close attention to landscape all denote an Impressionist
inspired work.В  However, in works such as Boating Near Rueil and
Boating ManetВ’s unwavering affinity for his models contradict the
Impressionist egalitarian approach where no one element is more important than
the next.В  Manet also refused to adopt the ImpressionistВ’s colour theory and
continued to use the В‘outlawedВ’ black pigment.В  Upon examining several of
ManetВ’s works done during his summer in Argenteuil it becomes very obvious that
Monet and the ImpressionistВ’s had an impact on ManetВ’s work; however, Manet
never came to embrace certain key elements that characterized the impressionist
movement. (Graham Setters 1998, from 2nd term paper,
Herstmonceux, Queen's University)

**Chapter 18**

## Muddled Intentions

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

Muddled Intentions

Government regulation of historic artwork is one peripheral issue that concerns art experts and affects the value of artwork. It is easier to move, sell, export, and bequeath decorative art—that which does not fall into the restrictive category of national treasure. Once catalogued and traceable it becomes risky to do anything with it across borders without proper documentation. And by now I had slipped out of  without being duly recognized. Who had been responsible for this faux pas?

The state of affairs for cultivated art lovers in  is even more tenuous. The unfortunate French not only have counter-productive import and export regulations, but “they are taxed on their net worth, and as a result they are often reluctant to disclose ownership of valuable works of art.” It seems unjust that because some have a penchant for beautiful and rare objects, they are penalized by way of wealth taxes. “This type of taxation is certainly detrimental to genteel aspirations and indubitably fosters philistinism,” I’m quoting Bob here.

Competition between public institutions is one reason that certain important artwork is disparaged. As each competes to increase its holdings, monies by way of benefaction, attendance for their exhibitions, prestige, and government support; it becomes evident why a request for help in assessing a painting that comes from outside their own clique might go unheeded. It must be acknowledged that since the ‘competitive public museum’ has become politically and economically dominant in the art world, things have irreversibly changed. Disregard by curators for the private collector seems forever entrenched.

Irrationally, this is the same authority needed to bestow surnames to misunderstood “orphans,” those not yet rediscovered masterpieces. Such indifference or conflict of interest is disheartening when I contemplate how many of my brethren have likely been discounted. Soon it would become glaringly apparent to Graham and Bob that JMW’s oeuvre in particular has endured the cruel scrubbing of an unkind “guardian!”

Several years ago, Bob received a gracious letter from Alistair Smith. It came after an audience with him and the restorer, Anthony Reeve at the National Gallery in . In it, he categorically confirmed the exclusion of outsiders: “As a member of staff of the National Gallery, I am not allowed to authenticate paintings in private hands, and while I do not wish to be obstructive, I would be very grateful if you would remove my name from any printed materials which you are to issue.”

This attitude is prevalent within universities as well. Much of the present art establishment propagates the belief that all significant and relevant art should be public art, and in many cases public property. In his autobiography written over a quarter century ago, the former longtime curator of the National Gallery in Washington, John Walker, was already concerned over the shift in museum politics, but at the time defined it in terms of a clouded search for “relevance (a term its proponents never clearly define). The desire to be considered ‘relevant,’ vague as it is, seems to denote fewer funds for collecting and more for social purposes.” He still believed that museums should be places of enjoyment and enlightenment, and should not function in community relations, in solving racial problems, in propaganda for any cause.

Henry McBride, formerly America’s most prominent art critic, who started with The New York Sun in 1913 and finished with its demise in 1950, was incensed by what was happening around him. “In the 1930s he bore down heavily on propaganda in art which he felt was swamping the American scene.” Add the edict Berenson affixed to his post-doctoral study centre in : “I want this Institute to promote aesthetical and humanistic, rather than philosophical and antiquarian interests,” and a picture of the political climate that led up to the modern era begins to emerge.

In , the influential critic, Anthony Blunt, chose to see in Marxism, the cure for every aesthetic ill. He believed that “the New Realists, the Mexican painters Diego Rivera and José Orozco had demonstrated the way to paint the human condition without compromising their aesthetic responsibility…The great advantage of socialism was that it would selectively retain what was valuable in bourgeois culture and jettison the rest. The state, all controlling, enlightened and wise, would take over the role once played by the patron.” One does not have to think that Marxism was necessarily the root evil here. In 1930s Germany, with Mein Kampf, Hitler echoed the same manipulative sentiment when he insisted that theatre, art, literature, films, the press, posters, and window displays had to be “regulated to serve the state and civilization.”

This concept of institutional and government control was eventually also adopted by the British art establishment, but in their case it was embraced mainly to promote national and commercial interests. Even Kenneth Clark in the 1930s was a short-term proponent of this ostensibly unworkable ideology. There is no doubt that  was then sympathetic to socialist aims. “It is evident,” he wrote to his mother early in the Second World War, “that socialism is the only solution. A centralized government would be bound to have flaws but to have profit as the only motive is even more dreadful.”

 Little did he realize that eventually his youthful mutterings would in a paradoxical fashion become somewhat reality. Yes, overwhelming control would develop, but it would be positioned mostly in the hands of the aristocratically managed British government. The elite few in control were to benefit commercially to an even greater extent than the private collector had ever done. The further one moves away from  the weaker the grip; but assuredly, the probing fingers of this gentrified class wander internationally.

With such eminent art historians and leaders reading from the same page, it is no wonder that the direction of attitudes has shifted away from the individual collector. An intellectual Marxist like Blunt needed little persuasion to believe that the best hope of preserving common European heritage from what Steiner terms “prostitution of the money market’ was by allying himself with the Communist system that not only promised but ostensibly did make public all private art collections.” It is interesting to note that Martin Butlin attended the Courtauld Institute during Anthony Blunt’s tenure.

Bob’s lack of success in dealing with Alistair Smith at the National Gallery was the first time he had experienced the art world running scared. For many years John Walker had encouraged the vetting of art at the National Gallery in , and “he always felt it was wrong that so many museums worried either by the possibilities of lawsuits or out of ineptitude and laziness, refused to help the public in what he considered an essential way.”

Institutional employees that refuse to give their fair, honest, and diligent opinion on privately owned artworks, and even for those traditionally accepted pieces, betray integrity as well as the public trust. Whatever the reason for their negligence, their espoused mandate to protect rare art treasures regardless of where they are found has been compromised. If something is not fully appreciated, it will be less protected. If a work of art is not given credit for being authentic by those in authority, chances are, by virtue of unintended consequences it will eventually be lost or destroyed.

The misanthropic ideal that holds political viewpoints above the search for truth, and the procrastination induced by fear of litigation are sure signs of decay within any system. It cannot be denied that some of those heavily endowed institutions are doing important things in the way of education and availing treasures to a broader public, but some of what is going on would certainly be a shock to those ‘late’ private collectors who made it all possible in the first place with their munificent bequests. They would certainly be disenchanted by the inharmonious insult to collectors orchestrated by their misguided progeny, and from within the same museums that carry their names.

 The shift in trust toward institutional authority is largely misplaced according to author Huon Mallalieu. He wrote that “it is the private collector who often knows more than the seemingly grander and confident experts at museums and auction houses. The reason for this is obvious: he has to back his eye with his own money. The others may lay their reputations on the line, but not, in the short term, their livelihoods.”

Here, the person who comes to mind first is my former owner James Orrock. Not only did he take the necessary risks and buy as much art as he could afford, he did it fruitfully as a confident self-taught expert. The art critic for the Athenaeum wrote that “one is always glad, whatever the reason, when one provokes Mr. Orrock into epistolisation. He never takes pen in hand without advancing the cause of true art, the cause we all have at heart.”

How the early inquiries about me were handled by Christie’s was interesting. Apparently, after many miscommunications, Bob never did talk to the senior person in the department. This may have been a strategy, a way of distancing anyone with decision-making power from the issue. It was probably presumed that if need be, the whole matter could be more easily dropped or at least ignored. When the fingerprint evidence was introduced, the matter became serious again. This was likely for worries over potentially intrusive publicity. Fingerprint evidence found on a painting of such importance that had been rejected for so many years was sure to generate public and media interest.

Inherent in the art world are numerous prejudices which often express themselves in the form of snobbery, and particularly when dealing with valuable art. It is truly a hotbed of discrimination, and ironically, exclusivity is often viewed as a goal, a badge of honour per se. The fallout is that those artworks or people that are not tied to the inner workings become unwanted outsiders. With paintings selling for tens of millions of dollars the art world has become big business. Even powerfully rich and influential companies eagerly salivate on the doorstep of the twin auction firms. It is characteristic of big business to dislike people or situations that appear hard to handle, and within the art world (one of the world’s most fragile markets) such a truism is never more grounded.

Loosely structured on customary procedures the art world is understandably insular. That the system is ordered by precedence is hard to deny when in 1998 Watson wrote that of Sotheby’s, “Among its board of directors and its advisory board were eleven people with royal and other aristocratic titles.” The problem comes when the art of a particular artist such as Turner, Rembrandt, Manet, or Picasso, becomes so highly valued that snobbery, exclusivity, and market manipulation by way of cartels or monopolies cause insurmountable restrictions for outsiders. It is therefore understandable that intruders such as John Anderson or Bob are unable to find justice at the feet of those concomitant gatekeepers. No matter whom the gatekeepers are—estate lawyers, boards, commissions, trusts, institutes—invariably they end up spreading their tentacles to fashion alliances and allegiances into their own collective. For the uninitiated the consequence of interaction with such special interest groups certainly promises to garner the same result that  endured. Utter disregard!

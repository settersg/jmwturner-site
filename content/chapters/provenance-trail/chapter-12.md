**Chapter 12**

## The Jolly Art Sleuth

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

The Jolly Art Sleuth

Shortly after Bob contacted the previously mentioned scientist, Marilyn Laver, and the gathering of material from her 1982 CCI report on yours truly had begun, she sent him a small news clipping. It didn’t seem relevant. It had something to do with a Canadian fellow discovering JMW’s fingerprints on an oil painting. He had bought it from a picker who found it mixed together with a jumble of old and broken frames. The artist was unknown. Surprisingly, the soiled landscape which was later purchased by the Biró family produced the proverbial rags to riches story!

 It was not long after this  family started to restore it that they realized it was somehow special. According to the news article, they noticed fingerprints on the upper left edge of the stretcher. A remnant of an illegible label was found. They subjected it to intense scrutiny; the latent ink had bled through the obliterated piece of paper and the stain appeared to read: “J.M. William Turner.” With hopes of having an original JMW Turner their inquiry intensified. At the end of their efforts and ten years later their 1,000 dollar chance purchase returned nearly 200,000 dollars by way of a Phillips auction in .

As published in the Philip’s sales catalogue of , Early British and Victorian Paintings, the painting was first reported by David Hill in the Turner Society News #66, March 1994. The discovery attracted world-wide media interest. Coverage focused on fingerprints found in the paint at the top left of the foreground tree. This had been positively cross-matched with fingerprints on samples in the Turner Bequest at the Tate Gallery. As Bob reread the catalogue entry and the clipping I knew something was stating to percolate.

Clearly, the acceptance of the painting was based on fingerprint evidence, at which point other scientific and curatorial opinion was brought in for additional support. While the considerable publicity that followed changed the minds of several disbelievers, it was the print match that set everything in motion. Tate authorities had previously condemned it as being by “someone painting in the spirit of Turner.”

 Even the slightest hint of dissention can send a painting to its death. From the Tate there had been more than a hint! J.H. Duveen gave insight about how an authentic work can be “spoiled by enemies who by an untraceable word here and there spread the idea that it is not exactly right! That kind of malevolence is often to be found, no matter how august the art circle concerned.” Without the fingerprint, the Birós would have been out ranked and without hope.

It is known that Turner used his thumbprint in trees for a rapid effect

                    Vale of Avoca detail, greatly enlarged fingerprint

and that he would also “make the texture of a stone with a single pressure of his thumb.” “Turner had formed the habit of working the paint with his fingers: thumbprints are to be found on many early drawings. They are nearly always present somewhere in his finished watercolours. This technique was particularly effective for building up his trees; he blurred the more distant leaves by dabbing his finger in the damp paint, then he articulated the forward leaves with brushwork. This gave his trees volume in space, significant form, and the illusion of motion.

There had already been academic discussions about the possibilities of using fingerprint evidence to help identify an artist’s work. In 1948 Otto Kurz in his book called Fakes acknowledged that the “study of the imprint of the artist’s fingers on pictures has been much advertised as a means of identification, but nothing has ever come of it.”

The Birós ultimate success was due to their inventive genius and tenacity, they triumphed over almost insurmountable odds. The painting was apparently unsigned and had absolutely no traceable provenance, but as leading edge technology would prove—the little guy was right—the Birós made history.

The coincidence is uncanny that this painting was the same one referred to by the local art dealer from ; the one, who in the The Vancouver Sun had disparaged me prior to the Aldergrove auction in 1997. “I just despair,” he was quoted, “of people going to auctions and getting carried away by the event. It’s really a feeding frenzy at auctions, with everyone thinking they’re going to get a deal. I strongly advise people: ‘Buyer beware’… the dealer, who is a student of Turner’s work, quotes last year’s international sales records for authentic Turner paintings: ‘the highest price paid at auction in 1996 was 180,000 dollars for a large landscape with rainbow, a work that Turner actually signed and then recorded in his own journal.” The painting referred to, is the same painting found mixed in with a bunch of old frames in . In fact, it did not have provenance or a signature. This was Biró’s painting! And as for its reception by the Turner art establishment, Landscape with Rainbow had barely been dusted off.

The Biró family is indeed a gifted father and two-son team. I shouldn’t have made them sound like novices. They’ve juggled art world vagaries for two generations already. Leading up to their fingerprint line-of-attack, the Birós had developed formative techniques to investigate such minutiae. Paul, the younger son had previous experience in photography and computer enhancement.

Bob grumbled assertively as he made his daily journal entry, “Oftentimes the presence of a fingerprint is the only evidence drawing the executioner to the guillotine.” He then carried on with his entry: “A student of Turner’s work, David Hill, eventually gained sufficient confidence to give “stylistic” support for the painting. This was after Scotland Yard corroborated the fingerprint. Within the conservative art world it seems that new methods of art study are looked on with distrust lest they upset the status quo.”

I don’t want to get ahead of myself here, but there are important Turner experts that still refuse to fully accept Landscape with Rainbow. There are qualms within the curatorial community regarding any new research tool and/or major discovery. New information can contradict standard scholarship and potentially compromise reputations. And legal fears over changing the status of an artwork appear pandemic. A sale of this painting at a different time and place and supported by additional expertise would surely have brought a multiple of what the Biró’s realized—several million I’m sure.

When given the Biró news clipping by Marilyn Laver, Bob had no idea of its significance and that the Biró story would soon connect personally. At the time, Bob ignored the crumpled scrap and tucked it away at the back of his “Turner file.” Three years later, that same art-sleuth was to spend nearly a week in , nose to my pigments, spotlight beaming relentlessly.

What brought their name to Bob a second time was the Internet. The ethereal Landscape with Rainbow led him to their Art Restoration and Forensic Studies in Art website affectionately called Biró the Art Sleuth. Their laboratory had expertise in everything from restoration to forensic studies using fingerprint imaging technology. The FBI had even shown an interest in their novel fingerprint-imaging techniques.

Bob’s short e-mail inquiry evolved quickly into a commitment to hire Peter Paul Biró, the sleuth member of the Biró family. Art sleuth par excellence, a man of many talents! He had moved to  with his parents and older brother at the age of fourteen. They were refugees from   where his ‘papa’ had worked for many years as an art conservator at the Museum of Fine Arts.

In getting credentials for their own Turner the Birós dealt with their frustrations differently than Bob. They went right for the jugular, forcing their painting through auction, and likely in defiance of important curatorial opinion. Bob and Graham thought they could accomplish their own goal through art-historical study of JMW and his contemporaries. In other words, I was being treated like one of the family, another one of their “orphans” per se. Little did they realize that there were political problems that out flanked the simple task of dredging up the truth. There were scoundrels lurking beneath the bleachers. Untangling how they were connected to influential dealers, scholars, auction houses, galleries and museums was going to be more difficult than he realized. It soon became clear that status within the art community was based as much on longevity and power of wealth as it was upon altruism and erudite credentials.

Reading many tomes about fakes and forgeries opened Bob’s eyes somewhat but the puzzle pieces did not fit easily. Combined with collecting and reading a hundred-plus books on Turner, he became more than confident that there was no deception – I was the real thing! With such irreproachable provenance, and powerful scientific proof there should be no problem getting me accepted. In all my monumental grandeur I should have been considered the Turner find of the century. But rather than an explosion of excitement, the indifference became stifling for poor Bob—I was slowly becoming his nemesis.

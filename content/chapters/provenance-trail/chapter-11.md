**Chapter 11**

## Orrock Esq.

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

730 AS, the Property of James Orrock Esq.

“Hello, this is Jeremy Rex-Parkes, I am away from my desk at the moment, but if you would like to leave your name and number I will get back to you as soon as possible.” Just bad timing! Each time Bob phoned the archivist at Christie’s in  he was away. Because of the eight-hour time difference he was calling at  then again at  in the morning. He left his name and number once, but to no avail. Bob mumbled, “The repetitive nature of the job must have driven poor Jeremy to distraction.” Out of frustration Bob was about to give up. “One last try,” he grumbled.

“Oh yes, I’m sorry,” Jeremy apologized. “I was not able to return your call yesterday, the message was cut off before I got your number …” Bob interrupted – “Oh, that’s fine, I’m happy enough just to make contact.” The formal impersonal voice of Jeremy’s answering machine had become one of gentle conciliatory charm. Bob’s mental image was of a middle aged docent with a scholar’s demeanor; weathered, slight, and gray. It is amazing how a strange voice automatically conjures up an image, probably an image of someone better than real. Or maybe not.

Before phoning that morning, Bob had been reviewing his progress. Since the beginning of the rescue efforts (in deference to my noble provenance) he’d been aware of a stock number stenciled on my stretcher. He rightly assumed that during the struggle by scientists and the courts to find answers this was a stone already turned. It was an indication that I had sold at public auction, and because Mr. Dowding the defense lawyer, told him I had sold through Sotheby’s and it was a Sotheby’s representative that was called in as an expert witness, he assumed it was their number. The court case took place almost twenty years ago. It was understandable that much of the information Dowding gave him embodied only dim recollection. I remember Bob mentioning off hand that the number looked like Christie’s. He took the suspicion no further.

  Volumes had accumulated about me since the west-coast auction almost three years earlier and only a short list of challenges remained. One was linking the number on my stretcher with a Sotheby’s catalogue entry. As always, provenance is a key factor. Bob decided to track down information about the stencil number, while Graham was still trying to get the balance of elusive court documents in . From Sotheby’s, , Bob was assured by a young voice that it could very well be a Sotheby’s number from long ago. He was disappointed to learn that because of my age a manual search of old catalogues was required. To facilitate this, the girl requested a photograph and description of me. It was at this point that Bob decided to contact Christie’s just on the off-chance the number was theirs.

In the meantime, the task of tracking down the remaining court documents was beginning to look futile. Graham was told that criminal case documents can only be released to the parties who had been directly involved in proceedings. The only person who could help was Dowding, and he seemed too tired.

Again, as with Sotheby’s Christie’s needed time to search archives. One relief was that it could all be done online. Bob sent quality images of the sales number and label along with the following note, “We were more than happy when you confirmed this might be a Christie’s number. There has been information suggesting the painting came from the estate of one of J.M.W. Turner’s executors. The vintage label reads, Shipwreck, the Rescue J.M.W. Turner RA. The tattoo number is 73 OAS …”

The next morning, William, Bob’s pharmacist buddy from  woke him bright and early. “Happy Birthday,” he drawled. After an amiable chat, Bob checked his e-mail. Mr. Rex-Parkes had already replied. It had taken him less than a day.

Good Morning Mr. Setters; It was 73OAS and the sale was 4th June 1904, ‘The Highly Important Collection of Pictures and Watercolour Drawings,’ the property of James Orrock, owing to ill health and is ordered away from London, Lot 140, JMW Turner, RA, The Shipwreck   63 X 87 inches.

       Presented by the artist to his executor, Mr. Griffith.

       From the collection of Sir J. C. Robinson.

I hope the information is what you were looking for, and if you have other stock numbers, do not hesitate to contact me. Regards, jrp.

Stunning! Bob had to read these few words several times before they sunk in and the implications began to jell. “What a great birthday surprise,” he chuckled. Not only was this substantial provenance, it took my history directly back to JMW himself. Bob didn’t realize it but this exciting news would generate a research project in and of its self. Colin B. Bailey, at the time Chief Curator at the National Gallery of  had dropped in for a visit shortly after this revelation. He was touring and lecturing about a National Gallery exhibition he was promoting, Renoir’s Portraits: Impressions of an Age. He authored the accompanying catalogue. He and Bob discussed a number of the “orphans” around home, including me. Without prompting, and on his return to , Dr. Bailey sent the entire catalogue from the Christie’s 1904 Orrock . The same catalogue from whence Jeremy Rex-Parkes quoted.

     Who were all these people mentioned in the sale’s listing? Bob started his search. He was like a kid with a new toy. Several journal entries followed…

 …James Orrock R.I., R.O.I. (1829-1913) the Victorian/Edwardian gentleman from whose collection Shipwreck, the Rescue sold at Christie’s in 1904, was a renowned collector of both paintings and Oriental ceramics and was himself a widely exhibited artist

     James Orrock R.I., R.O.I., print after an oil painting by Orrock

In fact, some of what has come down to us about Turner’s secreted working methods has done so by virtue of Orrock quoting his own painting master, W.L. Leitch (who knew Turner personally).

Orrock had been twenty-one years old on Turner’s passing, and was only ten years younger than Turner’s champion—Ruskin. He knew Ruskin well. [Bob didn’t know yet but there was an oversize two-volume biography written about Orrock by Byron Webber: published in , Chatto and Windus 1903: James , Painter, Connoisseur, Collector.] Some of the artfully produced books Orrock had personally illustrated during his lifetime, in the style of Turner, included: Mary Queen of Scots; Old England - her story mirrored in her scenes, and In the Border Country. References to Orrock are found in several other publications about Turner including: The Studio; The Genius of J.M.W. Turner R.A, and Turner and Ruskin. As an artist, renowned collector, and foremost advocate of Turner and British art, Orrock must be considered a premier Turner connoisseur and expert of his time.

     …Orrock acquired Shipwreck, the Rescue from Sir John Charles Robinson R.E. (1824-1913) the landscape painter and etcher. From 1879 until 1901, Sir Charles held the venerable post of Surveyor of Her Majesty Queen Victoria’s Pictures, and was highly respected for his understanding and appreciation of art at an exemplary level. He served as the first Superintendent of Collections at the  (1852-1869), later to be called the  and .

When the rooms in  House occupied by Turner’s pictures and drawings were required for other purposes, the Government decided to build a series of temporary galleries in connection with the Museum at .  This 1858 establishment of what amounted to a makeshift Turner Gallery was in lieu of a proper facility as prescribed by Turner in his Bequest. It was under Robinson’s aegis that this extensive collection of Turner’s work was housed, protected, and displayed, while plans were still underway for a permanent and exclusive facility to house the trove—those hundreds of paintings and thousands of works on paper. At the midterm of his tenure Robinson would have been the conductor of this extensive project, and by association he would have been a leading connoisseur of Turner’s work.

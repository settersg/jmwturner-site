**Chapter 7**

## The Implacable Judgment

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

The Implacable Judgment

Visnei had skipped the country to parts unknown…

It was fortunate that Mr. Dowding had the foresight to save what trial information he did in the summer of 1982. It had been so long since the trial that all other copies were lost to the shredder! Another positive discovery that Graham made with the help of Professor Bruce McDougal from the UBC Faculty of Law was the file number and thus the judgment transcript from 1982.

In the County Court of  regarding Her Majesty The Queen Against Franz Visnei, His Honour gave his reasons for judgment (excerpt):

“Franz Visnei is charged with two counts of attempted fraud, one count involving a painting said to be by Turner and the other count involving a painting said to be by Picasso. I find Visnei guilty on both counts.

“The chronology of events giving rise to these charges has been outlined by a number of crown witnesses. During 1980, Dr. T. N. Crow had purchased a painting said to be by Gainsborough from one Siegfried Norton, a rather shadowy character who features in the evidence of a number of Crown witnesses.  He changed his name to Roger Norten and some time before the denouncement of the circumstances alleged by the Crown he made his departure from  to take up residence in . In December 1981, Norton [sic] was in touch with Dr. Crow offering to sell another painting, this one by Turner. Then in February 1982, Dr. Crow received a telephone call from Visnei who introduced himself as a friend of Norten and who expressed an interest in purchasing the Gainsborough. Again, in April 1981[2], Visnei telephoned Dr. Crow this time offering to sell a painting by Turner. By now, however, Dr. Crow had become suspicious and called the R.C.M.P. As a result, it was arranged that Corporal Peter Montague of the commercial crime division should pose as a wealthy friend of Dr. Crow who was seeking a type of investment through which he could hide his earnings of several millions of dollars from the income tax authorities…”

        “The various references made by Visnei to the Turner may be summarized in very short form as follows: that he purchased the Turner from Sotheby’s in London in 1979 for £475,000 (about $2,500,000 in Canadian funds); that he was prepared to sell for $2 million as he was in need of funds to clear the title to property in Langley he had recently purchased from Roger Norten. And as to the Picasso, Visnei stated that it was owned by Baroness Jane von Einem of a very old German family who was prepared to sell the Picasso at a very reasonable price, as she was moving. There was, in addition, a very curious telephone call made by Visnei to Mrs. von Einem from which it appeared that she was prepared to accept $150,000 for the Picasso.

        “It is on these representations that the Crown bases its case. Simply put, the Crown says that these representations were false; that Visnei knew they were false; that he deliberately made these representations to create a false impression as to the value of the two paintings; and that he did so in an attempt to dishonestly deprive Corporal Montague.

        “In support of its allegations, the Crown tendered a formidable body of evidence from persons who had dealings with the accused during the period alleged in the indictment…”

        “Louis Peter Nuber, an art dealer of considerable international experience and a resident of Vancouver since 1971, told how he had been introduced to Visnei in the fall of 1981 by Norten as a man who was said to own or have access to sources of art worth some $50 million. Visnei showed Nuber photographs of a number of paintings including the Picasso mentioned in the indictment. As a result of this introduction and several subsequent meetings with Visnei, Nuber was persuaded to go to  in the hope that he might be able to purchase some of the works of art, which Visnei was offering for sale. In November 1981, Nuber and his partner made a fruitless trip to  virtually chasing Visnei and searching him out in , , , ,  and . Back in , Nuber accepted an invitation from Norten to view the Turner. Here he met Visnei again who stated he had purchased the Turner and that it was worth $2 or $3 million. In view of his European experience, however, Nuber was not interested in the Turner.

       “Jane von Einem a writer and entrepreneur, living in  and having business interests in  was called as a witness. She stated, incidentally, that she had married Baron von Einem but did not consider herself a baroness and did not use the title. More significantly, she stated that she had been introduced to Visnei in the fall of 1981 by Roger Norten. Initially her discussions with Visnei were in connection with a process for the liquefaction of coal developed by an Italian scientist who Visnei claimed to represent…”

    “Several months later Visnei arrived in her office with the Picasso and asked to leave it there, as he had to leave the city. She agreed. And then, several weeks later Visnei sent two more paintings to be left in her office. In due course, Visnei returned and in later discussions he asked if she knew anyone who could give him a loan on the security of the Picasso so he could invest in her mining business. She referred him to Robert Kemeny. Then one Saturday morning, several weeks later, Visnei came to her office and picked up the several paintings and she was surprised that Visnei had telephoned her later in the day asking about the price of the Picasso when he always had said it was worth $200,000. On the following Monday, he told Mrs. Von Einem that he had been in touch with a millionaire who had bought the Turner for $2 million and that he had to go out to make a “statement” in connection with the sale. Mrs. Von Einem further testified that she never felt that Picasso was hers to deal with.

        “Robert Kemeny also described the meeting with Visnei at Norten’s farm when Visnei showed him the Turner he was offering for sale. Visnei also produced a number of papers and a photocopy of a portion of a Sotheby’s catalogue describing the painting. Visnei did not indicate who the owner was, or the price expected, but he did say that a painting by Turner would be worth between $1 and $2 million. Visnei talked to Kemeny some four or five times up until April asking him to make an offer on the Turner but Kemeny declined. Kemeny also said that after the trip to Norten’s farm he saw the Picasso in Mrs. von Einem’s office and when talking with Visnei he said he would expect an offer of about $500,000 for the Picasso.

     “The last witness called by the Crown was Herbert Mostyn Richard Jones, for the last eight years the security manager for Sotheby Parke Bernet & Co., , and before that an officer of the  Metropolitan Police. In this position he testified that he had access to, and was familiar with the records kept by Sotheby’s in the regular course of their business. Indeed, he brought a prodigious number of these records to court. He explained in detail the procedure of Sotheby’s from the receipt of goods through to sale and shipment to the purchaser… the Turner was sold to Dr. Horst Rudloff of … The records further indicate that the painting was shipped to Dr. Rudloff on …

        “Visnei gave evidence on his own behalf. The principle matters he dealt with may be summarized as follows:

From the very beginning of his dealings with Montague, Visnei said that he requested an interpreter be present as his command of the English language was not adequate. However, he says this request was denied by Montague who did not wish any additional persons to know of his attempt to hide his “black” money from the tax authorities. The tape recordings of his conversations with Crow and Montague may indicate that his English was far from perfect but it was certainly adequate for meaningful conversation…

It is Visnei’s evidence that the tapes have been altered, tampered with or edited or are incomplete and as a result do not accurately record his dealings with Montague and Crow. It was his evidence that the tapes had been deliberately tampered with to manufacture the lie that he stated that he had purchased the Turner for £475,000. Both Montague and Crow, however, stated that the tape accurately recorded the conversation…

(c) There is also a suggestion in Visnei’s defense that the search warrant held by the police when they seized the paintings in question and the other material from the Langley residence on Monday, May 4, 1982, was somehow deficient. Whether or not that may have been the case, however is of little consequence as the essence of the case against the accused is his deceitful intent.

Visnei stated that amongst the papers he had at his residence was a colour photograph of the Turner painting to which was attached an opinion of a German professor as to its value. These papers were not among those introduced into evidence by the prosecution and have never been found since. I gather that it is Visnei’s position that these papers, if produced, would be evidence of the value and authenticity of the Turner and they have been deliberately withheld by the police. Corporal Roberts, who took possession of all the articles seized, denied that any such papers were included…

(e) Visnei gives a curious account of how he came into possession of the Turner.

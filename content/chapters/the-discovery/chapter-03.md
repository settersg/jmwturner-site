**Chapter 3**

## Frosty's Nemesis

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

Frosty’s Nemesis

Bob my present owner started keeping a journal of his odd and not so wonderful art world experiences. He’s not the type one might expect to take on such a task. In fact, he’s a solitary sort of guy living in the middle of what seemed like nowhere when I first arrived. In any case here are a few of his notes about how he learned of me. One last thing! Don’t mind the bits of scribble in the margin, he fancies himself a seer.

Journal … It was late morning and time for a break from tending the gallery [art and antique]. I put my book down to pop outside for some fresh air. During the winter months’ business was slow and reading had become the biggest part of my day. An acquaintance that knew of my interest in old paintings wandered up and started telling of his “great find.” Although he was reservedly enthusiastic, it was obvious he was trying to find a buyer. He said it was a painting by Turner and that it was presently being used as a study piece for the art students at the  in . My first question was, "Which Turner?" When told it was John Turner, I returned the volley, "Which John Turner?"

My enthusiasm was at low ebb. When he articulated, J.M.W, my whole perspective changed. Joseph Mallord William Turner (1775-1851). Frosty’s J.M.W. pronouncement seemed unlikely. As this somewhat disheveled looking fellow continued, my imagination was trying to make a connection between his appearance and what might be the owner of a precious work of art. In all fairness, Frosty came by his look honestly. He had just returned from retrieving a new addition for his auto wrecking yard. Later I learned of his ability to maneuver a wreck onto the back of a flatbed truck with little more than brute physical force—his own.

Frosty gave a short description of the painting, “It’s eight feet long. A lifeboat full of people, beside a long pier, is just about to be swamped. There are people in the water and a ship sinking in the distance.” He then gave a scant explanation of where it came from; something to do with the law courts, but the details proved somewhat mysterious and I got the feeling that he wanted to keep it that way.

Depending on the true author, the price he wanted for the picture was either far too low or far too high. Frosty was asking a knight’s ransom for a pauper—100,000 dollars. It is an uphill struggle to gain support for a moderately important painting that has inadequate documentation, and as for a major piece by one of the world’s greatest masters, forget it. At eight feet wide, it was not a picture that could be lost in an attic for a century only to be found during a search for Halloween costumes.

Whatever its history, it had to be an unusual one, and although Frosty told me that he had commissioned a professional appraisal a few years earlier, which claimed it was worth three times his asking price, he still could not tell me anything of the painting’s origin. If painted by J.M.W. Turner, why was this seemingly dispossessed painting, this “orphan”, not catalogued as one of the artist’s accepted works? Was it the object of professional condemnation or even more problematic— fake, or stolen? This worry faded as Frosty wandered off. The painting was probably bogus anyway, and in my mind the matter was closed. Back to more mundane concerns… End of Journal entry…

By the bye, I should formally introduce you to my new caretaker. His name is Robert but most people call him Bob. Why he’s been mucking around with pictures that are out of his league is beyond me.

He’s intercepted a number of fallen orphans (as he names us) on our way to purgatory (as I call it). As a result of an opinion given in haste, inaccurate interpretation by a trusted potentate or for other reasons—an owner can understandably lose faith in his or her painting. Peter Watson in his book, Manet to Manhattan confirms that great paintings are always sneaking through salerooms masquerading as something else. For many dealers this is part of the excitement of the art trade.

By highlighting one particular case of mistaken identity, Watson disparages the highest echelon at Christie’s… “The Veronese-El Greco, however, was more than just another lost Old Master,” he wrote. “The fact that it had been misattributed by the chairman of the company was symptomatic of the low point to which Christie’s had sunk…”  I didn’t actually read this. I overheard Bob talking about it. But from what I’ve seen and heard in the past, more insidious reasons beyond incompetence and apathy for making a bad assessment of a painting can include: bias, self-interest, jealousy, legal fears, corruption, or just plain greed.  Some of these I’ve witnessed firsthand. What about the “quick draw” art evaluation, the one that most non-confidants receive when searching for a knowledgeable opinion? If one sends a

photograph of the object, and a photo is the only thing available for the expert to interpret, his or her task is nay on impossible. An improper interpretation might be as much a product of inadequate information as anything else. Even with the item in hand the challenge often goes beyond the time available for a proper appraisal. Bob recently sent a professional large-format black and white photograph, plus a colour transparency of a grey colour-wash drawing to the Wildenstein Institute in Paris. He assumed from the images that it would be easily recognized as an aqueous-based medium. Hah! It was not until the picture was sent to Guy Wildenstein in  for final assessment that they realized it was not an oil painting at all. From the photo they thought it was grisaille oil on paper. This is the “long lost painting” or prototype of the catalogued print. The exact rendition of the print attests to this conclusion. It would be impossible to do a colour wash drawing (watercolour), precisely, using a print to copy from… but it is quite possible to do an etching or engraving copying a colour wash drawing. Walter Bayes, the professional watercolorist cum Turner biographer: “With water colour you never quite foresee the result, it is a matter of accident… it snaps a decision long before most painters have really done what they meant.”

                             François Boucher “Le moineau apprivoisé”

                                           engraved by René Gaillard

The shortage of time, and the trouble involved in seriously analyzing a ‘piece’ discourages many experts from giving a proper analysis in the first place. It is also good to remember that in many cases an expert’s rank or post might substantially exceed his or her abilities. I once heard Bob blurt out in frustration. It was a grandiose statement just after he’d received a letter back from the Tate Gallery. “Connoisseurship cannot be granted by appointment, taken by political influence, or usurped by the power of wealth. Connoisseurship must be earned.”

    The next time Bob heard from Frosty he had just returned from  with me. “Frosty’s eight-foot would-be Turner,” is how Bob phrased it.  Nonetheless Frosty persuaded him to visit his home where I was safely tucked away in a shipping crate. Frosty propped my reinforced plywood container against the side of his house and busily started undoing the large latches that held things together. At the same time, he was making intermittent sweeps with his foot in order to put the run on a persistent little dog. Fido was set on marking me as his own.

Soon the cover was down and plastic vapor-barrier drawn back. The moderating sun of an otherwise cold day lit up my surface with an alarming glare. It was almost painful for Bob as he squinted with a grimace. I felt great! He adjusted my position trying to make out details. Here, I’ll let Bob tell it in his own words. The way he wrote it in his journal…

 …“It is a dark picture of what looks like survivors in a rescue boat being tossed around by violent waves—all crammed together were sailors, mothers and children; some huddled, others braced against the deadly elements. This first impression was not great. The darkness of the pigments, lack of detail, tremulous subject matter—and the glare! With poor coordination and a couple of expletives, we got the painting turned stretcher-side up, while much to his wife’s dismay, Frosty finally made contact with the busy little dog. She removed this leaking, and now yelping distraction to the safety of her kitchen while Frosty and I continued our assault on the picture.

I found the back was more interesting than the front; this is a terrible thing to admit, and falls into the same disrespectful realm as discussing the quality of a frame while neglecting the painting. I was only somewhat familiar with Turner’s more popular works, and the painting now before me did not have a characteristic Turner feel. On the stretcher there were a few old auction numbers, a label with an inscription, a tag with a number, and a confusion of other marks. These at least indicated that others had previously taken the work seriously.

I was not yet proselytized and ready to sing its praise but was certainly more interested after seeing it in the flesh as it were. The old inscription quilled on the vintage label read: "Shipwreck, the Rescue, J.M.W. Turner R.A.," and the faded ink stencil mark, 73 OAS was typical of that used by a leading British auction firm. The natural shrinkage in the wood of the stretcher frame had caused the label to bulge in places. This, along with its patina led me to believe that the label was as old as the nineteenth century stretcher.

                             Shipwreck, the Rescue, label on stretcher

**Chapter 4**

## Other End of the Paintbrush

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

Other End of the Paintbrush

Bob’s Journal… Looking back previous to the Aldergrove auction sale, a  newspaper had written much about Shipwreck, the Rescue, and most of it was controversial. One of the articles quoted a well-known art dealer from Vancouver who disparaged the work on grounds that an important painting such as this was unlikely to be discovered locally. “I just despair” he said, “of people going to auction, with everyone thinking they are going to get a deal. I strongly advise people, ‘Buyer beware.’  I'm going out to see the painting myself tomorrow, but I can tell you that there is about as much chance of it being a genuine Turner as there is of it being a Leonardo da Vinci.”

Skeptical indeed! To take the liberty and paraphrase this prominent dealer: why would a rare painting be here, in this part of the world and unclaimed? The answer is simple: artwork can act as a tangible asset for those fleeing political strife, or those just wanting to move wealth to different parts of the world. Calamity in and of itself can dispossess just about anything. In addition, information about the history of artwork is often lost or distorted over generations of single-family ownership, and estate sales frequently catalogue such items poorly when they eventually come onto the market. It is not difficult to understand why an important lost painting would end up in a cosmopolitan centre like .

Even before the turn of the century, many of 's finest art treasures made their way across the . They had been delivered to this ‘unruly’ land by the most famous art dealers that ever lived—dealers who were reacting to huge fortunes being amassed by the industrial moguls of the day. These billionaires (by present standards) wanted to gather some of the old world culture under their wing and they made purchases from connoisseurs such as Duveen, Wildenstein, Seligmann, Gimple, Knoedler, and Durand-Ruel. An enormous number of European cultural treasures started moving west across the  by the third quarter of the nineteenth century. Frank Herrmann commented on how effectively “the antique exporters from  in particular, and  in general, had done their job in the era between 1870 and 1930, when  had become wealthy and  had become poor. Only the best had been good enough.” End journal…

     The morning after I had been securely deposited in dark solitude (which turned out to be Bob’s garage), he was back in the gallery, reading another biography about my creator, J.M.W. Turner R.A. Much of what I tell you now I learned later… As he sat trying to identify with the fury of Turner’s Snow Storm, he was distracted twice more by the news media. Many of their questions echoed those of the previous day, "What are you planning to do next and how will you determine if it is truly by the famous Turner?" They wanted something newsworthy so he played along as best he could.

He parroted his previous remarks from the evening before. About the importance of determining scientifically if the painting came from Turner's time period, etcetera, etcetera, blab, blab. His truer plan for the immediate future was to become completely familiar with Turner’s work. Then he could confidently go ahead with those same expensive tests he was promising the media.

To complete the necessary scientific tests a few problems had to be resolved anyway. The most frustrating was the lack of facilities available that could do the job. At the time it was Bob’s understanding that the only place in  capable of accomplishing the full spectrum of tests, The Canadian Conservation Institute (CCI), limited itself mainly to institutional work. The cost of roughly $20,000 for a comprehensive analysis seemed like a foolish expenditure unless he was absolutely confident about the painting’s authorship.

Page after page, Bob lived Turner’s life vicariously—what a terribly committed artist! “He feared that social entanglements would divert him from the business of painting.” His level of commitment as an artist was too great to allow for marriage; and even though he had a couple of serious relationships, little is known of these romances. I could add plenty, but it might not be proper to discuss here.  All I can say is that I lived under the sly old wizard’s roof for half a century and plenty of torrid stuff went on within ear shot. He was a most proper gent but JMW liked the ladies. What intrigue might I have witnessed if it weren’t for being stacked behind so many early starts and unsold canvases? I’m sure you already know about the disheveled state of his studio. And the cats, my god! It’s not much fun being close to the floor around a bunch of toms. Well, I shouldn’t bore you with complaints.

JMW’s tours at home, in , and abroad were frequent and extensive.  I remember him being gone for weeks and sometimes even months at a stretch. Unfortunately, few itineraries survive. The best records are his sketches en route and the limited number of his letters that are still around. Turner’s first two biographers, Thornbury and Hamerton both confirm the dearth of Turner correspondence. On the basis of rarity alone, Thornbury assigned his letters great value. Hamerton, along with many subsequent biographers, mocked Turner’s literacy: “Like most uneducated men, he disliked letter-writing, and he carried this dislike to a degree involving positive discourtesy to others.”

 How could Hamerton so confidently declare Turner discourteous? A bit harsh and arrogant wouldn’t you say? There might have been the odd recorded faux pas made by the preoccupied and possibly forgetful artist, but many letters are known to have been lost or destroyed. Rev. Trimmer, Turner’s close friend and one with whom he often corresponded, unwarily incinerated many. There was also Turner’s eccentric attitude toward the multitude of formal invitations he received. Letters remained unopened on Turner’s table for months. “They only want my autograph,” he used to say.

Turner’s father was pleased with the “edycation” he was able to afford for his son in an age when few but the elite were formally educated. Turner certainly had a comfortable measure of humanity and his attendant modesty and introspection is well illustrated in an 1845 letter to Charles Robert Cockerell. He begs pardon from Cockerell’s wife: “I beg her pardon for being so disorderly and deserve expulsion.”

Much of Turner’s graphic note-taking from his travels stayed in his gallery on Queen Anne Street along with the rest of us pictures. Eventually most of us “deadwood” made it to the Tate. The Turner Bequest they call it, hah. I’m sure glad I was eventually sold off. At least I had a bit of a life. Nonetheless it’s interesting to hear what those little colour-notations are now bringing in the marketplace. Many of them were only a few strokes of watercolour over pencil scribbles. The price has sky rocketed beyond reason. In 2002, Sotheby’s gave an estimate on a wistful and simple one, barely a picture, but 150,000-200,000 pounds made it a very expensive stain indeed. And as for those finished watercolours by Turner that never made it to the Tate; they have changed hands for as much as 2.04 million pounds (2001)—not a sum that would facilitate forming a scrapbook full.

They say that Turner was a good money manager and became wealthy in his own lifetime. His wealth did come largely from picture sales. Funny thing is, at the end of it all when the old wizard passed away most of his great artworks (including me) were not included in his Bequest to the Nation. They’d been sold throughout his long active career.

 It’s the Clore Gallery that holds the Turner Bequest as part of the Tate Gallery holdings. The Tate officials understandably want their institution to be seen as the greatest repository of Turner treasure. In the process they’ve managed to dominate the world of Turner, the staff and devotees almost exclusively referee any potential new discovery. According to Bob, herein lays a conflict of interest and the first thread in the complex veil of deception inherent in the art authentication process.

     Turner was a solitary worker, and while he was creating large masterpieces, he never enjoyed the labour of student helpers, as did many of his colleagues. This accounts for the uniqueness of his work, and removes him from the common argument that haunts many other artists—whether a picture was entirely by the artist’s hand or done as a collaboration with students. Those who would like to limit the oeuvre of certain artists would argue that a questionable work was done entirely by a student or follower. When an artist has a stable of underlings it can become difficult to differentiate the work of a talented student from that of his or her master. Thus the influential skeptic for whatever furtive reason can exclude and condemn such a work offhand.

The solitary and secretive Turner has spared the Turner-scholar this particular challenge. However, there is one other creative ploy available for those with the desire to mask the truth of new Turner art discoveries—the invention of hitherto unknown artists. This practice has had a long history that goes back at least to Bernard Berenson and The Italian Painters of the Renaissance. Berenson’s creations were not meant to muddle the truth though, but only to establish repositories for stylistic similarities. The artists he was “creating” were from a time that few art-biographical records were kept, and therefore, out of necessity much of his classifying included invention. We will soon be introduced to a raft of mystery-artists invented in order to double for Turner.

     Exceptions to Turner’s secretive working method came later in his career, on what were called varnishing days. These days came just prior to the  exhibition and were meant to allow time for final touchups. Turner, who had been somewhat of a recluse early on, became prone to violent displays of virtuosity, heaping on copious amounts of pigment to add light and texture. To understand this sudden, theatrical demonstration by one who had been so zealously protective of his methods and procedures, it should be remembered that in 1835, “Turner was emerging from a period in the early years of the decade marked by signs of uncertainty in his art…His performance at the British Institution was not an act cooked up to dazzle his peers, but the revelation of a technique developed over the years in the privacy of his painting room and presented as the confident affirmation of his prodigious gifts.”

This display astounded onlookers and generated much banter amongst his colleagues. It follows that, of the huge store of oil sketches he kept in his studio, the ones that made it to exhibition must have ended up more highly finished.

 With passage of time and changes in attitude over what constitutes art, much of the sketchy work that was left in Turner’s Bequest to the Nation and other pieces which were once considered mere beginnings have come to be accepted as works of great art. There have been important exhibitions of such work, firstly at the Louvre, and later with a traveling exhibition organized by the Tate Gallery called, From Turner’s Studio. It is likely that Turner purposely left certain works at various stages of completion. Was it that Turner created many of these loose and spontaneous works to document his inventiveness, and possibly even to assist later followers and art students to understand art techniques? He did make provision in his will for such sketches to be exhibited regularly. Already by 1939 A.J. Finberg wrote that “a very decided preference was being shown for his unfinished over his completed works.”

My loose brushwork and unfinished nature initially sullied Bob’s opinion of me, and it was not until he was able to view me from a distance and under proper lighting that my image came together. He believes and rightly so, that regardless of my aesthetic appeal, my seeming lack of finish, in and of itself directs an attribution towards Turner. “It is unlikely that anyone but Turner would have left such an expensive undertaking on such a grand scale summarily finished.” Yes… his unique habit of leaving similar epic undertakings incomplete has been universally accepted—and appreciated, I might add.

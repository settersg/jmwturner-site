**Chapter 23**

## Fury and the Neglected Lover

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

A.m. Aug 21— Fury and the Neglected Lover

The complaints regarding frivolous and untruthful expert opinions are all too justified. They have caused a reaction, so that timorous minds nowadays go to the extremes in judging negatively or with reserve. The people concerned say ‘no’ in order not, at all events, to be considered with the ‘yes-men.’ Now prudence is not only the mother of wisdom but also the daughter of ignorance. What must be done to steer the right course between the rocks of conciliator compliance on the one hand and a negative attitude, on principle, on the other?

   - Max J. Friedlander, On Art and Connoisseurship, 1942

According to the Butlins, when the Greeks sent a delegation to England to make inquiries about the Elgin Marbles, they were stonewalled, stewed in bureaucracy, and ultimately “they went away.” As Bob was to find out later, this ploy had been used on Paul Biró. Frances assured, “He just goes away, goes away.” Whether it was simple procrastination, a highbrow desire to sweep it under the rug or a quasi-conspiracy, one can only speculate—but Bob got the impression that some of their cast made a winning science of it.

The fallout had settled from the previous day’s critique, and most everyone had forgotten what was debated—everyone but Graham. While the rest were wine tasting, he stayed home with me. He fumbled through several tomes looking to refute Martin’s strongest stylistic argument against me: the argument dealing with my varying degrees of ‘finish’. For example, Martin claimed that my pier was more finished than the wrecked ship.

From his own B & J Catalogue, both visually and in the text, Graham found the exact opposite of Martin’s assertion. As the group stood in the living room, barely awake, Graham flipped from bookmark to bookmark to illustrate his discovery. A.J. Finberg left no doubt when he wrote that “Turner’s style differed in two opposite directions from the one then in vogue; it differed not only by deliberate vagueness or looseness of definition in appropriate places, but also, in other places, by an excessive vigour of realization.”

With feigned humility and mumbled concurrence Martin was able to brush off his previous verity as inconsequential by laughing, “So you’ve proven me wrong.” He then swiftly changed the topic by directing everyone to my backside (verso if you please). At Martin’s urging blithe connoisseurs had become prudent scientists in a flash.

     Graham had won a critical stylistic argument, but disappointedly at this same juncture realized that he was up against something other than scholarship. He was starting to believe, as Bob had suspected for years, that there were other issues at stake in the art world beyond the truth. Was the control by experts predominantly grounded in the “looking, looking, looking, looking” principal as Martin insisted? It certainly left the door open for discretionary decision-making. Certainly, the necessary skills for making the best decision could be more faithfully gained by taking everything into account, including science and scholarship.

Martin must have had himself convinced that little else had “weight”, that connoisseurship was the sole path to expertise. And what about his adherence to the more love-struck approach that required almost a romantic devotion to the subject painting before one could make a commitment? The ideal of  Gowing, Martin’s admired predecessor at the Tate had implored, “When a letter from your girlfriend is dropped through the door, you don’t have to open it and read the name to know who it’s from.” Friedlander concisely assailed this theory when he wrote that “coldly analytical scholars make fewer mistakes.”

By exclusively using Gowing’s subjective approach for authenticating paintings (the “accidental impression” as Friedlander would call it); the door was left ajar for biased decision-making. Misattributions made on a subjective basis—although not always fraudulent—can result in skewed historical perspective—the history upon which an artist’s reputation including Turner’s depends. Naturally, the person positioned as the established expert on a particular artist would like to guard against challengers from beyond the reach of their own influence. What better way to do this than to restrict the basis for their authority to the mystical and difficult to challenge power of connoisseurship?

Friedlander wrote, “Since nobody can be called to account or produce proofs, since everything depends upon confidence and blind faith, it is authority which is demanded, claimed and striven for—at times even created artificially.” He added that, based on more strident activity in the marketplace, there is an increased demand for expertise, which contributes to the increased power of the expert, and to the danger of misusing this power.

Isolationist efforts with regard to ‘British Historic works of art’ apparently began in the 1960s with the creation of a keepership by the same name. At that time,  Gowing was likely the main push behind taking control of this politically sensitive part of the Tate collection. Gowing had certainly been at the centre of ‘establishment’ politics—he had been Trustee of both the National Portrait Gallery and Tate Gallery for several years. In such an environment it would be desirable to pass the reins of authority along within one’s own circle of influence, thus keeping the decision-making authority in friendly hands.

As I mentioned the group had been drawn away from stylistic arguments to my verso, but as the physical evidence started to mount in support of Turner’s involvement, Martin shifted focus to his world of William Blake—almost as if Dr. Jekyll had become Mister Hyde everyone was treated to another of Martin’s charming anecdotes. Undeterred, Graham brought them back to Turner with a quote from Daphne Lawson. As previously mentioned, this professor from the  (teaching with the ISC program at Herstmonceux) wrote a lengthy supplement to his Turner paper. Dating me based on the progression of Turner’s lozenge shaped composition, her conclusion was 1802-04. Martin immediately searched for an alternative argument, “I’m afraid it’s more than the lozenge, it’s the waves.” It appeared that my waves were Martin’s only other argument at the time, and somewhat frustrated, he sniped a quote from his master again, “You come into the room as Lawrence Gowing says, and recognize it as your lover or you don’t – and I’m afraid, I don’t!”

Graham sent Bob an interesting quote from his Anthropology of Art course subsequent to Martin’s visit. It destroyed the foundation for such affection:

    The word 'connoisseurship' makes some people uncomfortable; it has gained connotations of mystery and elitism.  The mystery aspect associated with connoisseurship is usually overdone.  It is often relatively easy for a connoisseur to demonstrate the knowledge by producing different samples for the novice or student to compare and test.  Connoisseurship is knowledge that is hard to describe, but the connoisseur who wants to pass on her or his knowledge can do so by pointing out differences in examples and providing a variety of samples.

    The charge of elitism that connoisseurship receives occurs when a craftsperson or connoisseur claims the right, by virtue of his or her experience, to be trusted to pronounce on matters that other people say are a matter of taste or opinion.

Friedlander audaciously began his chapter on connoisseurship. “CHARLETANISM,” he wrote, “was the professional malady of experts and springs from the unstable nature of artistic judgment.”

The stylistic debate Graham and Bob had with Martin made them wonder if they were all talking about the same picture. When they suggested that an area of my waves was possibly added later at the time the picture was refurbished for , and at a time Turner’s brushwork was notoriously broad, Martin protested, “They are feeble little dabs!” And when Bob pleaded that some were sweeps extending nearly a foot long, he seemed about to boil so Bob changed the topic to James Orrock. The fire was not out though. When Bob asked if Orrock was a connoisseur, he blurted out, “He just didn’t know…he was a collector, and he was probably cheated by Agnew’s.” Immediately stopping himself, “Oh, shee!” as if drawing back from a precipice, he then gave a short half-hearted laugh.

 As for Orrock being cheated: with the force of his artistic and collecting prowess, he likely advised Agnew’s, Christie’s, and that elite element of the British art trade, especially on works by Turner. He was certainly not in a position to be cheated. At the Christie’s “Highly Important” sale of his pictures in 1904 there were eighteen works by Turner alone. The Duveen family of art dealers and experts, at the time greatly respected Orrock’s connoisseurship, and as an old man his friendship and mentorship to James Henry Duveen seemed almost family-like.

The connection between Orrock and this charmed group was indubitably close, possibly too close. To appreciate this rather stealthy alliance, all one has to do is follow the trail of the record breaking Walton Bridges back and forth between Orrock and Agnew’s. This was in the same year that A.J. Finberg singled it out for high praise in his classic 1910 publication: “Mr. Orrock’s Walton Bridges…in all its essential beauty…”

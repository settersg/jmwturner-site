**Chapter 31**

## Farewell or Freedom

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

Farewell or Freedom

Besides duty, the other operative word for Martin was Keeper, “a really old fashioned term” that he seemed to cherish beyond reason, a word that broadly speaking seemed to denote somebody proudly responsible for maintaining the best interests of Nation and Crown. If this is true, then Martin had supremely fulfilled his obligation. If not for his tenacious diligence and iron grip on his tiny part of the  or rather the Turner kingdom of artworks, things would have been much different. Well, at least there would be a lot more Turners around to compete with Tate Gallery primacy.

It is also certain that his calling was based more on a cultural orientation than the law of some great conspiratorial society. But regardless of the will or fancy of it all, the repercussion of such an insular system is likely to be without the proper checks and balances to preserve Turner artworks for their own sake. Martin had been a dedicated soldier for the nation for forty-five years, and likely, without fully realizing the source of his motivation or ultimate goal. Frances had previously volunteered with confidence, “We are very middle class.” And when it was suggested to Martin that he might one day become Sir Martin,  again volunteered that he had “done nothing for it.” Surely untrue, but likely said in the kindest sense,  was possibly just egging him on.

Martin shared a little more Tate scuttlebutt about—as he put it—the somewhat “impatient and short-tempered” Andrew Wilton. According to Martin,  had been frustrated recently by being passed over for an administrative position, and was relegated to the library stacks as a researcher of sorts. If Martin was correct about the distain that those in his circle felt for such activity, this was true banishment. Martin consoled him with stories about his own grand adventures to faraway places. One might presume that one day Andrew Wilton will be on the road fulfilling his own duty as Martin had been over the years, to be treated as a celebrity abroad.

Martin: “He’s been sort of side-tracked from being head of a department into being told, ‘You get on with scholarship and leave the administration to somebody else.’ So he’s a bit bitter about that, but it makes him rather a bit more interested in my next trip, to Australia or some far flung place, where one day he’ll also be treated as a celebrity rather than sitting at his desk dealing with the mail.”

Martin admitted being impatient at times, and particularly with certain colleagues from provincial museums. He had his own coy way of avoiding conversations with such people. He would get his poor “young innocent secretary” to lie for him and say that he was out of his office. Much to Martin’s sense of fun, she would coo with trepidation, “Martin, you’ve taught me to lie.”

Martin highlighted the inner workings and insular ideal of English museums, wherein private collectors were purposely excluded. He explained how the top auction houses willingly forsook their lifeblood—the general public. They circumvented fair-trade practices as would be expected from any other firm, by selling privately to museums pictures destined for sale to the public. Select museums such as the Tate apparently have free run to go in and pick and choose long before pieces are ever catalogued. In addition, the museums are able to offer tax incentives to the owners through government for such activities. The deal is called a “private treaty sale” whereby the owner is effectively tempted with tax advantages in order to capitulate. Likely, it should be assumed that those at the top of the museum community bully the auction firms into such practice. These museum officials are often the ones that authenticate or condemn pictures sold by the same auction houses.

Martin: We were always being criticized because we had a bad representation of Scottish pictures [at the Tate], and particularly of Allan Ramsay [1713-84], a very good sensitive portrait painter. This was mainly because whenever anything came up, the Scots always bought it. They must have hundreds stuffed away in the cellars.

Through one of my colleagues having seen one in the offices of Sotheby’s… [Before it came up for sale] we managed to do what we call a “private treaty sale.” Which means that the owner gets a tax advantage and we get it cheaper, and the only person that loses is the…

Again Martin seemed momentarily lost for words, he knew someone was the loser but was unable to articulate the full dimension of such activity. He carried on describing their coup d'état concluding with, “It never was publicly on sale because we got it before it came up for auction. It’s much easier to buy a picture with tax advantage than go to the auction where you get no tax advantage and you’re up against the world!”

It is probably going too far to say that the auction houses are bullied into such nefarious activity. It may have all begun in 1781, when King George III was passing a Christie’s sale near Marlow on the . James Christie hastened to set out some curious ivory chairs for His Majesty’s inspection. The furniture caught the King’s fancy. “Christie’s promptly withdrew the lots from the auction and sold them directly in a royal private-treaty sale. They are in the Royal Collection to this day.” Clearly this was the top of the slippery slope.

The evening ended on an amusing note. Martin’s job had obviously been very rewarding for a person who considered travel a hobby. So much so that he gauged the number of trips abroad by the size of his loose change bag. This great loot bag came out every time Martin was encouraged to go on an excursion. After dumping it on the floor he would go in search of the appropriate coinage to match the country of destination.

Events of the next couple of days in  were the dressing on the weeklong adventure, thanks in large part to Dr. Gibson’s and my daughter Sarah’s help in planning activities. There was very little discussion about art. What time was not filled by pre-planned activities (whale watching, high tea at , the Royal BC Museum, antiquing) was filled with food, drink, and fun repartee.

                     Graham, Martin, Sarah, and sea-lions at Race Rock

The whale watching was planned for  and everything seemed all set until word came down that the boat was having engine problems. The initial concern was that everyone would have to suit up in waterproof gear and scramble into a high-powered rubber dinghy as an alternative. The group was convinced that, what appeared a bizarre way to track down the whales, might in-and-of-itself be exciting. Alas, the engine was repaired and before long, the captain, naturalist, and twenty or so family-styled adventurers boarded the luxury theatre—a vessel that lorded over the dinghies.

The captain/engine-repairman had done his job well. The powerful engine growled and thrust the smiling faces towards open water—destination—beyond Race Rock. This was where the pod of killer whales was apparently hidden. Frances and the captain were front and centre as the biting sea breeze flailed their hair and garments.

                               Frances Butlin and captain/engine repairman

The captain had no time to change back into his uniform after his greasy ordeal with the engine; however, he made a fine comrade for the majestic Frances as they both perched side-by-side atop the cabin. The only point at which the vital craft seemed diminished, a veritable Raft of the Medusa, was when the dinghies sped by in a mist at break-neck speed, engines roaring behind the passengers who were bound-in like soggy dolls. It was uncertain whether trading blind excitement for comfort and luxury had been the right choice—as it turned out, the dinghies got closer to the whales.

The pretty on-board naturalist gave as much information about the whales and their environs as could be expected within the few hours of the excursion. Her wide-eyed enthusiasm was contagious. At one point she literally had everyone eating out of her hand. After dragging a length of seaweed from the water, she explained that this was the same slippery foil on which the sea lions fed. She entreated that all partake of this gourmet delight. From the salty depths, to the slippery deck and into reluctant mouths, yum! There were friendly barks and groans from the distance as those same sea lions watched the people-circus from atop their slithery Race Rocks.

On the way back to the inner harbour, the trusted craft churned in the mist of the flatulent dinghies. While watching Martin and Frances on the upper deck one could see that they were in their element. Graham and Sarah had been dragged below deck for coffee and to avoid hypothermia. The two limeys held fast, they had apparently found their bliss.  reminded Bob of what his English grandmother expressed about his otherwise refined mother: “She’s made of toough stooff!”

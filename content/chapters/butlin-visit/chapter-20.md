**Chapter 20**

## The Arrival

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

The Butlin Visit

Assumptions made previously by Bob and Graham were based on events that occurred historically. Now it was time to meet one of the most prominent art world insiders in person. Was the clandestine nature of art world activity at the uppermost level about to be unveiled? To avoid forgetting any of the points that Martin might offer about me and the other paintings, Graham decided to wear a recording device—a wire.  Hah, this was starting to sound quite James Bond.

With the microphone pinned to his chest, and ever ready to garner another piece of the art world puzzle, Graham persevered the full week. The attention that Martin gave to their questions and the general chatter during excursions and at mealtimes was enlightening. Hour after hour, during simple socializing, or testy disagreements, Graham discreetly hovered in the wings. Martin Butlin, the accepted authority on historic British art and in particular on JMW Turner and William Blake had been authorized as a senior consultant for Christie’s to inspect me. But more importantly, he provided the vital information needed to confirm Bob and Graham’s hypotheses that the clandestine intentions and actions of those in positions of influence have the art world running scared.

 By the time Martin and his wife  arrived, their visit had evolved far beyond what was first intended; a trip of pure business had become a vacation with a purpose. The tourist adventure provided for them was ample, and the careful planning paid off. Everything went like clockwork. The distance from home, casual social environment, and the “straight-forward and simple people” impression Martin had about Canadians, disarmed him. His banter over the next several days wrote the concluding few chapters of a book that would have otherwise been only speculation.

 Bob assumed that one of his strategies during the visit would be to protect interests back home. As a Commander of the  (C.B.E.), and thus “keeper” of British best interests, his tenacity was admirable and unshakable. Churchill would have been proud. It also became apparent during the many hours of intense discussion over art that the position he had as Keeper at the Tate was as much a personal philosophy to be upheld, as it was a job to be done. Was it also a charge that Butlin carried with him over to Christie’s as senior consultant after he retired and his administrative responsibilities at the Tate were divided?

It appeared that he had in the past ignored all inquiries about me or at best treated them lightly. Requests to the Tate for assistance had taken place over many years, frustrating the previous owners in the process, and it was not until Martin was tempted to Canada that he was finally obliged to defend his mandate and personal ideology and give a concrete opinion on me. Of the hard evidence gathered—that in most courts would conclusively support a JMW Turner authentication, Martin’s contradictory and at times befuddled attempts at denying it, were as much humorous as they were exasperating. His crafty arguments against the evidence might have seemed less futile if he had all his facts to begin with. If he had read carefully the information Bob sent him beforehand he would have been more prepared to ward off the onslaught of both scientific and stylistic arguments, or else accept them.

Regardless, he was the expert that everyone must defer to on questions of authenticity, because, in his field he effects a “monopoly” with the two most powerful auction firms. And if any other firm or dealer had the audacity to sell a newly discovered or rediscovered Turner outside this rarified circle, they would not only risk low prices, but also risk being sued for fraud. Without Martin’s support it was clear that a work could never be properly accepted, gain a fair and equitable status, or bring a fair and equitable price. Yes, with respect to Turner, Martin’s dominant position was synonymous with the two closely-knit auction firms, which in the 1990s controlled ninety percent of the four-billion-dollar worldwide auction trade—Christie’s and Sotheby’s.

Evening Aug 18 Arrival

Bob’s Journal entry, Aug 2001…

At the  airport as people were trickling down the offloading ramp, without needing to ask, I recognized our guests immediately. They seemed melded: , a sturdy mother figure, handsome for her years and the shorter Martin who seemed tucked into her munificent bosom as they carried on a deep conversation. More than anything else, it was his tailored suit and keen eyes that gave them away.

Oddly, one of the first things said was about business and money, and how important they were to art. I thought this irrelevant at the time, interjected aimlessly within the first few moments of our meeting, and even by the time Martin and Frances left for home, I had still not resolved this puzzle, although it was repeated similarly on one other occasion. Was this a hint that a fee should be paid for Martin’s expertise? If this was true, surely I would have heard about it long before they arrived. The only overt agreement we had made about my financial commitment was the weeklong all-expense paid visit to Canada. There might have been something missed in the translation; but nonetheless, later I learned that Christie’s was paying a per diem fee.

According to James Henry Duveen, “by the early part of the twentieth century, presents and douceurs to such experts had gradually changed into very high fees. Even the redoubtable Dr. Wilhelm von Bode, head of the imperial museums, began to expect more than just a present to one of his museums. Such arrangements with the experts did not remain a trade secret, but for example, came out under cross-examination in the sensational lawsuit in  about the portrait La Belle Ferronniere.” Might this still be the case! Surely, Martin would have been forthright about the issue if it was.

On our short journey from  to , Martin talked about many of his adventures abroad, and  was in the back seat with Graham getting a sense of her new surroundings. Her questions about local affairs, climate, the educational system, and of course Ogopogo, our legendary lake monster were answered by Graham with enthusiasm. That evening beside , Christine joined us for dinner at Salty’s, one of our favourite little ethnic restaurants. The taperecordings proved useless. The loud speakers that were meant to entertain with the likes of Bob Marley were positioned directly over head. The boisterous pedestrian traffic and the fact that Martin sat on the wrong side of Graham for the microphone to be effective were two other vexations.

     Our new acquaintances were more than entertaining—they proved to be the lead characters in a bizarre drama that was to play itself out over the ensuing week. Not only did Martin command attention, he also demanded it with a continuum of rhetoric, including anecdotes that entertainingly came across as tall tales. His talent for dramatic inflection and his impeccable timing was that of a great comedian. His frequent roars of laughter, groans of despair, and cracking retorts, might even at times convince those present that they were at a Broadway production. Needless to say, his personality must have gone a long way in that potent world he was connected to for so many years as a bureaucrat. Could this same personality and cleverness have kept that politically volatile environment off guard, working in his direction, and easier for him to control? What gave special zest to his Spy versus Spy delivery for many of his reminiscences and accounts was certainly his great interest in detective stories and crime fiction, the literature he seemed to cherish most of all. …End journal entry.

Yes Bob, but it is also vital to appreciate the contributions made by . She was helpful for the pauses she provided during discussions—they were somewhat like intermissions. At times, her comments hit the mark, but frequently her lingering expletives did nothing more than stop conversation. She always began with such charming nobility, but often the beginning was all she issued before a great long pause. Martin, who was ever anxious to tell a new story or repeat one, always filled the void. Expressions such as: “Hand A,” “yes-yes-yes,” “that’s marvelous but…” or just plain—“but”!  used the singular “but” so frequently that everyone was constantly looking over their shoulder to see if she was calling out derogatorily. Discounting when she was defending Martin from attacks against his oftentimes-impossible logic, she was very sweet to have around. You must admit.

Complementary to ’ seeming vacuity you played a similar role. While searching for what was really going on in Martin’s mind, your questions often sounded uninformed, and occasionally even bigoted. You must have assumed that  was doing the same with you—testing your knowledge, and possibly even your potential risk as a foe.

Graham was cool, mostly taciturn, but at times he shot from the hip with lucid remarks and pointed questions. His attempts to catch Martin contradicting himself were mostly veiled in easy conversation. Was I the only one that noticed that by the end of the week both Frances and Martin were increasingly careful answering questions… if they would answer them at all!

A.m. Aug 19 In the boudoir

“It’s a slow process,” was Martin’s first statement after seeing me for the first time. His privileged decision was not to be given immediately. For Martin, could seeing me for the first time have been like looking at an envelope in the post with his name on it, without knowing who sent it? His indecision was welcome; the visit was to last a week and there was certainly no need for a hasty verdict.

Christine and Frances carried on their visit talking about local affairs while Martin, Graham and Bob started up the stairs. My centralized placement on the wall in the living-room was fortunate. I could follow pretty much everything that was going on. The three halted at the top of the stairs. Martin had spotted a familiar book on the landing titled In Honour of Paul Mellon. It was like seeing an old friend. “Ah,” Martin sighed. “Paul Mellon—he was a great help to me!” Bob already knew the likely connection between Martin and Paul Mellon because of the Mellon/Yale involvement with British art and because Yale University Press published the B & J Catalogue. Without any prompting, Martin explained how his apparent affection toward Paul Mellon tied in with the Paul Mellon Centre for Studies in British Art, and  Press. “My whole life is sort of intertwined,” he confessed. Beside the free travel and book publishing support, the “dearest” benefit was affording a safe haven to court the girl of his dreams—.

**Chapter 29**

## The Astonishing Monogram

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

A.m. Aug 22—The Astonishing Monogram

Bob picked up the Butlins from their hotel for the last time. The plan was to stop home momentarily before striking out for . When offered a coffee for the road, Martin spontaneously erupted with another Tate Gallery musing. Martin’s life was inextricably tied to his mother institution; one could not even offer coffee without being thrust back to the Tate.

 “When the Tate was small, we all got together for a cup of coffee.” Although this appeared to elicit pleasant memories, Martin expressed his frustrations over how the staff would leave a project for coffee just as he felt they were getting something accomplished. He pondered over how the institution had grown so big; he was not certain who was who any more, or where they all belonged, “That’s the trouble everything gets bigger and bigger.” Perhaps Martin was too close to the matter to see or understand that the normal lifecycle of a bureaucracy is invariably tied up with “empire building.” If the funds and political will are present, growth is unbounded. The Tate being a focus for fervent National pride, it is unlikely that Martin will ever see a reversal of this trend.

As the last few things were being packed Martin was blindsided. “Sorry, to bring some business into the morning,” Graham interjected, “but can we go around one more time and write down a few points?” He led his compliant master back into the fray slowly. The result was splendid. The first whispers between Martin and Graham about the type of paper used for the Anderson Turner watercolour, ended with a rigorous battle over the inscription on my pier.

Things leaped off topic several times. Everyone was just about out the door and on their way, when Graham charged forth one last time. It was the inscription, J W T—it had not been discussed yet! Bob and Graham assumed that Martin had taken it into account already. The information about it was sent to Christie’s on compact disc in duplicate. One might have expected a short sensible discussion over the inscription, but what ensued was absolute pandemonium. Bob became disoriented when Martin said, “One would like to think it was put on later.” In effect this would make it fake. Apparently he was looking to Bob for support.

His intermittent efforts over the next twenty minutes trying to gain an ally in this regard went from gentle hope to near blasphemy, and ended in a pithy plea for compliance. He wanted the initials forsaken for the sake of expedience. “Well, what one would like to think…” he instructed, “is that the letters were put on later.” Within minutes he went further. “What I’m trying to make clear; it would be a damn sight better from everybody’s point of view, if it didn’t have any signa… initials on it at all!” Finally, with his next to last breath, “As I say, it would make life much easier if they weren’t there.” This surreal episode ended with a whimper.

Frances commandeered Bob’s black light to prove whether or not the antique varnish was truly over top of the letters. They both agreed it was. She then pointed a spotlight at the little squares as Martin exclaimed, “Now I do in fact see the ‘T’ much better.” As his eyes wandered off the mark to the top of the pier, and away from the sequence of three paint blotches wherein the letters were found, he scoffed, “Look, I’ve found a little face there!” Was this meant to infer that imagination can play games with perception? Likely, but for Martin, only selectively!

When all four belligerents eventually made their way into the study to view the enlarged computer images of the paint blotches (as Francis had entreated they do) Martin admitted hesitantly, “That definitely is a W, I do accept that.”

The discussions degenerated as  contended that the inscription had been scratched into the surface rather than inscribed into wet paint. Martin echoed, “That’s scratched.” In the end, Martin and Frances were stumped as to why the letters were there in the first place and eventually made all efforts to deny them completely. Soon they were calling them accidental markings. Yet Martin kept trying to answer his own question, ‘Why J W T and not J M W T’. §Just to remind everyone… In 1802, just after I was painted and at the time JMW was made an RA, he simultaneously made the addition to his name. He add the M for milord (AKA Mallord). It was partly a spoof and partly in deference to his mother’s family name. §

It was all but certain that the material Bob sent Martin about the inscription had been lost or neglected, and up until his visit, was unseen. He admitted, “The trouble is, I didn’t… probably, really look at them.” Without adequate lead time it was nearly impossible for him to formulate a successful off-hand confutation. In the process of attempting to do so, both he and Frances attacked the evidence and its purveyor—Bob—with surly shouts, akin to the way a master might control an unruly dog.

It was frightening how they contradicted and rebutted their own logic. First seeing, and then not seeing the inscription—agreeing that it was under the antique varnish; then swearing it was scratched deliberately into my surface, and finally declaring that the marks were merely accidental. The most disarming bit of faulty logic was the insistence that the initials were “done in two different ways,” scratched and inscribed. Things became more and more confusing.

With confidence fortified by her vocation as a restorer of pictures,  was energetically willing to help Martin grapple his opponents. Before the team succumbed to crying, “accidental, accidental!”  had used the word ‘scratch’ so many times that Christine was worried that their house was buggy. What she was inferring of course, was that the inscription was done into the dry paint, and thus should be considered a forged signature. The aging Martin had gnashed his teeth and gasped so many times that Bob was beginning to fear for his health.

The perceived differences between the highly magnified images of the J, and the W T, were caused by the slightly differing angle of light while being photographed, and the way in which it refracted through the antique varnish. It glistened more brightly within the hollows of the inscription (and other depressions in my paint surface also). The computer only enhanced the illusion. The underlying initials were all inscribed into the wet paint, all at the same time, and at the time the pier was painted. JMW took this narrow window of time before the paint dried, and into this all too convenient spot scribed his JWT. Whether the blotches themselves had been placed intentionally for the purpose, or whether he was inspired by their unintended, but tempting regularity, one will never know.

Why were Martin and Francis so tenacious? It seemed like a personal issue for them and an extremely critical issue at that, one that needed a commiserate battle as one might be obliged to endure for the sake of survival. Could it have been that this evidence was too powerful and too unyielding for Martin to plow under? Was he concerned about how he would deal with this point in his follow-up report for Christie’s?

Graham and Bob had been studying the inscription and its implications for a year, ever since Paul Biró found it while searching for fingerprints. They had analyzed it obsessively, and believed it was unshakable proof of a Turner connection. But the senseless shaking by Martin and Frances persisted until everyone gave up in frustration. Martin was already mumbling to Graham about how he would be able to dismiss the inscription in his report. His would-be acolyte stood entranced.

Would it not be expected, even out of curiosity, to try and reason why the initials are there in the first place? It was as though Martin and Frances had a vested interest in my status. There are undoubtedly issues that directly affect Martin, but the obvious ones should not have commanded such compulsive behavior.

They were not out the door yet! For the most part, Martin and Frances found their stay in , laid back—except for the cross-examinations. The morning of August 22 was one of those exceptions. It was necessary to give Martin one last trial before he escaped . Out came Bob’s book by John Anderson Jr., The Unknown Turner, the same publication and author that Martin was charged with reporting on for the Oxford Companion to J.M.W. Turner.

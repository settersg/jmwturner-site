**Chapter 26**

## Looking Blindly

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

Am Aug 21—Looking Blindly

It was already into the third hour of serious discussion that morning. Bob fell into conversation with , partly for her ability to carry on a two-way conversation, and possibly as an escape. Not so for Graham, his young blood was still well-charged as he tempted Martin upstairs and back to Ehrenbrietstein. He had found some comparable foliage in an “accepted” JMW painting to test Martin’s main argument against the oil sketch—the argument about the way Turner painted trees. On the way up the stairs the cadence of pounding footsteps was reinforced by Martin’s chant: “looked, and looked, and looked”; one “looked” for each ascending step. This was to remind Graham, without doubt of the importance of looking, looking, looking—the key to an expert’s magical powers.

After they reached the top of the stairs Graham got what he asked for—Martin’s connoisseurship. Comparing the trees in a small illustration from the B & J Catalogue, his low monotone whisper was hauntingly like that of a mystic. Was this a chestnut typically shared under such circumstances: not quite trite, not quite cliché? De-mystified! His invocation was carefully measured at first but ended with a bout of jubilant and whimsical laughter:

These [leaves] are more softly done and there is sort of unity about the whole of what grows off that branch. Which in a way, also, in fact, there’s much more variation here, in fact there’s two different-coloured trees. There’s a green one and a brown one, but here you go through the subtlety of… this is a green one, but you’re still seeing a bit of the orangey one through it, but there, there is more foliage and the modulation in the depth of green which is the green branches are in front of the green branches, front of the orange so they begin—there, as it were—the leaves are thicker. He wouldn’t have painted that from the trees, but his knowledge of having looked at trees when he went fishing.” Martin, then, with a burst of excitement and a lot more coherence shouted: ‘As I said he went fishing, and when you’re fishing you have an awful lot of time to look at trees. Ha, Ha, Ha!’

Was this simply an outburst of innocent joy or the cackle of a cunning oracle betraying a spoof? Martin was always full of good-natured fun, and after this entertaining ruse one could only feel charmed. He settled back temporarily to the task at hand, but soon Graham started asking short incisive questions. At first these were deflected indifferently, but as Graham persisted, Martin became slightly frustrated and started to make a more clear and hard appraisal. Frustration soon led to anger and eventually an outright savage condemnation of the poor little painting. Paradoxically, this attack led him to deceive his own “Hand” theory.

As Martin glared at the painted surface of the tiny “orphan,” he chastised: “The actual way the paint’s put on isn’t right and I’ve seen other things like this.”

Graham: What about the impasto?

Martin: “This blobby look, which doesn’t really—,” pointing to the centre of the painting he exclaimed, “What the hell’s that meant to be!” Pointing at another area, “What’s that meant to be?”

                            Orrock Ehrenbrietstein, detail of village

Graham: It’s very sketchy.

It should be remembered that Martin had initially dubbed this little Orrock painting Hand C. Quoting from his Oxford Companion, page 100: “Hand C’ is distinguished by the small size of the paintings, in oils, and by an over delicate prettification of Turnerian style and motifs.” This description is far from being hellishly incoherent and “blobby.”

Martin continued his assault: “Even in the most sketchy Turner you can tell…” He abruptly stopped himself as he started to recognize some of the content, “Oh! That is a sail of a ship; I don’t know what these are.”

Graham answered enthusiastically, “Actually, those are houses.”

Martin: “So they are, yeah.”

At this point Graham might have called forth Turner himself, or at least Ruskin, who had borne witness to a similar muddling situation. Ruskin had explained on behalf of Turner, “In a proof impression of a plate lately submitted to me, the engraver had failed to discern the distant representation of a village at the base of a hill, and had substituted some unintelligible nothings. Turner had run a heavy pencil line into the margin of the paper to intimate that these were ‘houses;’ and the miniature village seemed to come into focus as if by magic.” Yes, at Graham’s prompting the miniature village at the base of the hill came into focus for Martin, as if by magic (see image on back cover).

Graham Reynolds expressed well the power and immediacy of Turner’s brushwork at the apex of his career when he wrote that “his graphic shorthand enabled him to simplify what he saw into a few swift mnemonic strokes.” As one JMW painting to another, Martin’s hellish blobs might better be described as swift mnemonic strokes. One has to stand up for his own!

After a couple of more-pointed questions from Graham, Martin decided to shut down any more challenges by unabashedly calling the little picture a late nineteenth-century fake! Martin added condescendingly, “It’s interesting but it’s not worth a lot of money I’m afraid,” then snickered.

Undeterred, Graham made an enquiry about the support the picture was painted on. It was a book board that had a marbled design on the back very much like the boards seen on other Turner sketchbooks. Martin commented that Turner was willing to use ‘found’ supports but he would not concede or perhaps did not understand that it would be unlikely that a faker would have used such a scrap. Graham asked, “How would a nineteenth century faker even know Turner used book boards?” Martin replied, “He may not have even thought that anybody would be bothered to look at the back of the picture.”

It was a standard for the economy-wise Turner to use every available scrap, including the book boards of his sketchbooks. While reinterring the neglected remains of several of the late artist’s drawings back in their archive, Ruskin made a special note that Turner had used even book boards for his sketches.

Graham continued pressing for solid stylistic reasoning: “Is the way he used the palette knife in the sky like Turner?”

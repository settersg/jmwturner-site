**Chapter 21**

## Portrait of a Soul

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

A.m. Aug 20—Portrait of a Soul

The dinner went well but the zealous entertainment that evening precluded any tape-recording. George Kayser had all the time needed to observe his subject in many of his varied poses. As always, Martin was alive to the moment.

George, a renowned expatriate Czech artist had been living in Canada since the late 1960s, when the Soviets took control of his homeland. It forced him and his young family out in search of greener pastures, and less politically charged ones. As a leading exponent of the Modern art movement, he gained significant stature and financial success before he was forced to flee. Once arriving in , he struggled to get his work understood, never mind marketed and sold. Now, as a vigorous old man his appreciation for life is beyond financial success. His grandchildren, his pipe, Czechoslovakian beer, and a swim in Okanagan Lake—this complex of simple pleasures is what fashions the personal Shangri-La of his twilight years.

He is a master at capturing personalities, but more importantly, he has an unusual ability to paint people while they move—skills he mastered while painting conductors and quartets during their performances. This was utterly essential for painting a dynamic person like Martin.

To get a good personality portrait Bob knew it was better if George had the opportunity to observe his subject at length beforehand, and this wanted doing while Martin was being as uninhibited as possible. It was best that Martin was unaware of the stealthy goal. At the Theo’s dinner party George sat directly across from Martin. By the end of the evening, Martin was told about the proposed portrait—he agreed whole heartedly. The next morning, before George arrived with his easel and paint box, Martin had already been dodging art questions for an hour. This was ideal. Martin was energized in a way he most likely felt familiar, the immediacy of the resulting visage said it all (see image No. 12).

Both he and Frances were astounded that it took so little time to complete—one and a half hours. They were also enthusiastic about the results.  coddled Martin by disagreeing with him when he complained, “It makes me look much younger.” Discussions about Kayser and his expressive talent, particularly for doing portraits came up several times during their weeklong visit. With portraiture somewhat out of fashion it is rare to find an artist like Kayser. Jokingly, Graham commented that even the Queen of  had difficulty finding an artist that could do the job right. He had apparently seen a recent attempt.

Evening Aug 20 Magnum Dinner

Bob’s Journal entry…

The winery visits that were planned for the afternoon went like clockwork. We visited  Cellars as part of our wine tasting tour because it was on the way home for the Kaysers. George had created a large exterior mosaic for the winery several years earlier (see image No. 19). It proved a splendid photo opportunity. After a few more wine tastings in the Naramata area, the Butlins were dropped back to their hotel to rest. Before long, the five of us including Graham were seated for dinner at Magnums on the lake, located in the Butlin’s hotel.

Lighthearted chatter and well-primed imaginations led us on a few of Martin’s early travel adventures while he was installed at the Tate Gallery. Martin was certainly no stranger to North America, and his first visit described as a ‘grand tour’ lasted three and a half months. In the  he was already seen as somewhat of a celebrity.

His experiences were varied: from being held up in a frigid little domicile in Louisiana where noses were turning blue from an overzealous air-conditioner, to his royal treatment in a chauffeur-driven limousine in New York. The opulence of the Vanderbilt mansion impressed him of course, but his excitement was most evident while discussing, for example, the swamps and alligators of the south, that mysterious setting for his favorite literature—detective thrillers.

There were apparently a lot of Turners in private collections in the  at the time, at least until  Gowing, Martin’s senior at the Tate organized an exhibition in  with Martin acting as expert. It was early in Martin’s career, but he fulfilled his purpose as the incredulous art expert quite admirably. The cover for this operation was the exhibition, Turner: Imagination and Reality held at the  in 1966. Martin made it clear that he dismissed all the Turner paintings brought in for assessment.

 In support of this exigency, Gowing had a manifesto: “Our own frame of aesthetic reference is permissive to the point of chaos.” It is clear that permissive attributions were not to be tolerated. This harsh position must have fostered fear in the young Martin—forging a similar hard-line attitude. 1966 was a massacre.

Pardon me. There was one exception to the carte blanche dismissal of those formerly-accepted Turner paintings brought in for assessment at the  exhibition. It was a “very fascinating” watercolour from the daughter of Mrs. Vanderbilt. Of the Americans, the Vanderbilts and Mellons seemed to bring the warmest memories for Martin. There was in fact, a strong link between the Vanderbilts and British aristocracy. Consuelo Vanderbilt became the Duchess of  a generation before Martin’s own era.

The  exhibition was also the first chance Martin had to condemn some of those “ Turners” that he had heard so much about. According to his recollection, the following is how one of those watershed days unfolded (an excerpt):

This was the famous  exhibition arranged by the amazing man called  Gowing. This was Turner, the modern. It absolutely established Turner in smart circles in . Most New Yorkers wouldn’t [normally] look at anything older than five years. Then suddenly they saw Turner… Then of course it meant that everyone brought in their famous Turners [to be assessed]. This is when I began to see these  Turners, which I talked about, these amateur things. I’d never seen them before.

One day there was waiting for me in this office space I worked in—a very good watercolour. I said: “Well, this is really quite something, whom does it belong to?” “A member of staff,” was the answer. “Why isn’t she here?” and to Martin’s query came the telling reply, “She was frightened you’d dismiss it like you’d dismissed everything else!” “Well this is fascinating,” continued Martin, “where did she get it?” Reply: “Well her mother is a Vanderbilt.”

Martin certainly learned to appreciate this aristocratic American family early; they had chauffeured him around and taken him to their art laden “three hundred room mansion” to see the proverbial “Turner in the dining room.” This relates to a whimsical story Martin told about how the wealthy in  would impress others of the same ilk during dinner parties by displaying their would-be Turners in the dining room.

Knowing the directorship makeup of the  and the titled British interests involved with the exhibition, it was possible to make a rough correlation between money in  and aristocratic privilege in . Certainly the names listed, like Rockefeller, Phillips, Whitney, and Ford would mesh comfortably with the names on the British side of the equation. Names such as: His Excellency Sir Patrick Dean, Sir Collin Anderson, The Right Honourable Viscount Radcliffe, and Sir Frank Francis, The Right Honourable Lord Robbins, and Sir Philip Hendy.

Our dinner party was the first opportunity for us to corner Martin about the legendary John Anderson Jr., the author of The Unknown Turner. Martin was the authority who had condemned the book to the fakes and copies section of the Oxford Companion, so presumably we were about to find out the true story.

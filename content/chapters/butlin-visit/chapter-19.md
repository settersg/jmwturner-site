**Chapter 19**

## The Stage Was Set

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

The Stage Was Set

The stage was set. All the efforts of the last several years were to be decided worthy or worthless in one short moment, the length of time it took to grimace or grin. Martin Butlin had agreed to come to  and assess me firsthand. The subject of much misfortune and dubious criticism for decades, I was now about to take my just place in history. Or was I? What if Butlin, one of the most renowned and politically influential British art experts condemned me?

Arrangements for the trip began with Mrs. Butlin answering the telephone in her flat in —her husband was out. According to Bob she had a very light British accent and the tone of her voice spoke of polite modesty. He hoped when phoning back in an hour, as she suggested, he would find Martin Butlin of the same temperament. He was not disappointed; his tension was relieved by Butlin’s overt friendliness. Bob described the Okanagan and  as a great place to vacation, and once he was assured that Butlin was willing to make the journey, he invited his wife to join him. Bob suggested they treat the trip like a mini-vacation.

Bob was encouraged when, after seeing the photographs he gave the painting a fifty-fifty chance of being right. Nicholas White had used a figure of only ten percent. Two points that Butlin seemed to bristle over during the telephone conversation were: the provenance as it related to James Orrock, and the fingerprint evidence. Of course the latter was the strongest available proof that could dislodge a curatorial opinion. This inauspicious hint might have sparked a horrible premonition, but Bob was unwilling to fall prey to paranoia at this warm and fuzzy point in their early relationship.

Butlin also disputed whether all the pieces from the 1904 Orrock sale had been genuine. This disparagement seemed misplaced considering Orrock’s fame as a connoisseur. I can vouch that he was one of the main experts of his era. Many an influential visitor to his home, many of whom brushed me with their cloaks, were awed by what they saw—including John Ruskin, and Cosmo Monkhouse—both JMW experts.

Not only had Orrock emulated JMW throughout his own painting career, he had been responsible for recording for posterity some of JMW’s secret working methods. Martin’s incredulous declaration did not account for the unimpeachable provenances previous to Orrock—that of Griffith and Sir Charles Robinson. Of the many Turner pictures that Orrock owned in the late 19th century, there were some that shocked the auction world with their high prices. Walton Bridges, which sold as the ‘lot’ just before me, and Rockets and Blue Lights which brought an international record 25,000 pounds nearly a century ago. It is obvious that Orrock was not in the habit of buying dubious works.

Of Orrock’s oil paintings listed in the B & J Catalogue, all of them found their way to prestigious collections: Mountain Landscape with a Lake (B & J 38) to the Fitzwilliam Museum, Cambridge; Walton Bridges (B & J 63) to the National Gallery of Victoria, Melbourne; Newark Abbey on the Way (B & J 65) to the Yale Center for British Art; Rockets and Blue Lights (B & J 387) to the Sterling and Francine Clark Art Institute; and, Off the Nore: Wind and Water (B & J 476) to the Yale Center for British Art.

     On reviewing his first telephone conversation with Butlin, Bob reasoned that my pedigree might not have been the clear target of malice; it was at the point when he mentioned Ehrenbrietstein that the rebuff occurred. He had mentioned that it had also come from the Orrock collection. Butlin then muttered the cruel snipe. But more than anything it was the attack on the fingerprint evidence that set Bob aback. Butlin seemed confident that generally speaking, fingerprints were being faked—a friend had told him so. The Internet did not reveal any information about this type of fraud so Bob decided to get a reaction from one who should know better than anyone—Peter Paul Biró.

E-mail May 23 01: “Bonjour Paul, I hope this email finds you and yours happy and in good health. I’ve been told that fingerprints are now being faked. Is this possible? If so, how would this affect using them as evidence to support a painting’s authenticity?” The next day Paul replied. He assured Bob there was no worry, “the fakes are easy to catch by an expert examiner—they just look poor—a rubber stamp usually.”

I caught Bob mumbling about the overly tight collaboration that existed for many years among members of the Turner cult. Those connected to special interest groups as diverse as the Victoria and Albert Museum, National Gallery, British Museum, Tate Gallery, the twin firms of Christie’s and Sotheby’s, and a limited number of connected dealers. Some of these tight-knit relationships were soon to be revealed during casual conversations with Bob’s visitor from abroad. In the meantime, he remained troubled over how many of the people within Butlin’s circle had been acrimonious toward me for so many years. He worried, how could they possibly support me now and at the same time save face and maintain allegiances.

There was more that added to his discomfort. What about the hapless sting when I was embroiled in not only legal wrangling, but also criminal ones? How did this make Butlin feel? Certainly he knew many of the sordid details and as rumour might have it, probably more than what actually took place. Such a kafuffle would make any civil servant with a secure job reticent about getting involved.

It was easy to reason that there were a number of tentacles attached to the beast that bated Bob and Graham. They were only beginning to understand where these were anchored but were yet oblivious to the underlying ogre—the system of royal honours and patronage. Patrician support often relates to prestige, power, and money. This is not a brilliant revelation by any means; aspirations such as these have been rooted in British culture for many centuries. Was an agent of this same giant to arrive before long on Bob’s doorstep here in ?

E-mail from Martin Butlin (excerpt):

1) I could come on my own, flying out from  on Saturday 26th May and returning on Thursday 31st. Unfortunately, my wife, who is a professional picture restorer, has a number of commitments that make it impossible for her to take time off at the moment.

2) We could both come for about a week, flying out anytime on or after 17th August. I imagine, however, that you would not want to put things off so long.

You very kindly said that you would cover my expenses, basically the fare plus accommodation. However, before you commit yourself I would like to repeat my opinion, based on a photograph of the picture, that the painting is more likely not to be by Turner than to be genuine—this just to make sure that there is no misunderstanding!

 It was most kind of you to invite my wife as well but, unfortunately, it does not look as if this will be possible unless you are prepared to wait until August. My wife sends her greetings and warmest thanks for your generosity; she is only sorry that it does not look as though she will be able to take advantage of this.

Bob sent a quick reply: It would be disappointing if your wife were unable to accompany you, so I suggest we wait until August. I am pleased to hear that she is a picture restorer; …. Have you had the opportunity to read the multi-page document prepared about the painting? Mr. White has a copy. Please let me know your decision, and if you decide on the later date I will send you some information about the sunny Okanagan.

The dates were soon set, and the Butlins were coming for the third week in August. The itinerary was slowly taking shape and along with a package of information about British Columbia and the bank draft for their airfare, Bob took the opportunity to do a little more proselytizing over me—particularly about the 1844 letter to Griffith.

**Chapter 25**

## The Only Painting Ever

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

A.m. Aug 21—The Only Painting Ever!

“J. C. Robinson, a brilliant connoisseur, whose purchases on behalf of the  laid the foundations of its richness…” Sir Oliver Millar

Graham and Bob were fortunate to hear firsthand Martin’s hierarchal listing for his colleagues in the world of Turner expertise. His ranking was a response to their enquiry, and it seemed to work on down from himself, through the ranks at the Tate, culminating with those supplementary types on the outside—the more scholarly or sociological ones. Although those on the outside had some good points according to Martin, it was clear that their opinions were considered irrelevant when it came to authenticating artworks by JMW.

The main weakness of those on the fringe was apparent—they had not worked with the pictures at the Tate Gallery. This, regardless of the point made earlier that the sampling at the Tate was not fully representative of the artist’s work. Martin grudgingly agreed with this but insisted the Tate was a critical element for learning the ropes nonetheless. For obvious reasons, the isolated exception was Evelyn Joll from Agnew’s, co-author of the B & J Catalogue; he topped the list along with Martin but had recently passed away. There was no other name mentioned outside of the museum community. According to Martin, Evelyn Joll had been the diligent scholar for the catalogue and this along with his access to the all-important Agnew files made him indispensable at the time.

 It is best to hear it in Martin’s own words. As a commander might do, he set his jaw firmly and started to recite: “There is myself, there is Andrew Wilton, there is David Blayney Brown, there is Ian Warrell, and all three of those work at the Tate.”

Graham brought up David Hill, and after the laughter over his connection to Biró had subsided, Martin admitted, “Although Hill is very good, he is very unmethodical.” Afterwards granting that, “He obviously has a pretty good idea what a Turner looks like.” Later, Martin was slightly less generous, “David Hill—I wouldn’t rule out entirely.” Of course this was at a point when Martin was being worried again by Graham over Biró. He had mentioned the news article involving Landscape with Rainbow, and how David Hill strongly factored the fingerprint evidence into his assessment of the painting. Martin argued that he himself would have approached it the other way round, saying, “It looks absolutely right, and by Jove, look, there’s a fingerprint!”

 Martin had been very skeptical of the painting initially, but admitted to Graham, “[Although] I wouldn’t go to the stake for that one, I think it’s probably right.” Was it because Martin was unwilling to go to the stake that the painting did not sell at either Christie’s or Sotheby’s? As a result of the alternative sales venue, might we also suspect that this was the reason it sold cheaply compared to Kenneth Clark’s Folkestone, for example? It brought less than two-hundred thousand dollars versus the relative sum of twenty-eight million dollars for the  painting (by today’s valuation)—less than one percent the amount. Is Martin that powerful?

Graham also asked about John Gage. Martin said, “He’s very good on the theory, imagination and things like that.” He then succumbed to telling another of his dramatized anecdotes. “Gage came back from somewhere waving a picture. He said it was Turner’s off  cliffs, and everybody said, ‘Oh god!’ in strident disbelief. Turner did illustrate topographical work…that John Gage is fantastic at. Again, I think he slightly overdoes it. But he’s not the old-fashioned connoisseur. That’s now out of fashion, connoisseurship is rated as—down there. In some ways its fine until of course you have a problem like this [Shipwreck, the Rescue].” Martin felt empowered to use his latent skills as a connoisseur to their fullest for the present case. But was Martin saying that because John Gage was not an old-fashioned connoisseur, he would not be able to give a worthy opinion on me?

Martin: “He wouldn’t be the person I’d go to, to say that this painting is by any particular artist, but I would read John Gage because he writes much more interesting stuff about Turner.”

Marcia Pointon’s name came up several times and as Martin ‘rated’ her at this point he automatically retold the folkloric tale about the Peasant Girl and the Gold Tipped Hoe. Martin called it a classic. The trouble with Marcia Pointon, according to Martin was that, “She’s not a connoisseur, so she could build this great socialist interpretation of a picture.”  added, “And not look at the picture.” Martin agreed, “And not look at the picture, and probably even get the artist wrong.”

The Peasant Girl and the Gold Tipped Hoe: this particular anecdotal gem seemed to defend ostensibly what might be considered Martin’s own bulwark, the status quo; that which condemned the wildly over-interpretive craze based on sociology. On this point he counseled Marcia Pointon during one of her exhibitions; it was over the same ‘gold tipped hoe’. As a benevolent mentor might be expected, he tolerantly wove this particular allegory for all present with patient articulation:

 There was a little village street with a girl in roughish clothes leaning against a wall, with an older man talking to her. And, this was the rich old man trying to seduce the nice pure young peasant girl. And my question was, “Why do you think he’s a rich old man?” The reasoning was that he had a suit. Martin then instructed Ms. Pointon: “But then you know, in those days in  and up until recently it didn’t matter how poor you were, you sat around all day in a dark suit in a rush cane chair.” “Because he’s holding a gold tipped cane,” came the return volley! So I looked at her and said, “You know—sorry—you want to go and do a little work in the garden. That’s a garden hoe!” This whole thing was built up on this wrong fact. It was in fact the light shining on the wooden handle; it was unmistakably a hoe that’s used to root out roots and things you know. I’m not a gardener in agriculture or anything, but I know what a garden hoe is! (Laughter ensued.)

What we are left with are two disparate points of view stemming from two disparate dogmatisms. As Martin suggested, perhaps Ms. Pointin had wrongly approached her interpretation from a feminist perspective, looking for faults with the old man by inferring a masterly dominance over the symbol of vulnerable womanhood—the young maiden. Martin’s antithetical argument may have been setting the stage for his own interpretive tale. In the end, neither one seemed interested in the picture artistically. The romantic JMW would have rejected both ideas.

Momentarily, after Martin’s critique and bellicose laughter, things became unusually silent. Bob apparently thought he could catch him off-guard, so he slid in a question about a watercolour Martin had already seen in the dining room. It was titled in pencil on the verso, Vale of Avoca… The vintage Agnew frame, label and original inscription on the frame all pointed to JMW. There was also a provenance letter from the dealer, Lister and Son, which took it back to the not-so-inconsequential Rev. William Kingsley. The Reverend was a friend of JMW’s

                               Vale of Avoca detail, goats… greatly enlarged

Bob asked: “Do you think that’s a Turner, the watercolour with the goats?” The reason he focused on the goats, which were a minor part of the picture was that Martin had thought them outstanding the previous day. As described in Turner’s Liber Studiorum by W.G. Rawlinson, Turner’s deer and goats are rarely at fault, probably because they are generally associated rather with the wilder scenes of nature than with the surroundings of man.

Martin, after a long pause gave a curt reply “I’m not sure.” While clenching his teeth he gave a weak laugh and made a low growling noise. Trying to choose his words carefully: “I wouldn’t say: not my period, but I’d go to a watercolour specialist for that.” This was an odd thing for a person who had written a book titled Turner Watercolours back in 1962. The inside flap of the book states that “the practicing painter will be particularly interested in Butlin’s notes on the artist’s technique.”

For the Tate, Martin had also authored, Watercolours from the Turner Bequest in 1968. It was evident that for whatever reasons, Martin just did not have, in his “heart of hearts,” the confidence to make a decision and possibly “go to the stake for it.”

Martin confessed how stingy he had been in the past with Turner attributions. This dated back for more than thirty years of his tenure as a prominent Tate Gallery official. He appeared to express satisfaction that he only ever accepted one solitary painting. And when he reminisced about that particular picture, it was as though he was highlighting his prowess as a sleuth. His story started at Agnew’s in 1967 and dealt with a painting Martin made contentious in the first place—Neapolitan Fisher-Girls Surprised Bathing by Moonlight:

     I remember going to the Agnew exhibition and the picture looked wrong. At that moment I didn’t know it had a provenance that goes right back to . So I said to Evelyn Joll: ‘You know Evelyn, this looks wrong.’ And, Evelyn said: ‘Well look.’ He shows me the provenance – it goes right back to Robert Vernon. And I said, ‘Well I’m sorry, it may have a verdict or a thing that goes right back to Robert Vernon. But to me it looks wrong, and I say, just to me.’

## A Juggler's Paradise

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

A Juggler’s

In the case of second tier artworks—ones that are less core to the prestige of nations, quasi-monopolistic control is orchestrated by less influential groups. Seldom does one individual have the financial resources to promote and control the market of higher-priced artists independently. In the modern era, only one internationally-based dealer comes to mind, and that is Wildenstein and Co. But with the second tier and especially within niche markets, there can be both individuals and groups that affect the market as an oligopoly might. Most these participants are not graduates of renowned business schools or products of stock market and financial circles as the famous dealers Kahnweiler, and Rosenberg, or financiers such as William Weinberg, or investment bankers such as Charles Angrand (who would move from collector status to become financial director of Sotheby’s), but in their own awkward way the less powerful still affected their own small sector of the art market.

The collecting-cataloguing-marketing schema was no better exemplified than by the Sotheby’s sale of the American Weinberg collection. J.B. De la Faille had catalogued and effectively closed the book on van Gogh’s oeuvre (Weinberg was the first major collector under this cataloguer’s tutelage), In the meantime, Sotheby’s, by means of exuberant marketing had already started the upward spiral for works by modern masters. By the time they brought the Weinberg artworks back to  from the , they were worth many-fold what they had been at time of purchase, several sold for record-breaking prices. The art market has not looked back or downward since. But this has little to do with the second tier. Producing a complete catalogue raisonné for an artist of the second tier is seldom cost-effective, unless of course, a bigger long-term scheme is planned.

The third class of art is often referred to as decorative. Although these artists are “listed” quality artists, they are subject to the vagaries of the free market system and are traded freely. It is this type of art that is open to just about anyone to trade, buy, and sell; and pieces usually change hands from a paltry few thousand dollars, to several thousand. There is rarely a dispute over authorship because the stakes are low. If one traces the life history of artists such as John Delisle Parker, Jiří Kaiser, Gerald Roach, and Zeljko Kujundzic it is easily seen how these third-tier artists are undervalued—in fact, unjustly neglected.

If a major work by an artist such as van Gogh, Picasso, Rembrandt, Pollock, Monet, or Turner were to sell for under 100,000 dollars, such a low price would shake the market. Conversely, if a painting by an artist sold for double what might be expected, then the overall price level for the artist’s work would rise. When this happens one might justifiably suspect market manipulation.

According to one newspaper report: in the mid-1920s sensational and also suspect bids, under the aegis of commissaires-priseur Alphonse Bellier, established several previously little-known artists. The winners here, of course, were the artists themselves and the dealers who had presumably stockpiled their works. There was a tradition attached to the Hotel Drouot where Bellier worked, concerning the sales of antiques. “It portrayed a mysterious gang of dealers rigging all the auctions (the bande noire). The notion was adapted to interpret sales of modern art—the dealers were accused of buying at inflated prices, paintings by artists whose work they had in stock.”

Could the idea of bidding up prices also be used to enhance the reputation of the auction house where such a record-breaking painting sold? Certainly! Has it happened? Yes—in a roundabout way at least. In 1956, the innovative chairman of Sotheby’s, Peter Wilson, fiercely fought to secure for sale at auction an important painting by Nicolas Poussin rather than having it sell through a dealer as it would normally have done at that time. In order to secure the sale, he promised a guaranteed sale price. The painting sold for less than the guarantee, but although Sotheby’s had to pay the difference, a record price was made for the year. This was revolutionary, and the ensuing publicity drew more artwork into the auction arena. Reports of high and record-breaking prices have been fuel for the twin auction firms’ marketing strategy ever since.

Had the world of fine art—around which cultural identity is formed, become little more than a “cash cow” for the influential? The two top European dealers, Kahnweiler and Rosenberg, had trained in commerce, in commodities in , , and . In , July 1915, Tony Tollett delivered a lecture at the , and Literature and Arts dealing with the corporate influence of a few dealers in French art. “He said that these dealers quickly prospered by cultivating wealthy collectors, by buying all the works of an artist in order to speculate with full control over the stock, and by falsely inflating the prices. He blamed them for having influenced and bribed critics and having started art magazines in order to better control the market.”

The modern business model and exploitation that can accompany such practice was being applied to the world of art and apparently with great pecuniary reward. With Kahnweiler, we are first led to assume that his venture into collecting art as a young man was altruistic and mainly driven by an innocent passion for his subject. It might be interesting to rationalize this in light of his introduction to the world of finance under the aegis of his maternal uncles, who had specialized in buying and selling precious metals for over a century, and enjoyed a considerable fortune. “They could best be described as bankers, speculators, entrepreneurs or brokers.”  In  they quickly acquired a position on the stock market and controlled gold and diamond mines in . After being sponsored by those same family members, Kahnweiler opened a gallery in . The way he was to apply his ancestral wit in controlling the earliest bits of his own art-world empire—Braque, Derain, Vlaminck, and van Dongen—was described by Assouline: “When he decided to buy he bought everything... Exclusivity was the basis of his actions and the only rule he would never forsake:”

To the delight of both Kahnweiler and Picasso, with a 1960 exhibition held at the Tate Gallery in , they were ostentatiously gathered into the fold of the omnipotent triumvirate. “Roland Penrose organized the most complete exhibition ever held on a living artist at the Tate Gallery, naturally Kahnweiler was in attendance and gave Picasso a detailed report as his special envoy: the number of rooms, the distance between paintings, the chronology of the hanging, the names of the dealers Sotheby’s had invited, the opening party held in a tent set up in front of the Tate with paella, flamenco dancers, and art critics singing his praises. Everything was there.”

As a result, there would be no looking back for Picasso, or at his prices—he was now a “made” artist. The pivotal influence of those aforementioned cognoscenti is somewhat veiled here, but it is fair to assume that the British auction firms, dealers and allied curators were collaborating to the utmost.

An even more worrisome unnatural pressure on art prices happened within the last couple of decades. There was an enormous price-spike for Impressionist and modern paintings, this time as unintended consequences. It is mentioned here in order to show how, no matter what side of the economics equation price pressures come from, the art establishment is in the wings to guide matters in an orderly direction. This major price-spike was driven by unmanageable demand as opposed to intentional limitations on supply. Thousands of expensive impressionist and modern paintings made their way to  as a way to offer bribes during real estate dealings. Obviously the pressure of this abrupt increase in demand impacted prices internationally, “they not only rose sharply; they rose madly as well.” But this artificial price increase was not the worst of it. Works were sold, then immediately bought back for many times their value in order to secretly bribe people or at least surreptitiously transfer funds during land deals.

With the stock market downturn in 1987 and subsequent real estate market collapse in , these over-valued paintings were redirected into the hands of creditors who wanted rid of them. “When Michael Ainslie later analyzed the top twenty Japanese buyers at Sotheby’s in the years 1984-92, he discovered that seventeen of them were either bankrupt, in jail, or under serious investigation.” Christie’s and Sotheby’s have made it clear that if these paintings were dumped over a short period of time the art market would collapse. These paintings still sit in waiting. Surely the appropriate gatekeepers will ensure an orderly reintegration for some of those forsaken masterpieces notwithstanding the suspicious rumors that are already circulating over the authenticity of many of those works!

Most recently there has been a challenge to Wildenstein’s primacy in the world of Impressionist painting. This dealt with a Manet. In 1997, Sotheby’s refused to sell the painting, which had been fully authenticated and catalogued by Wildenstein. Suzanna Andrews joins the chorus about “the growing dominance of the auction houses over the market.” The frustrated Alec Wildenstein complained: “We happen to make the book on Manet. I think we know a bit more about Manet than either Sotheby’s or Christie’s.”

Is this a case where there are more aliens under attack by the Brits in their attempt to expand their already dominant position on the world stage? Could it be that Sotheby’s and Christie’s, the two firms at the top in the auction-house-dominated marketplace are attempting to shake loose a bit of the Wildenstein hegemony? The market for French paintings is likely the most lucrative in the world at this point in history. Controlling it would of course be a substantial coup, the twin firms are already the only effective venue to get top dollar for any class of art; all they need now is to take control of Impressionist expertise.

Bob and Graham are presently investigating three un-catalogued French artworks by historic artists controlled by the Wildenstein Institute, including a painting by Manet (image No. 8).  The Wildenstein Institute is at the core of the French art world and it is under their tutelage that many catalogues raisonnés are produced. The French art establishment, with full support from government and appropriate legislation, has entrenched rules that govern who are the official experts to hold court over each of their most important artists. In France, officialdom greatly cherishes the art-dependent tourist trade, and in turn they must dread how dysfunctional the fragile art world could become. The additional difficulties that arise in  are too complex to deal with here, but the problem with such entrenched expertise was poignantly described by Duane R. Chartier, Ph.D:

 "Legal edict as is the case in  where the government often confirms heirs or designates as the legal authenticators of recently deceased artists. This has created many untenable and often anti-academic situations where substantially unqualified people with far too much of a personal stake in the outcome are determining the authenticity of works."

When researching a painting, Pool Near Ornans signed Courbet, the Wildenstein Institute in  directed Bob to Jean-Jacques Fernier at the Institut Gustave Courbet. He was described first and foremost as the son of the first publisher of the Courbet catalogue. This was clearly meant to infer superior credentials. It is apparent that the French government and those concerned with such matters favour nepotism and lineage even for the production of catalogue raisoneés. Customs and proper protocol can thus become entrenched, and threats to the status quo and threats against market stability can therefore be more easily thwarted. It is not incongruous that the concept of droit moral originated in .

Durand-Ruel was either innocent or indifferent to the implications of his stealthy actions throughout the 1870s; in his memoirs from that time he stressed that a dealer must always do whatever he could to protect the prices of his artists. Wherever possible, he sought to obtain a monopoly of an artist’s oeuvre, buying up works from other dealers or collectors, and even, “bidding up’ pictures at auction to prevent prices from falling.” But how does this relate to more recent times? Could this have been where market control of an artist’s oeuvre was first orchestrated and the seed from which the modern art industry grew?

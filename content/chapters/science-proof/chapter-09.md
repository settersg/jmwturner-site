**Chapter 9**

## The Impenetrable Shield

> *Excerpt from Art World's Dirty Little Secret by R. Malcolm Setters / Graham Setters*

The Impenetrable Shield

 The art world has hesitantly gone along with the world’s greater reliance on science and technology. Watching from the sidelines I’ve wondered if this creeping empiricism hasn’t hampered people’s intuitive and emotional abilities. I like what Bob once wrote…

The need for a diverse outlook is vital when moving among grander ideas and philosophies. When talking about glowing sunsets based on glazing techniques, motherly love based on the Christian Madonna, or movement in a picture based on geometric orientation, one is left empty. We must be able to mate illusion with reality, imagination with knowledge and spirituality with the mundane in order to fully appreciate the world around us, particularly art.

Imagine yourself a technician in a laboratory, denuding a work of art by exposing its hidden secrets—like dissecting an angel. It is sacrilege unless done by conservation scientists with at least the will to turn their temporal deed into something less carnal. Should they confess a deeper emotion than mere curiosity? Some do, some do not, and some pretend. Those that do are enlightened, while those that do not will find only lead white in the fragile wings of Murillo’s angel or bitumen in the haloed grottos of el Greco.

     JMW himself made a great synthesis of seemingly diverse interests during his own lifetime, as would be expected of a “genius.” While somewhat abandoning his poetry and allegorical paintings, he looked toward Natural sciences for inspiration with “some of his works even imitating the iron filings of Faraday’s 1851 experiments with magnetism.”

Turner’s colleague, John Constable, proclaimed that in such an age as this, painting should be understood not as a poetic aspiration, but as a pursuit, legitimate, scientific, and mechanical. Alas, within the context of the same era and pressed by the hard edge of trade psychology, Morris Eaves advanced that “if art can claim science and mechanics as it once claimed poetry, in a country whose scientific and technological strengths are boosting the well-established strength of commerce, then painting may participate in the English success.”

     When my scientific documents arrived from Marilyn Laver, they looked great. They came in a tidy three-ring binder and had a dramatic black and white photograph of me on the cover. There were several large format photographs: full colour, infrared, ultraviolet, electron-microscopic. Detailed photographs were taken of things like my title label and stretcher, and there were several x-radiographic images as well. It had page after page of test results, with pigment names broken down into linguistically impossible contrivances. There were even photographs that made me look like a ghost. In the introduction, Marilyn described the genesis of her report as follows:

     In 1982, a scientific examination study was undertaken at the Canadian Conservation Institute (Ottawa) on a painting on canvas entitled “Shipwreck – The Rescue” in order to determine whether the materials and technique of the work were consistent with those of J. M. W. Turner.

    The painting was part of a group of works; which included others in the style of Picasso, Collins and Gainsborough. The request for the study came from the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (Cpl. B. Roberts, Vancouver) and Interpol (John Lyons, ). Time for the investigation was limited as the information was required for a trial in  in 1982. A verbal report was made to Crown Prosecutor Powell in  by Marilyn Laver; however, expert testimony was not required in court. As the case was settled on the basis of other evidence, further analytical work was not pursued; no formal report was required and none was written. In 1997, Bob Setters, the current private owner of the painting requested an evaluation of the data from the earlier study, resulting in this report, prepared in cooperation with CCI.

     Once the police had seized me there was great care taken to guarantee all evidence would be of forensic nature. My shipping crate was sealed with a multitude of official RCMP stickers, I even had my own Mountie as escort (nice kid), and once I arrived in  much care was taken to secure the results of testing and preserve the chain of custody. My stretcher was marked ‘ML’ by Marilyn Laver on entry into the building and all samples were kept in a locked vault or secure laboratory while under analysis.

     In the report Marilyn’s observations and conclusions were interspersed with guarded comments trying to explain the enigmatic. At the same time she candidly relied on the 1997 reply from Townsend. It was clear this text would take Bob much energy to digest. He began with Marilyn’s carefully worded conclusions:

The painting shows evidence of age which is consistent with a work from the period around 1800. It has undergone several conservation treatments, including retouching and attachment of three lining canvases. It bears a varnish layer with the milky fluorescence which often results from aging. The use of lead antimonite yellow [ yellow] also suggests a 1750 -1850 date range, the period when the pigment was most popular.

Many of the stylistic features observed suggest that this is an unfinished work; for example, the lack of detail in many areas of the composition, and the thinness of the paint with the ground layer and pentimenti visible.

The materials from this painting which were analyzed were available to J.M.W. Turner in his lifetime. The pigments identified suggest the more restricted palette characteristic of his early years (before about 1805), prior to his well-known experimentation with new materials. Relative to published analyses of Turner works, some of the pigment usage is uncommon: ultramarine in a green mixture, lead antimonate yellow [ yellow], and barium sulfate in the ground layer...  It can be concluded that physical evidence suggests that the painting originates from the time period of J. M. W. Turner and comprises material available to him… The question of attribution to any particular artist should be addressed by a curatorial expert on Turner’s work based on content and style.

A really important point here: Sir Richard Westmacott (one of a party about to start for ) asked Turner if he could do anything for him, “No,” said Turner, “Unless you will bring me some  yellow.” Hamerton, one of JMW’s first biographers also supports Marilyn’s statement about Turner’s experimental nature: “He used any new colour that the experimentalizing ingenuity of modern chemistry could invent for the temptation of an artist.”

 …The stretcher in “Shipwreck, the Rescue” consisted of four vertical members and three horizontal ones, keyed at the corners and at each join with the perimeter. By comparison, Townsend mentions that in the early years, many of Turner’s paintings had homemade stretchers with the diagonal cross-members generally found in the eighteenth century. However, this stretcher may have been built later…

 The label on the stretcher reading “Shipwreck – The Rescue   J.M.W. Turner   R.A.” may also have been affixed at this time. The paper of the label was not a wood pulp or Japanese paper; hemp or bast (linen) were possibilities (i.e. rag paper), neither of which are unique to Turner’s period.

     If I remember rightly my present stretcher is from Brown of High Holborn, a firm that JMW was patronizing circa 1830-1850. I’m pretty sure it was about that time… He was refurbishing some of us early works trying to find buyers—about the time Ruskin was coming around. In any case it has the proper configuration and construction, including beveled inner edges of the stretcher bars. It’s the kind JMW was buying after daddy passed-on in 1829. It was a relief to be getting a little TLC after fifty years of neglect. I hate recalling that damp moldy environment. Under normal circumstances there would have been no need by this point for such a dramatic restoration on such a young painting. Oh yeah, this is when they slapped the label on my stretcher. Finally I’d been given a proper name—Shipwreck, the Rescue!

In keeping with this timing, which followed the 1843 publication of the first volume of Ruskin’s Modern Painters (with its spirited championship of JMW’s art) “his pictures were again in keen demand, and he was encouraged to put some earlier canvases into repair to tempt the new purchasers who now came to the gallery.”

It’s funny that as yet no body has noticed my title label and how it adheres to the wood of my stretcher. Even the seasoned wood of quality furniture, and in this case my stretcher, naturally shrinks given enough time. The top on a small Georgian tea table can shrink as much as three-quarter inch across one plane, leaving the top slightly oval. This happens with stretcher wood also; the wood shrinks in one direction—across the grain. This is relevant here because while my stretcher wood has shrunk over one and a half centuries, my paper label has not. The result is that my label has wrinkled, and in places has separated from the wood, and chipped. This is significant because it guarantees that the label is vintage and that both it and my stretcher are from Turner’s era (see image No. 4).  I’m adding some of this detail for the doubting Thomases out there, the ones that don’t recognize me for what I am.
